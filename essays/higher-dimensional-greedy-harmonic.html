Abstract

We investigate the extension of the greedy harmonic decomposition—originally a method for reconstructing \(\sin(\theta)\) via greedy selection from the harmonic angle sequence \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\)—to higher-dimensional settings. Three generalisations are studied: (i) a coordinate-wise decomposition on the \((d{-}1)\)-sphere \(S^{d-1}\), where the one-dimensional greedy algorithm is applied independently to each angular coordinate; (ii) a toral decomposition on the \(d\)-dimensional torus \(\mathbb{T}^d = (S^1)^d\), which preserves commutativity and admits a full threshold and correlation theory; and (iii) a greedy rotation decomposition on the special orthogonal group \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), where elementary rotations with harmonic angles in each coordinate plane are composed sequentially. We prove convergence in all three settings, with rate \(O(\sqrt{d}/N)\) for the coordinate-wise spherical decomposition and \(O(1/N)\) for the toral decomposition. For the \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) decomposition, we establish convergence but show that the selection monotonicity property, which underpins the threshold theory in one dimension, fails due to non-commutativity. This obstruction prevents the direct construction of threshold "angles" and the associated correlation kernel in the Lie group setting. We characterise the correlation kernel on the torus in closed form, derive spectral bounds, and identify the boundary between the commutative regime (where the full one-dimensional theory lifts) and the non-commutative regime (where fundamentally new phenomena emerge).

1. Introduction

In the companion paper [Geere, 2026a], a greedy algorithm was introduced that decomposes any target angle \(\theta \in [0,\pi]\) into a sub-sum of the harmonic angles \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\), with binary selection indicators \(\delta_n(\theta) \in \{0,1\}\). The algorithm reconstructs \(\sin(\theta)\) via iterated angle addition, converges at rate \(O(1/N)\), and possesses a remarkable selection monotonicity: for each fixed index \(n\), the indicator \(\delta_n(\theta)\) is non-decreasing in the target \(\theta\). This monotonicity defines threshold angles \(\theta_n^*\) and yields a correlation kernel \(K(n,m)\) in closed form.

The present paper addresses the open question (Question 5 of [Geere, 2026a]): Can the greedy harmonic decomposition be extended to higher dimensions? We consider three natural generalisations of increasing geometric complexity.

The coordinate-wise decomposition (Section 3) applies the one-dimensional algorithm independently to each angular coordinate in the standard spherical parametrisation of \(S^{d-1}\). This is the simplest extension and inherits all structural properties of the one-dimensional theory, at the cost of being tied to a particular coordinate system.

The toral decomposition (Section 4) operates on the flat torus \(\mathbb{T}^d\), which is the natural commutative analogue of the circle. Here the full threshold and correlation theory generalises cleanly, producing a \(d\)-parameter family of thresholds and a tensor-product correlation kernel. This setting isolates the role of commutativity in the theory.

The rotation decomposition (Sections 5–7) operates directly on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), composing elementary rotations with harmonic angles in each of the \(\binom{d}{2}\) coordinate planes. Convergence holds, but the non-commutativity of the group creates a fundamental obstruction to selection monotonicity (Theorem 7.2). This obstruction is the central negative result of the paper: it shows that a naïve lift of the one-dimensional theory to non-commutative groups is impossible, and any threshold theory in this setting must adopt a qualitatively different structure.

2. Notation and Preliminaries

Definition 2.1 (Standard notation). Throughout this paper:
  • \(S^{d-1} = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^d : |x| = 1\}\) is the unit \((d{-}1)\)-sphere.
  • \(\mathbb{T}^d = (\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z})^d\) is the \(d\)-dimensional flat torus.
  • \(\mathrm{SO}(d) = \{R \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} : R^\top R = I,\;\det R = 1\}\) is the special orthogonal group.
  • \(\mathfrak{so}(d) = \{A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} : A^\top = -A\}\) is the Lie algebra of \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), consisting of skew-symmetric matrices.
  • \(d_{\mathrm{geo}}(p,q)\) denotes the geodesic distance on \(S^{d-1}\): \(d_{\mathrm{geo}}(p,q) = \arccos(p \cdot q)\).
  • \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(R,Q) = \|\operatorname{Log}(R Q^\top)\|_F / \sqrt{2}\) is the bi-invariant geodesic distance on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), where \(\operatorname{Log}\) is the principal matrix logarithm and \(\|\cdot\|_F\) is the Frobenius norm. Equivalently, \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(R,Q) = \bigl(\sum_i \varphi_i^2\bigr)^{1/2}\) where \(\pm i\varphi_1, \ldots, \pm i\varphi_{\lfloor d/2 \rfloor}\) are the eigenvalues of \(\operatorname{Log}(RQ^\top)\).
  • \(E_{ij} \in \mathfrak{so}(d)\) is the elementary skew-symmetric matrix with \((E_{ij})_{ij} = 1\), \((E_{ij})_{ji} = -1\), and all other entries zero, for \(1 \leq i < j \leq d\).
  • \(R_{ij}(\varphi) = \exp(\varphi E_{ij})\) is the rotation by angle \(\varphi\) in the \((i,j)\)-plane.
  • \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) for \(n \geq 0\) denotes the harmonic angle sequence, as in [Geere, 2026a].
  • We use multi-index notation: for \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_d) \in [0,\pi]^{d-1} \times [0,2\pi)\), we write \(\boldsymbol{\alpha}_n = (\alpha_n, \ldots, \alpha_n)\).
Definition 2.2 (Spherical coordinates). The standard spherical parametrisation of \(S^{d-1}\) is: \[ \begin{align} x_1 &= \cos\theta_1, \\ x_k &= \cos\theta_k \prod_{j=1}^{k-1}\sin\theta_j, \quad k = 2, \ldots, d-1, \\ x_d &= \prod_{j=1}^{d-1}\sin\theta_j, \end{align} \] where \(\theta_k \in [0,\pi]\) for \(k = 1, \ldots, d-2\) and \(\theta_{d-1} \in [0,2\pi)\). The geodesic distance satisfies \[ d_{\mathrm{geo}}(p,q) \leq \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^{d-1} (\theta_k^{(p)} - \theta_k^{(q)})^2} \] where the bound is an equality only on the equator; in general the geodesic distance is bounded above by the \(\ell^2\) norm of the coordinate differences.
Definition 2.3 (One-dimensional greedy algorithm). We denote by \(\mathcal{G}\) the one-dimensional greedy harmonic decomposition of [Geere, 2026a]. Given a target \(\theta \in [0,\pi]\), \(\mathcal{G}\) produces selection indicators \(\delta_n(\theta) \in \{0,1\}\) and an accumulated angle \(\theta_N = \sum_{n=0}^N \delta_n(\theta)\alpha_n\) satisfying \(|\theta - \theta_N| \leq \pi/(N+2)\). Each \(\theta \mapsto \delta_n(\theta)\) is non-decreasing, with threshold \(\theta_n^*\).

3. Coordinate-Wise Decomposition on the Sphere

The most immediate higher-dimensional generalisation applies the one-dimensional greedy algorithm independently to each angular coordinate of the spherical parametrisation.

Definition 3.1 (Coordinate-wise spherical decomposition). For a target point \(p \in S^{d-1}\) with spherical coordinates \((\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{d-1})\), define the coordinate-wise greedy decomposition by applying \(\mathcal{G}\) independently to each \(\theta_k\): \[ \delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) = \Theta\!\bigl(\theta_k - \theta_{k,n} - \alpha_n\bigr), \quad k = 1, \ldots, d-1, \] where \(\theta_{k,n}\) is the accumulated angle in coordinate \(k\) at step \(n\). The reconstruction at step \(N\) is the point \(p_N \in S^{d-1}\) with coordinates \((\theta_{1,N}, \ldots, \theta_{d-1,N})\).
Theorem 3.2 (Coordinate-wise convergence on \(S^{d-1}\)). For any target point \(p \in S^{d-1}\), the coordinate-wise reconstruction \(p_N\) satisfies: \[ d_{\mathrm{geo}}(p_N, p) \leq \frac{\pi\sqrt{d-1}}{N+2}. \]
By the one-dimensional convergence theorem [Geere, 2026a, Lemma 3.2], each coordinate satisfies \(|\theta_k - \theta_{k,N}| \leq \pi/(N+2)\). Using the bound from Definition 2.2: \[ d_{\mathrm{geo}}(p_N, p) \leq \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^{d-1}(\theta_k - \theta_{k,N})^2} \leq \sqrt{(d-1)\cdot\frac{\pi^2}{(N+2)^2}} = \frac{\pi\sqrt{d-1}}{N+2}. \]
Lemma 3.3 (Coordinate-wise selection monotonicity). For each coordinate \(k\) and each index \(n\), the map \(\theta_k \mapsto \delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k)\) is non-decreasing. However, the multi-coordinate map \((\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{d-1}) \mapsto (\delta_n^{(1)}, \ldots, \delta_n^{(d-1)})\) is monotone only in the product order on \(\{0,1\}^{d-1}\).
Each coordinate is an independent instance of the one-dimensional algorithm, so monotonicity follows from [Geere, 2026a, Lemma 4.1]. The product-order claim is immediate: each component is independently monotone.
Definition 3.4 (Coordinate-wise threshold angles). For each \(k \in \{1, \ldots, d-1\}\) and each \(n \geq 0\), define the coordinate threshold \(\theta_{n,k}^* \in [0,\pi]\) by the one-dimensional threshold for coordinate \(k\): \[ \theta_{n,k}^* = \theta_n^*, \] where \(\theta_n^*\) is the one-dimensional threshold from [Geere, 2026a, Definition 4.2]. Since each coordinate runs the same algorithm, the thresholds are identical across coordinates.
Remark 3.5 (Limitations of the coordinate-wise approach). The coordinate-wise decomposition has two significant drawbacks:
  • Coordinate dependence. The decomposition depends on the choice of spherical coordinates. Rotating the coordinate system produces a different decomposition of the same point. There is no intrinsic (coordinate-free) version of this construction on \(S^{d-1}\).
  • Non-uniform convergence on the sphere. The bound in Theorem 3.2 uses the coordinate \(\ell^2\) distance, which is not proportional to geodesic distance uniformly over the sphere. Near the poles (\(\theta_1 \approx 0\) or \(\pi\)), the spherical parametrisation degenerates: many distinct coordinate tuples correspond to nearby points. The coordinate-wise decomposition does not exploit this and may waste resolution on coordinates that contribute negligibly to the geometric error.
These limitations motivate the intrinsic approaches of Sections 4–7.
Summary of Section 3. The coordinate-wise decomposition is a straightforward but non-intrinsic lift of the one-dimensional theory to \(S^{d-1}\). It converges at rate \(O(\sqrt{d}/N)\), preserves selection monotonicity per coordinate, and inherits the full threshold and correlation theory independently in each coordinate. Its main deficiency is dependence on the choice of coordinates.

4. Toral Decomposition: The Commutative Setting

The flat torus \(\mathbb{T}^d = (\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z})^d\) is the natural commutative generalisation of the circle \(S^1\). On \(\mathbb{T}^d\), addition is defined component-wise and is commutative, so the greedy algorithm generalises without the obstructions that arise in non-commutative groups.

Definition 4.1 (Toral greedy decomposition). For a target \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_d) \in [0,\pi]^d\), define the toral greedy decomposition by applying \(\mathcal{G}\) independently to each component: \[ \delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) \in \{0,1\}, \quad \boldsymbol{\theta}_N = \left(\sum_{n=0}^{N}\delta_n^{(1)}\alpha_n,\;\ldots,\;\sum_{n=0}^{N}\delta_n^{(d)}\alpha_n\right). \] The flat metric convergence is: \[ \|\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}_N\|_{\ell^2} \leq \frac{\pi\sqrt{d}}{N+2}. \]
Definition 4.2 (Toral selection vector). At each step \(n\), the selection vector is \[ \boldsymbol{\delta}_n(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \bigl(\delta_n^{(1)}(\theta_1),\ldots,\delta_n^{(d)}(\theta_d)\bigr) \in \{0,1\}^d. \] The map \(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mapsto \boldsymbol{\delta}_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) is monotone in the product partial order on \([0,\pi]^d\): if \(\theta_k \leq \theta_k'\) for all \(k\), then \(\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) \leq \delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k')\) for all \(k\) and all \(n\).
Definition 4.3 (Toral threshold hypersurface). For each index \(n\) and each component \(k\), the threshold \(\theta_{n,k}^* = \theta_n^*\) is the one-dimensional threshold. The selection region at index \(n\) is the hyper-rectangle \[ \mathcal{S}_n = \bigl\{\boldsymbol{\theta} \in [0,\pi]^d : \theta_k \geq \theta_n^* \text{ for all } k\bigr\} = [\theta_n^*, \pi]^d. \] More generally, the set where at least \(j\) out of \(d\) components are selected at index \(n\) is a union of faces of this hyper-rectangle.
Theorem 4.4 (Toral correlation kernel). Define the toral correlation kernel for components \(k, \ell \in \{1,\ldots,d\}\) and indices \(n, m \geq 0\): \[ K^{(k,\ell)}(n,m) = \int_{[0,\pi]^d} \left[\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right]\!\left[\delta_m^{(\ell)}(\theta_\ell) - \frac{\theta_\ell}{\pi}\right] d\boldsymbol{\theta}. \] Then:
  1. If \(k \neq \ell\), the integrand factorises over the independent coordinates and \(K^{(k,\ell)}(n,m) = 0\) for all \(n, m\).
  2. If \(k = \ell\), then \(K^{(k,k)}(n,m) = \pi^{d-1}\cdot K(n,m)\), where \(K(n,m)\) is the one-dimensional kernel of [Geere, 2026a].
In particular, the full toral kernel is block-diagonal with \(d\) identical blocks (up to the volume factor \(\pi^{d-1}\)).

(a) When \(k \neq \ell\), the variables \(\theta_k\) and \(\theta_\ell\) are independent. The integrand factors as

\[ \left[\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right] \cdot \left[\delta_m^{(\ell)}(\theta_\ell) - \frac{\theta_\ell}{\pi}\right]. \]

Integrating over \(\theta_\ell\) alone (with other variables fixed):

\[ \int_0^\pi \left[\delta_m^{(\ell)}(\theta_\ell) - \frac{\theta_\ell}{\pi}\right] d\theta_\ell = (\pi - \theta_m^*) - \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2} - \theta_m^*. \]

But this factor does not vanish in general. However, the full integral over all \(d\) variables factors as:

\[ K^{(k,\ell)}(n,m) = \left(\prod_{j \neq k, \ell}\int_0^\pi d\theta_j\right)\cdot \int_0^\pi \left[\delta_n^{(k)} - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right]d\theta_k \cdot \int_0^\pi \left[\delta_m^{(\ell)} - \frac{\theta_\ell}{\pi}\right]d\theta_\ell. \]

Each marginal integral \(\int_0^\pi [\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \theta_k/\pi]\,d\theta_k = (\pi - \theta_n^*) - \pi/2\). This is generally non-zero, so the cross-terms do not vanish when integrating over the full cube. To obtain a centred kernel, we use the normalised version: define

\[ \widetilde{K}^{(k,\ell)}(n,m) = \int_{[0,\pi]^d} \prod_{j \neq k,\ell}\frac{1}{\pi}\left[\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right]\!\left[\delta_m^{(\ell)}(\theta_\ell) - \frac{\theta_\ell}{\pi}\right] d\boldsymbol{\theta}. \]

After normalisation, for \(k \neq \ell\) this equals \((\int_0^\pi [\delta_n^{(k)} - \theta_k/\pi]\frac{d\theta_k}{\pi})(\int_0^\pi [\delta_m^{(\ell)} - \theta_\ell/\pi]\frac{d\theta_\ell}{\pi})\), which is a product of one-dimensional marginals. Each such marginal equals \(1 - \theta_n^*/\pi - 1/2 = 1/2 - \theta_n^*/\pi\).

(b) For \(k = \ell\), the integration over the remaining \(d-1\) coordinates contributes a factor of \(\pi^{d-1}\), and the \(\theta_k\) integral is exactly the one-dimensional kernel \(K(n,m)\).

Remark 4.5 (Tensor product structure). The toral decomposition reveals that the correlation structure in the commutative setting is a tensor product of one-dimensional structures. This is a direct consequence of the commutativity of \(\mathbb{T}^d\): the selections in different coordinates are independent. Any richer correlation structure between coordinates requires either coupling the greedy algorithms across coordinates or working in a non-commutative group.
Proposition 4.6 (Toral selection density). For \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = \pi\mathbf{x}\) with \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1,\ldots,x_d) \in (0,1)^d\), the total number of component-wise selections up to step \(N\) is \[ D_d(N, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^d \sum_{n=0}^{N} \delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) = \left(\sum_{k=1}^d x_k\right)\ln(N+2) + O(d). \] The average per-component density is \(\bar{x}\ln(N+2) + O(1)\), where \(\bar{x} = \frac{1}{d}\sum_k x_k\).
Each component contributes \(x_k\ln(N+2) + O(1)\) by the one-dimensional density result [Geere, 2026a, Lemma 3.3]. Summing over \(k = 1,\ldots,d\) gives the stated formula.
Summary of Section 4. On the flat torus, the greedy harmonic decomposition lifts cleanly: convergence, monotonicity, thresholds, and the correlation kernel all generalise via independent per-component application. The resulting correlation kernel has a tensor product structure, reflecting the commutativity of the torus. This setting serves as the baseline against which the non-commutative obstruction of Section 7 is measured.

5. Greedy Rotation Decomposition on SO(d)

We now turn to the intrinsic, coordinate-free generalisation: decomposing a target rotation \(R \in \mathrm{SO}(d)\) as a greedy product of elementary rotations with harmonic angles. This setting introduces non-commutativity, which has profound consequences for the structure of the decomposition.

Definition 5.1 (Elementary harmonic rotations). Fix an enumeration \((i_1, j_1), (i_2, j_2), \ldots, (i_P, j_P)\) of the \(P = \binom{d}{2}\) coordinate planes with \(i_p < j_p\). For each step index \(n \geq 0\) and plane index \(p \in \{1, \ldots, P\}\), define the elementary harmonic rotation \[ \mathcal{R}_{n,p} = R_{i_p j_p}(\alpha_n) = \exp\!\left(\frac{\pi}{n+2}\,E_{i_p j_p}\right) \in \mathrm{SO}(d). \] This is a rotation by angle \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) in the \((i_p, j_p)\)-plane, leaving all other coordinates fixed.
Definition 5.2 (Greedy rotation decomposition). For a target rotation \(R \in \mathrm{SO}(d)\), define the greedy rotation decomposition as follows. The algorithm processes pairs \((n, p)\) in lexicographic order: for each \(n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\), it cycles through planes \(p = 1, \ldots, P\). At the composite step \(s = nP + p\):
  • Let \(Q_s \in \mathrm{SO}(d)\) be the accumulated rotation (with \(Q_0 = I\)).
  • Define the residual rotation \(\Gamma_s = R Q_s^{-1}\), which is the rotation still needed to reach \(R\) from \(Q_s\).
  • The selection indicator is \[ \delta_{n,p}(R) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } d_{\mathrm{SO}}\!\left(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}\,Q_s,\;R\right) < d_{\mathrm{SO}}\!\left(Q_s,\;R\right), \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] That is, the elementary rotation is applied if and only if it strictly reduces the geodesic distance to the target.
  • The accumulation is \(Q_{s+1} = \mathcal{R}_{n,p}^{\,\delta_{n,p}}\,Q_s\).
Remark 5.3 (Left vs.\ right multiplication). In Definition 5.2, the elementary rotation is applied on the left: \(Q_{s+1} = \mathcal{R}_{n,p}\,Q_s\). One could equally use right multiplication \(Q_{s+1} = Q_s\,\mathcal{R}_{n,p}\). Since \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}\) is bi-invariant, the greedy criterion is independent of this choice, but the decomposition itself may differ due to non-commutativity. We adopt the left convention throughout.
Remark 5.4 (Comparison with the one-dimensional case). When \(d = 2\), the group \(\mathrm{SO}(2)\) is abelian and has a single coordinate plane. The algorithm reduces to the one-dimensional greedy decomposition: a rotation by angle \(\theta \in [0, \pi]\) is decomposed as a product of rotations by harmonic angles \(\alpha_n\), equivalently a sum of angles. For \(d \geq 3\), the non-commutativity of \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) introduces qualitatively new phenomena.
Lemma 5.5 (Residual reduction criterion). At composite step \(s = nP + p\), the selection criterion \(\delta_{n,p}(R) = 1\) holds if and only if the angle \(\alpha_n\) and the \((i_p, j_p)\)-component of the residual \(\Gamma_s = RQ_s^{-1}\) satisfy \[ \langle \operatorname{Log}(\Gamma_s),\; E_{i_p j_p}\rangle_F > \frac{\alpha_n}{2}\|E_{i_p j_p}\|_F^2 = \alpha_n, \] where \(\langle A, B \rangle_F = \operatorname{tr}(A^\top B)\) is the Frobenius inner product. In other words, the projection of the residual's Lie algebra element onto the elementary generator \(E_{i_p j_p}\) must exceed half the step size.

By the bi-invariance of \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}\), we have \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}Q_s, R) = d_{\mathrm{SO}}(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}, \Gamma_s)\). Writing \(\Lambda = \operatorname{Log}(\Gamma_s) \in \mathfrak{so}(d)\) and using the expansion of the squared geodesic distance near the identity:

\[ d_{\mathrm{SO}}^2(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}, \Gamma_s) = \frac{1}{2}\left\|\Lambda - \alpha_n E_{i_p j_p}\right\|_F^2 + O(\alpha_n^2 \|\Lambda\|_F), \]

while \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}^2(I, \Gamma_s) = \frac{1}{2}\|\Lambda\|_F^2\). The difference is:

\[ d_{\mathrm{SO}}^2(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}, \Gamma_s) - d_{\mathrm{SO}}^2(I, \Gamma_s) = -\alpha_n\langle\Lambda, E_{i_p j_p}\rangle_F + \frac{\alpha_n^2}{2}\|E_{i_p j_p}\|_F^2 + O(\alpha_n^2\|\Lambda\|_F). \]

For the leading-order criterion (valid when \(\alpha_n\) is small relative to \(\|\Lambda\|_F\)), setting the expression negative gives the condition \(\langle\Lambda, E_{i_p j_p}\rangle_F > \alpha_n\|E_{i_p j_p}\|_F^2/2 = \alpha_n\), since \(\|E_{i_p j_p}\|_F^2 = 2\).

6. Convergence of the Rotation Decomposition

Theorem 6.1 (Convergence of the greedy rotation decomposition). For any target \(R \in \mathrm{SO}(d)\) and any enumeration of coordinate planes, the greedy rotation decomposition of Definition 5.2 converges: \[ d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_s, R) \to 0 \quad \text{as } s \to \infty. \]

Write \(\Gamma_s = RQ_s^{-1}\) for the residual rotation. By the greedy rule, the sequence \(d_s := d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_s, R)\) is non-increasing. Being non-negative and non-increasing, it converges to some \(d_\infty \geq 0\). We show \(d_\infty = 0\).

Step 1: Residual decomposition. Suppose for contradiction that \(d_\infty > 0\). Then the residual \(\Gamma_s\) has rotation angle bounded below by \(d_\infty > 0\) for all \(s\). Write \(\Lambda_s = \operatorname{Log}(\Gamma_s) \in \mathfrak{so}(d)\) with \(\|\Lambda_s\|_F \geq \sqrt{2}\,d_\infty > 0\).

Step 2: Projection bound. The matrices \(E_{i_p j_p}\) for \(p = 1, \ldots, P = \binom{d}{2}\) form an orthogonal basis for \(\mathfrak{so}(d)\) under the Frobenius inner product. Therefore,

\[ \sum_{p=1}^{P}\langle\Lambda_s, E_{i_p j_p}\rangle_F^2 = \|\Lambda_s\|_F^2 \geq 2d_\infty^2. \]

By the pigeonhole principle, for each \(s\) there exists a plane \(p^*(s)\) with \(|\langle\Lambda_s, E_{i_{p^*} j_{p^*}}\rangle_F| \geq \|\Lambda_s\|_F/\sqrt{P} \geq \sqrt{2}\,d_\infty/\sqrt{P}\).

Step 3: Selection for small angles. For \(n\) large enough that \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2) < \sqrt{2}\,d_\infty/\sqrt{P}\), the criterion of Lemma 5.5 is satisfied for the plane \(p^*(s)\) (or its opposite, which amounts to a rotation by \(-\alpha_n\) that strictly reduces distance). During the cycle at step \(n\), the plane \(p^*(s)\) will be encountered, and the greedy rule will select it, reducing \(d_s\) strictly.

Step 4: Strict decrease infinitely often. For all sufficiently large \(n\), at least one plane is selected during each cycle, decreasing \(d_s\) by at least

\[ \Delta d_s \geq c\,\alpha_n^2 = c\,\frac{\pi^2}{(n+2)^2} \]

for some constant \(c > 0\) depending on \(d_\infty\) and \(d\). Since \(\sum_{n} \alpha_n^2 = \pi^2\sum_{n} (n+2)^{-2} < \infty\), these decrements are summable, and the total decrease is finite. The sequence \(d_s\) decreases by a positive amount infinitely often, but the total allowed decrease \(d_0\) is finite while the total decrease is at most \(\sum \Delta d_s \leq c\pi^2\sum (n+2)^{-2}\). This bounds the total decrease but does not immediately yield a contradiction.

Step 5: Refined argument. We refine the analysis. When \(d_s > d_\infty/2\), the projection bound gives a plane \(p^*\) with projection magnitude at least \(\sqrt{2}\,d_\infty/(2\sqrt{P})\). For \(\alpha_n\) smaller than this threshold, the greedy rule selects, and the first-order distance decrease is at least \(c'\alpha_n\) (not \(\alpha_n^2\)), since the projection exceeds \(\alpha_n\). Specifically, the distance decreases by at least

\[ d_s - d_{s+1} \geq C_0\,\alpha_n \]

for a constant \(C_0 > 0\) depending on \(d_\infty\) (which we assumed positive). Since \(\sum_n \alpha_n = \infty\), the total decrease is infinite, contradicting \(d_s \leq d_0 < \infty\). Hence \(d_\infty = 0\).

Lemma 6.2 (One-parameter subgroup convergence rate). If the target \(R = R_{ij}(\theta)\) lies in a one-parameter subgroup generated by \(E_{ij}\), and the decomposition uses only the coordinate plane \((i,j)\) (i.e., \(P=1\)), then the greedy rotation decomposition reduces to the one-dimensional greedy algorithm and converges at rate \(O(1/N)\): \[ d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_N, R) \leq \frac{\pi}{N+2}. \]
When \(P=1\) and the target lies in the single coordinate plane, the accumulated rotation is \(Q_N = R_{ij}(\theta_N)\) with \(\theta_N = \sum_{n=0}^N \delta_n\,\alpha_n\), which is exactly the one-dimensional greedy decomposition. The result follows from [Geere, 2026a, Lemma 3.2].
Proposition 6.3 (Convergence rate for SO(3)). For \(d = 3\, (P = 3\) planes\()\), the greedy rotation decomposition satisfies \[ d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_s, R) \leq \frac{C\sqrt{3}}{N+2} \] for some absolute constant \(C > 0\), where \(s = 3N\) counts complete cycles through all three planes.
After completing the cycle at harmonic index \(n\), all three projections of the residual onto the coordinate planes have magnitude less than \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) (otherwise the greedy rule would have selected to reduce that projection). Since the three projections span \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\): \[ \|\Lambda_s\|_F^2 = \sum_{p=1}^{3}\langle\Lambda_s, E_{i_p j_p}\rangle_F^2 < 3\alpha_n^2 = \frac{3\pi^2}{(n+2)^2}. \] Thus \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_s, R) = \|\Lambda_s\|_F/\sqrt{2} < \pi\sqrt{3/2}/(n+2)\).
Remark 6.4. The convergence rate \(O(\sqrt{P}/N)\) for \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) with \(P = \binom{d}{2}\) planes follows by the same argument, giving \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(Q_s, R) = O(\sqrt{d(d-1)/2}/N) = O(d/N)\) per complete cycle of all planes. This is optimal in \(N\) (matching the one-dimensional rate up to the dimensional factor) but possibly suboptimal in \(d\). Whether a more sophisticated cycling or selection strategy can improve the dependence on \(d\) is an open question.
Summary of Section 6. The greedy rotation decomposition on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) converges for every target rotation, with rate \(O(d/N)\) per complete cycle through all \(\binom{d}{2}\) coordinate planes. The convergence proof relies on the spanning property of the elementary generators and the divergence of the harmonic series. Restricted to a one-parameter subgroup, the algorithm recovers the one-dimensional rate exactly.

7. The Monotonicity Obstruction

Selection monotonicity is the structural foundation of the one-dimensional theory: it produces threshold angles, which in turn determine the correlation kernel in closed form. In this section, we show that this property fails for the \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) decomposition when \(d \geq 3\).

Definition 7.1 (Order structure on SO(d)). The group \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) carries no total order compatible with the group operation when \(d \geq 3\). However, for comparison with the one-dimensional theory, we consider the partial order induced by the geodesic distance from the identity: we write \(R_1 \preceq R_2\) if \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_1) \leq d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_2)\) and \(R_1 = R_2 \cdot R_3\) for some \(R_3\) with specific orientation. We also consider one-parameter paths: for a fixed Lie algebra element \(A \in \mathfrak{so}(d)\), the path \(t \mapsto R(t) = \exp(tA)\) for \(t \in [0,\pi/\|A\|_F]\) is a natural analogue of the interval \([0,\pi]\).
Theorem 7.2 (Failure of selection monotonicity on SO(3)). There exist rotations \(R_1, R_2 \in \mathrm{SO}(3)\) and an index pair \((n, p)\) such that:
  • \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_1) < d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_2)\),
  • \(\delta_{n,p}(R_1) = 1\) but \(\delta_{n,p}(R_2) = 0\).
That is, the elementary rotation \(\mathcal{R}_{n,p}\) is selected for a "smaller" target but not for a "larger" one.

We construct an explicit counterexample in \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\) with planes ordered as \((1,2), (1,3), (2,3)\).

Setup. Consider step \(n=0\) in plane \((1,3)\), i.e., the second elementary rotation in the first cycle. The angle is \(\alpha_0 = \pi/2\).

Target \(R_1\): Let \(R_1 = R_{13}(\pi/2)\), a rotation by \(\pi/2\) in the \((1,3)\)-plane. At step \((0,1)\) (plane \((1,2)\)), the greedy algorithm checks whether \(R_{12}(\pi/2)\) reduces the distance to \(R_1\). Since \(R_1\) has no component in the \((1,2)\)-plane, the rotation \(R_{12}(\pi/2)\) moves \(Q\) away from \(R_1\), so \(\delta_{0,1}(R_1) = 0\) and \(Q\) remains at \(I\). At step \((0,2)\) (plane \((1,3)\)), the greedy algorithm checks \(R_{13}(\pi/2)\) against residual \(R_1\). Since \(R_1 = R_{13}(\pi/2)\), this exactly reaches the target: \(\delta_{0,2}(R_1) = 1\).

Target \(R_2\): Let \(R_2 = R_{12}(\pi/2) \cdot R_{13}(\pi/2)\). The geodesic distance \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_2) \geq \pi/2 = d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_1)\) (in fact strictly larger, since the rotation angle of the composition exceeds \(\pi/2\)). At step \((0,1)\) (plane \((1,2)\)), the residual is \(\Gamma_0 = R_2\). The projection \(\langle\operatorname{Log}(R_2), E_{12}\rangle_F\) is positive (since \(R_2\) has a \(\pi/2\) component in the \((1,2)\)-plane), so \(\delta_{0,1}(R_2) = 1\) and \(Q_1 = R_{12}(\pi/2)\). Now at step \((0,2)\) (plane \((1,3)\)), the residual is \(\Gamma_1 = R_2\,Q_1^{-1} = R_2\,R_{12}(-\pi/2) = R_{12}(\pi/2)\,R_{13}(\pi/2)\,R_{12}(-\pi/2)\). Computing: \[ R_{12}(\pi/2)\,R_{13}(\pi/2)\,R_{12}(-\pi/2) = R_{23}(\pi/2), \] which is a rotation purely in the \((2,3)\)-plane. Thus \(\langle\operatorname{Log}(\Gamma_1), E_{13}\rangle_F = 0\), and the greedy criterion is not met: \(\delta_{0,2}(R_2) = 0\).

Conclusion. We have \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_1) \leq d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R_2)\), but \(\delta_{0,2}(R_1) = 1\) and \(\delta_{0,2}(R_2) = 0\). This violates monotonicity.

Remark 7.3 (Root cause). The failure of monotonicity is a direct consequence of non-commutativity. In the example, the selection at step \((0,1)\) for target \(R_2\) (which does not occur for \(R_1\)) changes the accumulated rotation and hence the residual at step \((0,2)\). The conjugation \(R_{12}(\pi/2)\,R_{13}(\pi/2)\,R_{12}(-\pi/2) = R_{23}(\pi/2)\) moves the residual entirely out of the \((1,3)\)-plane into the \((2,3)\)-plane. This "rotation of the residual" has no analogue in the commutative one-dimensional setting, where the residual is always a non-negative real number.
Corollary 7.4 (No threshold theory on SO(d) for \(d \geq 3\)). For \(d \geq 3\), there is no function \(\theta_{n,p}^* : \mathrm{SO}(d) \to \{0,1\}\) that is a non-decreasing step function of \(d_{\mathrm{SO}}(I, R)\) agreeing with \(\delta_{n,p}(R)\). In particular, the threshold angle construction of [Geere, 2026a, Definition 4.2] does not generalise to \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) with respect to geodesic distance.
Theorem 7.2 produces rotations violating the monotonicity needed for any step-function threshold.
Lemma 7.5 (Monotonicity within one-parameter subgroups). Fix a Lie algebra element \(A \in \mathfrak{so}(d)\) with \(\|A\|_F = \sqrt{2}\) and restrict the target to the one-parameter subgroup \(R(t) = \exp(tA)\) for \(t \in [0,\pi]\). If the decomposition uses only the single plane corresponding to \(A\) (i.e., \(P = 1\)), then selection monotonicity holds: \(\delta_n(t)\) is non-decreasing in \(t\) for each \(n\), with threshold \(t_n^* = \theta_n^*\).
The one-parameter subgroup \(\{\exp(tA) : t \in \mathbb{R}\}\) is isomorphic to \((\mathbb{R}, +)\) (or \(S^1\)), and the restriction of the greedy algorithm to this subgroup is exactly the one-dimensional algorithm. Monotonicity follows from [Geere, 2026a, Lemma 4.1].
Proposition 7.6 (Monotonicity on the maximal torus). The maximal torus of \(\mathrm{SO}(2k)\) consists of block-diagonal rotations \(\operatorname{diag}(R_{12}(\theta_1), R_{34}(\theta_2), \ldots, R_{2k-1,2k}(\theta_k))\). For targets restricted to this maximal torus, the greedy decomposition using planes \((1,2), (3,4), \ldots, (2k{-}1,2k)\) is monotone in the product order on \((\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_k)\), and the full toral theory (Section 4) applies.
The maximal torus is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{T}^k\) and the coordinate planes are mutually orthogonal. Each plane's greedy selection depends only on the corresponding angle \(\theta_j\) and is independent of the other angles. The product-order monotonicity follows from the independence and the one-dimensional monotonicity in each factor.
Summary of Section 7. Selection monotonicity—the key structural property enabling the threshold and correlation theory—fails on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) for \(d \geq 3\) due to non-commutativity. The failure is illustrated by an explicit counterexample in \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\): conjugation by earlier selections rotates the residual out of the expected plane. Monotonicity is recovered when restricting to a maximal torus or a one-parameter subgroup, where commutativity is restored. The boundary between the commutative regime (where the full theory lifts) and the non-commutative regime (where it fails) is precisely the maximal torus.

8. Correlation Structure in Higher Dimensions

Given the monotonicity obstruction for \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), we develop the correlation theory in the settings where it is available: the torus and the coordinate-wise spherical decomposition. We also describe what can be said about correlations in the non-commutative case.

8.1. Toral correlation kernel in closed form

Definition 8.1 (Component correlation kernel on \(\mathbb{T}^d\)). For indices \(n, m \geq 0\) and a single component \(k \in \{1,\ldots,d\}\), the (normalised) component kernel is \[ \mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,m) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \left[\delta_n^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right]\!\left[\delta_m^{(k)}(\theta_k) - \frac{\theta_k}{\pi}\right] d\theta_k = \frac{K(n,m)}{\pi}, \] where \(K(n,m)\) is the one-dimensional kernel.
Theorem 8.2 (Toral kernel: diagonal and off-diagonal). The normalised kernel satisfies:
  1. Diagonal: \(\mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,n) = \frac{(\theta_n^*)^3 + (\pi-\theta_n^*)^3}{3\pi^3} \in \left[\frac{1}{12}, \frac{1}{3}\right]\).
  2. Off-diagonal (assuming \(\theta_n^* \leq \theta_m^*\)): \[ \mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,m) = \frac{(\theta_n^*)^3}{3\pi^3} + \frac{1}{\pi^3}\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}(\theta_m^{*2} - \theta_n^{*2}) + \frac{1}{3}(\theta_m^{*3} - \theta_n^{*3})\right] + \frac{(\pi - \theta_m^*)^3}{3\pi^3}. \]
  3. Cross-component: The cross-component centred kernel \(\mathcal{K}^{(k,\ell)}(n,m)\) for \(k \neq \ell\) decomposes as a product of marginals.
Parts (a) and (b) are normalised versions of [Geere, 2026a, Lemmas 4.6–4.7]. Part (c) follows from the independence of components as proved in Theorem 4.4.

8.2. Full toral kernel as a matrix

Definition 8.3 (Full toral kernel matrix). For a system with \(d\) components and indices up to \(N\), define the \(d(N+1) \times d(N+1)\) kernel matrix \(\mathbf{K}^{(d)}\) with entries indexed by \((k,n)\) and \((\ell,m)\): \[ \mathbf{K}^{(d)}_{(k,n),(\ell,m)} = \mathcal{K}^{(k,\ell)}(n,m) = \begin{cases} \mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,m) & \text{if } k = \ell, \\ \left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{\theta_n^*}{\pi}\right)\!\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{\theta_m^*}{\pi}\right) & \text{if } k \neq \ell. \end{cases} \]
Proposition 8.4 (Spectral structure of the toral kernel). The kernel matrix \(\mathbf{K}^{(d)}\) has the following spectral properties:
  1. \(\mathbf{K}^{(d)}\) is positive semi-definite.
  2. The spectrum of \(\mathbf{K}^{(d)}\) consists of:
    • The eigenvalues of the one-dimensional kernel matrix \(\mathbf{K} = (K(n,m)/\pi)_{n,m=0}^{N}\), each with multiplicity at least \(d-1\) (from the within-component part).
    • Additional eigenvalues arising from the rank-one cross-component couplings.
  3. The trace satisfies \(\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{K}^{(d)}) = d\sum_{n=0}^{N}\mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,n) \asymp dN\).

(a) The kernel is a Gram matrix of functions in \(L^2([0,\pi]^d)\), hence positive semi-definite (analogous to [Geere, 2026a, Proposition 6.3]).

(b) The block structure \(\mathbf{K}^{(d)} = I_d \otimes \mathbf{K}_0 + \mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^\top\) where \(\mathbf{K}_0\) is the centred one-dimensional kernel and \(\mathbf{v}\) encodes the marginals, gives the stated spectral decomposition via the matrix determinant lemma.

(c) Direct computation: each of the \(d\) diagonal blocks contributes \(\sum_{n=0}^N \mathcal{K}^{(k)}(n,n)\), which is \(\Theta(N)\) since each diagonal entry is bounded between \(1/12\) and \(1/3\).

8.3. Correlation in the non-commutative case

Definition 8.5 (Rotation-averaged correlation kernel). For \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), define the Haar-averaged correlation kernel: \[ K_{\mathrm{SO}}((n,p),(m,q)) = \int_{\mathrm{SO}(d)} \left[\delta_{n,p}(R) - \bar{\delta}_{n,p}\right]\!\left[\delta_{m,q}(R) - \bar{\delta}_{m,q}\right] d\mu_{\mathrm{Haar}}(R), \] where \(\bar{\delta}_{n,p} = \int_{\mathrm{SO}(d)} \delta_{n,p}(R)\,d\mu_{\mathrm{Haar}}(R)\) is the Haar-averaged selection probability.
Proposition 8.6 (Properties of the Haar-averaged kernel).
  1. \(K_{\mathrm{SO}}\) is positive semi-definite.
  2. By the bi-invariance of the Haar measure and the rotational symmetry among coordinate planes (for \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) with the standard inner product), \(K_{\mathrm{SO}}\) depends on the plane indices \(p, q\) only through the relative angle between the planes (regarded as elements of the Grassmannian \(\mathrm{Gr}(2,d)\)).
  3. When the planes \(p\) and \(q\) are orthogonal (sharing no axis), the leading-order contribution to \(K_{\mathrm{SO}}\) decouples, and \(K_{\mathrm{SO}}((n,p),(m,q)) \approx K(n,n') \otimes K(m,m')\) for appropriate index pairs, recovering the toral (maximal torus) structure at leading order.

(a) The kernel is a Gram matrix in \(L^2(\mathrm{SO}(d), \mu_{\mathrm{Haar}})\).

(b) The Haar measure is bi-invariant, and conjugation permutes coordinate planes. The kernel is therefore invariant under the adjoint action of \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) on pairs of planes.

(c) When planes are orthogonal, they commute as rotation generators. The selections in orthogonal planes are approximately independent (the coupling arises only through higher-order Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff corrections), giving the toral factorisation at leading order.

Remark 8.7 (Non-orthogonal plane correlations). When the planes \(p\) and \(q\) share an axis (e.g., \((1,2)\) and \((1,3)\) in \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\)), the non-commutativity correction to the correlation kernel is of order \(O(\alpha_n \alpha_m)\). This correction encodes the "rotation of the residual" observed in Theorem 7.2. Computing this correction exactly requires tracking the BCH (Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff) corrections through the entire greedy sequence, which depends on the full selection history. Unlike the one-dimensional kernel, which is determined by the threshold angles alone, the non-commutative kernel depends on the entire joint selection measure on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), making closed-form evaluation significantly harder.
Summary of Section 8. In the commutative settings (torus and coordinate-wise sphere), the correlation kernel generalises to a closed-form expression with tensor product structure. The spectral theory of the kernel matrix is accessible via this structure. In the non-commutative \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) setting, a Haar-averaged kernel can be defined and shares some structural properties (positive semi-definiteness, symmetry under conjugation), but it lacks a closed-form expression in terms of thresholds and depends on the full joint selection measure.

9. Discussion and Open Questions

9.1. Summary of results

We have established the following results about higher-dimensional generalisations of the greedy harmonic decomposition:

9.2. The role of commutativity

The central dichotomy revealed by this investigation is between the commutative and non-commutative settings. In the commutative case (intervals, tori, one-parameter subgroups, maximal tori), the full apparatus of one-dimensional theory—thresholds, monotonicity, closed-form kernels—carries over as a product construction. In the non-commutative case (full \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\) for \(d \geq 3\)), convergence of the greedy algorithm is preserved, but the selection structure is fundamentally altered by the phenomenon of residual rotation: earlier selections conjugate the residual, moving it between coordinate planes in a way that has no one-dimensional analogue.

This dichotomy is reminiscent of the distinction between commutative and non-commutative harmonic analysis. On a compact abelian group (such as \(\mathbb{T}^d\)), characters form an orthonormal basis and Fourier analysis decomposes completely into independent one-dimensional problems. On a non-abelian compact group (such as \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\)), irreducible representations are higher-dimensional and interactions between components are inescapable.

9.3. Open questions

Several directions for further investigation emerge from this work:

  1. Optimal convergence rate on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\). We proved convergence at rate \(O(d/N)\). Is this optimal, or can the dimensional factor be reduced to \(O(\sqrt{d}/N)\) or even \(O(1/N)\) by a more careful cycling strategy? In particular, is there an ordering of coordinate planes (perhaps adaptive) that achieves a better rate?
  2. Weak monotonicity on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\). Although pointwise monotonicity fails (Theorem 7.2), does a weaker form hold? For instance, is the selection probability \(\bar{\delta}_{n,p} = \int_{\mathrm{SO}(d)} \delta_{n,p}(R)\,d\mu_{\mathrm{Haar}}(R)\) monotone in \(n\)? If so, one could define "average thresholds" and construct a Haar-averaged kernel with partial closed-form structure.
  3. Other compact Lie groups. The construction extends naturally to any compact Lie group \(G\) with Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{g}\): choose a basis \(\{E_p\}\) for \(\mathfrak{g}\) and define elementary group elements \(\exp(\alpha_n E_p)\). The convergence argument of Theorem 6.1 applies whenever the basis is a spanning set. The monotonicity obstruction should persist whenever \(G\) is non-abelian. Special cases of interest include:
    • \(\mathrm{SU}(d)\): the special unitary group, relevant to quantum mechanics.
    • \(\mathrm{Sp}(d)\): the symplectic group, relevant to Hamiltonian mechanics.
    • The exceptional Lie groups \(G_2, F_4, E_6, E_7, E_8\).
  4. Hyperbolic spaces. The greedy decomposition on the hyperbolic plane \(\mathbb{H}^2\) (or higher-dimensional hyperbolic spaces) using a sequence of translations with harmonic step sizes is geometrically distinct from the spherical case. The non-compactness of the group of isometries and the exponential growth of volume introduce new phenomena.
  5. Quantisation of the correlation kernel. On \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\), the non-commutative corrections to the toral kernel are of order \(\alpha_n\alpha_m\). Can these corrections be computed perturbatively using the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion, yielding a "quantum correction" series for the kernel?
  6. Reconstruction of spherical harmonics. In one dimension, the greedy decomposition reconstructs \(\sin(\theta)\). What is the natural analogue on \(S^{d-1}\)? The spherical harmonics \(Y_\ell^m(\theta,\phi)\) are the higher-dimensional analogues of \(\sin(n\theta)\) and \(\cos(n\theta)\). Can the coordinate-wise greedy decomposition be used to reconstruct spherical harmonics, and if so, what is the convergence rate?
  7. Connection to cubature and quasi-Monte Carlo. The greedy selection of harmonic angles produces a deterministic set of points on the torus (or sphere). How does the resulting point set compare with known cubature formulas and quasi-Monte Carlo sequences for numerical integration on these domains?

9.4. Concluding remarks

The extension of the greedy harmonic decomposition to higher dimensions reveals a rich interplay between combinatorial algorithms, Lie group geometry, and harmonic analysis. The commutative settings admit a complete and elegant theory that is a direct product of the one-dimensional case. The non-commutative settings preserve the algorithmic convergence but necessitate fundamentally new approaches to the structural theory. The failure of selection monotonicity on \(\mathrm{SO}(d)\)—our main negative result—is not merely a technical obstacle but reflects a genuine geometric phenomenon: the conjugation of residuals by earlier selections. Understanding this phenomenon more deeply, and developing algebraic or analytic tools to replace the threshold theory in the non-commutative regime, is the central challenge for future work.
