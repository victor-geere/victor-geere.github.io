2. The Riemann Zeta Function and Its Moments

2.1 Definition of the Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function is defined for complex numbers \( s = \sigma + it \) with \(\sigma > 1\) as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} = \prod_p \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^s}\right)^{-1}, \]

where the product is over all prime numbers \( p \). It has an analytic continuation to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at \( s = 1 \). The critical line, where \(\sigma = \frac{1}{2}\), is central to the Riemann Hypothesis, which conjectures that all non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) have real part \(\frac{1}{2}\).

2.2 Moments of the Zeta Function

The moments of the zeta function on the critical line are integrals of the form:

\[ I_k(T) = \int_0^T \left| \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + it\right) \right|^{2k} \, dt, \]

which measure the average size of \(\left| \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + it\right) \right|^{2k}\) over \([0, T]\). These moments are crucial for understanding the distribution of zeta values and the zeros’ statistics. Historical results include Hardy and Littlewood’s second moment (\(k=1\)) and Ingham’s fourth moment (\(k=2\)).

3. Shifted Moments: Definition and Importance

Shifted moments generalize standard moments by introducing shifts \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2\) in the argument of the zeta function. For example, a shifted moment is:

\[ I_k(T, \alpha_1, \alpha_2) = \int_0^T \left| \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + i(t + \alpha_1)\right) \right|^k \left| \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + i(t + \alpha_2)\right) \right|^k \, dt, \]

where \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2\) are real-valued functions satisfying \(|\alpha_1|, |\alpha_2| \leq 0.5T\). When \(\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 0\), this reduces to the standard moment \(I_k(T)\). Shifted moments capture correlations between zeta values at nearby points, providing insights into the distribution of zeros and prime correlations.

4. The Sine Kernel in Random Matrix Theory

The sine kernel arises in random matrix theory, particularly in the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE), which models the eigenvalues of large Hermitian matrices. It is defined as:

\[ K(x, y) = \frac{\sin(\pi (x - y))}{\pi (x - y)}, \]

or in normalized form:

\[ K(u) = \frac{\sin(\pi u)}{\pi u}. \]

This kernel describes the pair correlation of eigenvalues, capturing level repulsion where eigenvalues avoid close proximity. Montgomery’s pair correlation conjecture suggests that the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function exhibit GUE-like statistics, with the sine kernel governing their local spacing.

5. Kösters’ Contributions: The Sine Kernel in Shifted Moments

5.1 Kösters’ 2008 Paper

In his 2008 paper, “The Riemann Zeta-Function and the Sine Kernel” (arXiv:0803.1141), Holger Kösters identified the sine kernel in the asymptotic behavior of shifted moments. He focused on the second and fourth moments with small shifts, such as \(\delta(t) = \frac{\pi \mu}{\log t}\).

5.2 Key Results

Kösters’ Theorem 1.1 states:

\[ \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{T \log T} \int_0^T \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + it + \frac{\pi \mu}{\log t}\right) \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} - it + \frac{\pi \nu}{\log t}\right) dt = e^{-i\pi(\mu-\nu)} \cdot \frac{\sin(\pi(\mu - \nu))}{\pi(\mu - \nu)}, \]

where the sine kernel appears on the right-hand side. For the fourth moment (Theorem 1.2), a related function involving \(\sin^2 x/2\) emerges. Kösters conjectured (Conjecture 1.3) that the sine kernel appears in higher even moments, aligning with the Conrey-Farmer-Keating-Rubinstein-Snaith (CFKRS) conjecture.

5.3 Why the Sine Kernel?

The sine kernel emerges because shifted moments relate to sums over zeta zeros via explicit formulas. For small shifts, zero correlations dominate, and the sine kernel captures these local statistics, mirroring GUE eigenvalue behavior.
