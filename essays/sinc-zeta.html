Introduction

The Riemann zeta function, denoted as \(\zeta(s)\), is a fundamental object in number theory, renowned for its deep ties to the distribution of prime numbers. This function, initially defined for complex numbers \(s\) with real part greater than 1, extends its influence through its zeros and product representations.

An intriguing connection has emerged between the zeta function and the sinc function, \(\text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x}\), particularly via the sine kernel. This link not only illuminates the statistical properties of the zeta function’s zeros but also connects number theory to random matrix theory, offering fresh insights into the behavior of primes.

Chapter 1: The Riemann Zeta Function

Definition and Basic Properties

The Riemann zeta function is defined by the infinite series: \[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \quad \text{for} \quad \text{Re}(s) > 1 \] This series converges absolutely when \(\text{Re}(s) > 1\). Through analytic continuation, \(\zeta(s)\) is extended to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at \(s = 1\).

Euler Product Formula

A key insight into the zeta function’s link to prime numbers comes from the Euler product formula: \[ \zeta(s) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^s}\right)^{-1} \quad \text{for} \quad \text{Re}(s) > 1 \] This product over all primes reveals how the zeta function encodes the distribution of prime numbers.

Chapter 2: Zeros of the Zeta Function

Trivial and Non-Trivial Zeros

The zeta function has zeros at the negative even integers, \(s = -2, -4, -6, \ldots\), called trivial zeros. It also possesses infinitely many non-trivial zeros in the critical strip \(0 < \text{Re}(s) < 1\), whose locations are central to number theory.

The Riemann Hypothesis

The Riemann Hypothesis conjectures that all non-trivial zeros lie on the critical line \(\text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2}\). This unproven statement, if true, would have significant implications for understanding prime number distribution.

Distribution of Zeros and the Sine Kernel

The pair correlation of non-trivial zeros exhibits patterns akin to those in random matrix theory, with the sine kernel, \(\frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x}\), appearing in the correlation function. This suggests a profound link between the zeta function and the sinc function.

Chapter 3: The Sinc Function and the Sine Kernel

Definition of the Sinc Function

The sinc function is defined as: \[ \text{sinc}(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} & \text{if } x \neq 0 \\ 1 & \text{if } x = 0 \end{cases} \] It plays a significant role in fields like Fourier analysis and signal processing.

Role in Pair Correlation of Zeta Zeros

Montgomery’s conjecture describes the pair correlation of zeta zeros with the function: \[ F(\alpha) = 1 - \left(\frac{\sin(\pi \alpha)}{\pi \alpha}\right)^2 \quad \text{for} \quad \alpha \neq 0 \] This matches the pair correlation of eigenvalues in the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE) from random matrix theory.

Connection to Random Matrix Theory

The similarity between the zeta function’s zeros and random matrix eigenvalues hints at a statistical analogy to chaotic quantum systems, a connection explored by researchers like Keating, Snaith, Borodin, and Strahov.

Chapter 4: Shifted Moments and the Sine Kernel

Explanation of Shifted Moments

Shifted moments involve integrals of zeta function products with small imaginary shifts, such as: \[ \int_0^T \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} + it + \frac{2\pi i \mu}{2 \log t}\right) \zeta\left(\frac{1}{2} - it - \frac{2\pi i \nu}{2 \log t}\right) \, dt \] These quantities reveal statistical properties of \(\zeta(s)\) along the critical line.

Appearance of the Sine Kernel in Moments

Studies, such as Kösters’ 2008 work, show that the sine kernel emerges in the asymptotics of shifted moments, with expressions like \(e^{-i\pi(\mu - \nu)} \cdot \text{sinc}(\pi(\mu - \nu))\), tying the sinc function to zeta’s behavior.

Implications for Prime Number Distribution

Through the explicit formula linking primes to zeta zeros, the sine kernel’s role in shifted moments influences the distribution of primes in short intervals, deepening our understanding of their statistical nature.

Chapter 5: Conclusion

The Riemann zeta function connects to prime numbers through its Euler product, zeros, and statistical properties, with the sinc function serving as a bridge to random matrix theory. These links highlight ongoing research directions and unresolved questions in number theory.
