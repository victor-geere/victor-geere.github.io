1. Introduction

The Riemann zeta function, initially defined as

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \]

for complex \( s \) with \( \text{Re}(s) > 1 \), extends via analytic continuation to the entire complex plane, excluding a simple pole at \( s = 1 \). Its non-trivial zeros, conjectured by the Riemann Hypothesis to reside on the critical line \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \), are deeply connected to the distribution of prime numbers. This lemma introduces two parametric functions, \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \), to explore the zeta function’s behavior through a continuous framework, focusing on symmetry and the critical line.

These functions arise from the zeta function’s integral representation and provide insights into its conjugate symmetry and zero locations. The goal is to establish a foundation that could contribute to a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis by elucidating the mechanisms governing the zeros.

2. Definitions and Parametric Functions

Consider a complex number \( s = \sigma + it \), where \( \sigma \) is the real part and \( t \) is the imaginary part. For real constants \( a \) and \( b \), and a positive real variable \( x > 0 \), we define:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \left[ a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x) \right] \]

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \left[ a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x) \right] \]

The term \( \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \) governs the decay and scaling of the functions, while the trigonometric terms \( \cos(t \log x) \) and \( \sin(t \log x) \) introduce oscillations dependent on \( t \). These functions will serve as tools to dissect the zeta function’s integral form.

They can also be written as: \[ f(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + b i) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (a - b i) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \] \[ g(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (b - a i) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (b + a i) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \]

3. Integral Representation of the Zeta Function

For \( \text{Re}(s) > 1 \), the zeta function admits an integral representation involving the gamma function:

\[ \Gamma(s) \zeta(s) = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \, dx \]

Writing \( x^{s - 1} = x^{\sigma - 1} e^{it \log x} \), and using Euler’s formula, we get:

\[ x^{s - 1} = x^{\sigma - 1} \left[ \cos(t \log x) + i \sin(t \log x) \right] \]

Thus, the integrand splits into real and imaginary parts:

\[ \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \cos(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} + i \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \sin(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} \]

Choosing \( a = 1 \) and \( b = 0 \), the parametric functions align with these components:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \cos(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} = \text{Re} \left( \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right) \]

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \sin(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} = \text{Im} \left( \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right) \]

Integrating \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \) from 0 to \( \infty \) yields the real and imaginary parts of \( \Gamma(s) \zeta(s) \), linking these functions directly to \( \zeta(s) \).

4. Symmetry and the Conjugate of \( s \)

For \( s = \sigma + it \), the conjugate is \( \overline{s} = \sigma - it \). The zeta function satisfies \( \zeta(\overline{s}) = \overline{\zeta(s)} \), and its functional equation relates \( \zeta(s) \) to \( \zeta(1 - s) \). To explore this symmetry, express the trigonometric terms in exponential form:

\[ \cos(t \log x) = \frac{e^{it \log x} + e^{-it \log x}}{2}, \quad \sin(t \log x) = \frac{e^{it \log x} - e^{-it \log x}}{2i} \]

Then, \( f(x, s) \) becomes:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + bi) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (a - bi) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \]

A similar expression holds for \( g(x, s) \). This reveals a symmetric dependence on \( s \) and \( \overline{s} \), paralleling the zeta function’s conjugate symmetry.

5. Significance of the Factor \( \frac{1}{2} \)

The factor \( \frac{1}{2} \) in the exponential forms of \( \cos \) and \( \sin \) ensures that contributions from \( x^{s - 1} \) and \( x^{\overline{s} - 1} \) are balanced. On the critical line, where \( s = \frac{1}{2} + it \), we have \( x^{s - 1} = x^{-\frac{1}{2} + it - 1} \), and the real part \( \sigma = \frac{1}{2} \) centers the symmetry. This balance may facilitate cancellations in the integral, contributing to the zeros of \( \zeta(s) \).

6. Connection to Zeta Zeros and the Critical Line

The Riemann Hypothesis asserts that all non-trivial zeros of \( \zeta(s) \) have \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \). The symmetry in \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \), especially the role of \( \frac{1}{2} \), suggests a mechanism for destructive interference when integrated, potentially yielding \( \zeta(s) = 0 \). The functional equation’s reflection across \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \) reinforces this, as the parametric functions encapsulate this symmetry continuously.

7. Conclusion

The parametric functions \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \) offer a continuous perspective on the Riemann zeta function, linking its integral representation to its symmetry and zeros. Their structure, particularly the symmetry across the critical line, provides a promising avenue for further investigation into the Riemann Hypothesis.
