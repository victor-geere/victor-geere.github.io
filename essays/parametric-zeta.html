1. Introduction to the Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann Zeta function, denoted as \( \zeta(s) \), is a cornerstone of analytic number theory. For a complex number \( s = \sigma + it \) with real part \( \sigma > 1 \), it is defined as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} \]

Through analytic continuation, \( \zeta(s) \) extends to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at \( s = 1 \). Its zeros are critical, with trivial zeros at negative even integers and non-trivial zeros in the critical strip \( 0 < \sigma < 1 \). The Riemann Hypothesis posits that all non-trivial zeros lie on \( \sigma = 0.5 \).

2. Parametric Functions \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \)

We explore \( \zeta(s) \) using its integral representation:

\[ \zeta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{x^{s-1}}{e^x - 1} \, dx, \quad \sigma > 1 \]

where \( \Gamma(s) \) is the gamma function. For \( s = \sigma + it \), we write:

\[ x^{s-1} = x^{\sigma - 1} e^{it \log x} = x^{\sigma - 1} [\cos(t \log x) + i \sin(t \log x)] \]

Let \( \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} = a + ib \), with \( a = \text{Re}(1/\Gamma(s)) \) and \( b = \text{Im}(1/\Gamma(s)) \). We define:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x)] \] \[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x)] \]

3. Role of Sine and Cosine in Parametric Functions

The terms \( \cos(t \log x) \) and \( \sin(t \log x) \) stem from \( e^{it \log x} \), capturing the oscillatory behavior of \( \zeta(s) \):

  • \( \cos(t \log x) \) influences the real part, shaping \( f(x, s) \).
  • \( \sin(t \log x) \) influences the imaginary part, shaping \( g(x, s) \).

These oscillations, driven by \( t \log x \), are key to the cancellations at \( \zeta(s) \) zeros.

4. Convergence and Zeta Zeros

For \( \sigma > 1 \), the integrals converge to:

\[ \int_0^\infty f(x, s) \, dx = \text{Re}(\zeta(s)), \quad \int_0^\infty g(x, s) \, dx = \text{Im}(\zeta(s)) \]

Both integrals are zero when \( \zeta(s) = 0 \), corresponding to its zeros.
