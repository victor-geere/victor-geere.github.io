Exploring Parametric Functions and the Riemann Zeta Function

Introduction

The Riemann zeta function is a fundamental object in mathematics, particularly in number theory. It is initially defined for complex numbers \( s \) with real part greater than 1 as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \]

Through analytic continuation, it is extended to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at \( s = 1 \). The function’s non-trivial zeros are believed to lie on the critical line \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \), a conjecture known as the Riemann Hypothesis. In this eBook, we introduce two parametric functions, \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \), which provide a continuous lens through which to view the zeta function’s behavior.

The Parametric Functions

For \( s = \sigma + it \), where \( \sigma \) is the real part and \( t \) is the imaginary part, and real constants \( a \) and \( b \), the functions are:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x)] \]

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x)] \]

Here, \( x > 0 \), and \( \log x \) is the natural logarithm. The term \( \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \) controls amplitude, while the trigonometric terms introduce oscillations based on \( t \).

Connection to the Riemann Zeta Function

The zeta function has an integral representation:

\[ \Gamma(s) \zeta(s) = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \, dx \]

for \( \text{Re}(s) > 1 \). Since \( x^{s - 1} = x^{\sigma - 1} [\cos(t \log x) + i \sin(t \log x)] \), we have:

\[ \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \cos(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} + i \frac{x^{\sigma - 1} \sin(t \log x)}{e^x - 1} \]

With \( a = 1 \), \( b = 0 \), \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \) are the real and imaginary parts, linking them to \( \zeta(s) \).

Symmetry and the Conjugate of \( s \)

The conjugate is \( \overline{s} = \sigma - it \). The functions can be written as:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + b i) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (a - b i) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \]

This symmetry reflects \( \zeta(\overline{s}) = \overline{\zeta(s)} \) and the functional equation’s properties.

The Significance of \( \frac{1}{2} \)

The \( \frac{1}{2} \) factor balances \( s \) and \( \overline{s} \) terms, crucial on the critical line \( s = \frac{1}{2} + it \), where it supports the cancellation of oscillations, aligning with the zeros’ location.

Conclusion

\( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \) offer a continuous view of \( \zeta(s) \), with symmetry and the \( \frac{1}{2} \) factor highlighting the critical line’s role in the Riemann Hypothesis.
