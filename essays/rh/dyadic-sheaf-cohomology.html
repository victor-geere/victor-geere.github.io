Introduction & Motivation

This document explores the purely speculative idea of a dyadic multiplicative sheaf cohomology on the adele ring \(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}\), proposed as a novel geometric approach toward proving the Riemann Hypothesis.

The central conjecture is that the first cohomology group \( H^1 \) of a carefully constructed complex should be:

  • trivial (acyclic) off the critical line \(\Re(s) \neq 1/2\),
  • one-dimensional (corresponding to zeros) exactly on the critical line.

This would geometrize the functional equation as an anti-involution of the complex itself, and potentially force the zeros to lie on \(\Re(s) = 1/2\) via a maximum principle or acyclicity argument.

Important disclaimer: This entire construction is conjectural and has no published precedent as of January 2026. It is an exploratory sketch only.

The Proposed Dyadic Sheaf Complex

We work on the adele ring \(\mathbb{A} = \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}\) with the restricted product topology. Introduce a dyadic filtration using powers of 2 at each place.

Define two sheaves (on the site of open subsets of \(\mathbb{A}^\times\)):

\[\mathcal{F}_0 \quad = \quad \text{sheaf of locally constant functions constant on dyadic balls } U_v = \{ x_v : |x_v|_v \in [2^{-k}, 2^{-k+1}) \text{ for some } k \}\]
\[\mathcal{F}_1 \quad = \quad \mathcal{F}_0 \otimes \chi_{\text{dyadic}}, \qquad \text{where } \chi_{\text{dyadic}}(x) = \exp\left(2\pi i \cdot v_2(\text{val}_v(x))\right)\]

(Here \( v_2 \) is the 2-adic valuation lifted to each place via the canonical embedding.)

The complex is

\[0 \longrightarrow \mathcal{F}_0 \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{F}_1 \longrightarrow 0\]

with differential \( d \) incorporating the idele norm and reflection \( s \mapsto 1-s \) via a twisted coboundary operator.

p-adic Sheaf Cohomology — Analogies & Differences

Classical p-adic (or ℓ-adic) cohomology, as used in the proof of the Weil conjectures (Deligne 1974), provides a powerful analogy. In the finite-field case, ℓ-adic cohomology of varieties yields zeta functions whose zeros satisfy an analogue of the Riemann hypothesis (with eigenvalues of Frobenius having absolute value \( q^{i/2} \)).

Key differences when trying to lift this to the adeles / Spec \(\mathbb{Z}\):

  • ℓ-adic cohomology works over finite fields with Frobenius acting; over Spec \(\mathbb{Z}\) there is no natural Frobenius.
  • p-adic cohomology theories (Monsky–Washnitzer, rigid cohomology, overconvergent F-isocrystals) are used to compute zeta functions of varieties over finite fields, but extending them to global fields or the adeles remains extremely difficult.
  • The dyadic approach here is deliberately not p-adic in the usual sense: it uses powers of 2 at every place (including archimedean) and focuses on dyadic (binary) decompositions rather than ℓ-adic continuity.

The hope is that the dyadic filtration provides a new kind of “arithmetic continuity” that ℓ-adic theories miss when moving to characteristic zero or to the adeles.

Explicit Differential Operator d

We propose the following conjectural definition of the differential \( d: \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1 \):

\[(d f)(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} 2^{-n} \sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} \left( f(2^{-n} \cdot \omega^k \cdot x) - f(x) \right) \cdot \chi_{\text{dyadic}}(\omega^k x)\]

where \(\omega = e^{2\pi i / 2^n}\) is a primitive \(2^n\)-th root of unity (dyadic approximation of circle), and the sum is taken over a dyadic approximation of the scaling orbit.

Motivation:

  • This resembles a discrete derivative along dyadic scalings
  • The twisting by \(\chi_{\text{dyadic}}\) incorporates the character that measures 2-adic order
  • The factor \(2^{-n}\) normalizes the average, suggesting a kind of “logarithmic derivative”
  • The limit as \(n\to\infty\) is conjectural (convergence in adelic topology)

A more algebraic version (avoiding roots of unity) could be:

\[(d f)(x) = \int_{\mathbb{A}^\times / \mathbb{Q}^\times} \left( f(2y) - f(y) \right) \cdot \chi_{\text{dyadic}}(y) \, d^\times y\]

with respect to a suitable Haar measure on the idele class group, but convergence and well-definedness are major open questions.

Computing H⁰ — Global Sections

The global sections \(\Gamma(\mathbb{A}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0)\) consist of functions constant on dyadic balls at every place, i.e., functions depending only on the dyadic valuations at finitely many places.

Because the dyadic group is generated by scaling by powers of 2, the space of global sections is expected to be related to functions invariant under the idele class group action modulo dyadic units.

\[H^0 \simeq \mathbb{C} \quad \text{(constant functions)} \quad \text{or possibly larger if cut-off is dynamic}\]

This is the trivial part; the interesting information sits in higher cohomology.

Computing H¹ — First Cohomology (Speculative Sketch)

The first cohomology \( H^1(\mathbb{A}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1) \) is conjecturally the space of "cocycles modulo coboundaries" twisted by the dyadic character.

A 1-cocycle is a collection of sections \( c(g) \in \Gamma(U_g, \mathcal{F}_1) \) satisfying the cocycle condition with respect to the covering by dyadic balls and the scaling action.

The key conjecture is:

There exists an anti-involution \(\tau\) on the complex induced by the reflection \( s \mapsto 1-s \) (via the functional equation) such that \[\tau^2 = \mathrm{id} \quad \text{only when} \quad \Re(s) = 1/2.\] Off the critical line, \(\tau^2 \neq \mathrm{id}\) forces the complex to be acyclic (\( H^1 = 0 \)).

On the critical line, a delicate cancellation occurs, producing exactly one-dimensional cohomology in each degree, with basis corresponding to each non-trivial zero.

This is the most speculative part: no explicit computation exists. The hope is that acyclicity off-line follows from a maximum principle on the adelic height function, while the critical line allows a Morse-type cancellation producing exactly the dimension of the space of zeros.

Relation to the Functional Equation & Critical Line

The functional equation \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\) should be realized as the statement that the anti-involution \(\tau\) satisfies

\[\tau^2 = \mathrm{id} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \Re(s) = \frac{1}{2}.\]

This would imply that non-trivial cohomology (hence zeros) can only appear when the symmetry squares to the identity — i.e., precisely on the critical line.

Major Obstacles & Next Steps

  1. Define the differential \(d\) explicitly using the idele norm and reflection map.
  2. Prove that the Euler characteristic (suitably regularized) equals \(\xi(s)\).
  3. Establish a maximum principle for the adelic height that forces acyclicity off-line.
  4. Show that on the critical line the complex has exactly one-dimensional cohomology per degree.
  5. Connect the resulting cohomology classes to the known zeros of \(\zeta(s)\).

This entire program is currently at the level of a thought experiment. No calculations, no theorems, no evidence — yet.

Conclusion

The first dyadic sheaf cohomology remains undefined and uncomputed as of January 11, 2026. The sketch above is a speculative proposal for what such a computation might look like if the dyadic sheaf complex were ever made rigorous. It represents one of the most novel (and currently most distant) directions that could, in principle, geometrize the Riemann Hypothesis via sheaf cohomology on the adeles.
