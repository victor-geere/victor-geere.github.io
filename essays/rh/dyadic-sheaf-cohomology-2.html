Critical disclaimer
This is **not** an existing mathematical theory. No paper, no preprint, no calculation, no evidence supports this construction as of January 2026. It is a purely speculative formalization created for the purpose of exploring whether sheaf cohomology with a dyadic flavor could geometrize the functional equation and the critical line property.

1. Site and Base Space

We work over the multiplicative group of ideles (or adele ring) equipped with a dyadic filtration.

Base space: Let \( X = \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times / \mathbb{Q}^\times \), the idele class group (with the quotient topology). Alternatively (more convenient for sheaves): consider the site consisting of open subsets of \( \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times \) with the restricted product topology.

We equip every local component with a **dyadic filtration**:

\[U_v^{(k)} = \{ x_v \in \mathbb{Q}_v^\times : 2^{-k} \leq |x_v|_v < 2^{-k+1} \}\]
for each place \( v \) (including archimedean places, where \( | \cdot |_\infty \) is the usual absolute value).

2. Definition of the Sheaves

Sheaf \(\mathcal{F}_0\) (degree 0 — dyadic locally constant functions)
For an open set \( U \subset \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times \), \[ \mathcal{F}_0(U) = \{ f: U \to \mathbb{C} \mid f \text{ is locally constant and constant on all dyadic boxes } U \cap \prod_v U_v^{(k_v)} \text{ for finitely many } v \} \]
Sheaf \(\mathcal{F}_1\) (degree 1 — twisted version)
\[ \mathcal{F}_1 := \mathcal{F}_0 \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \underline{\chi}_{\mathrm{dyadic}} \] where the locally constant sheaf \(\underline{\chi}_{\mathrm{dyadic}}\) is defined by \[ \chi_{\mathrm{dyadic}}(x) = \exp\left(2\pi i \sum_v v_2(\mathrm{ord}_v(x))\right) \] (with \( v_2 \) the 2-adic valuation extended in the obvious way to each place).

3. The Short Dyadic Complex

We consider the complex of sheaves
\[0 \longrightarrow \mathcal{F}_0 \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{F}_1 \longrightarrow 0\]
This is a complex in the abelian category of sheaves of \(\mathbb{C}\)-vector spaces on the site of \(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times\). The **global cohomology groups** are defined in the usual way:
\[H^q(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_\bullet) := H^q \bigl( \Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_\bullet) \bigr)\]
and the **hypercohomology** (if needed) via Godement or Čech resolutions.

4. Conjectural Differential Operator d

The most delicate part is defining a natural differential \( d: \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1 \). **Conjectural definition A** (dyadic difference operator — preferred):
\[(df)(x) \;:=\; \lim_{n\to\infty} 2^{-n} \sum_{k=0}^{2^n - 1} \bigl( f(2^{-n} \omega^k \, x) - f(x) \bigr) \cdot \chi_{\mathrm{dyadic}}(\omega^k x)\]
where \( \omega = e^{2\pi i / 2^n} \) (primitive \(2^n\)-th root of unity). **Conjectural definition B** (more algebraic — integral form):
\[(df)(x) \;:=\; \int_{\mathbb{A}^\times / \mathbb{Q}^\times} \bigl( f(2y) - f(y) \bigr) \cdot \chi_{\mathrm{dyadic}}(y) \, d^\times y\]
(with respect to a suitably normalized Haar measure on the idele class group). Both versions are currently **ill-defined** without further regularization and convergence proofs.

5. The Anti-Involution τ

The functional equation symmetry is encoded as an anti-involution on the complex.
Conjectural anti-involution \(\tau\): There exists a contravariant involution \[ \tau \colon \mathcal{F}_\bullet \;\longrightarrow\; \mathcal{F}_\bullet^\vee \] induced by the map \( x \mapsto 1/\overline{x} \) (reflection through the unit circle in each local component) together with the functional equation reflection \( s \mapsto 1-s \), such that \[ \tau^2 = \mathrm{id} \qquad \text{only when} \qquad \Re(s) = \frac{1}{2}. \]
This is the most speculative (and potentially most powerful) part of the construction.

6. The Cohomology Groups

The cohomology groups of our dyadic sheaf complex are the central objects of study. They encode the "obstructions" to solving the differential equation \(df = 0\) in the sheaf-theoretic sense, and conjecturally capture the zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

In sheaf cohomology, the cohomology groups \(H^q(X, \mathcal{F})\) measure the failure of the sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) to be acyclic on the space \(X\). For a complex of sheaves \(0 \to \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1 \to 0\), the hypercohomology groups \(H^q(X, \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1)\) are computed as the cohomology of the complex of global sections.

6.1 Global Sections and H⁰

The zeroth cohomology group \(H^0(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0)\) consists of the global sections of \(\mathcal{F}_0\), i.e., functions that are defined everywhere on the adele ring and satisfy the dyadic locality condition.

\[H^0(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0) := \Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0)\]

Since \(\mathcal{F}_0\) consists of functions that are locally constant with respect to the dyadic filtration, and the adele ring has many connected components in this topology, the space of global sections is expected to be quite small. In fact, we conjecture that it consists only of constant functions that are constant across all dyadic scales at every place.

\[H^0 \simeq \mathbb{C} \cdot 1 \quad \text{(the constant function 1)}\]

This reflects the fact that there are no non-trivial global "dyadic harmonic functions" on the adele ring — everything that is locally constant must eventually become constant globally due to the infinite product structure.

6.2 First Cohomology and the Differential Complex

The first cohomology group is more interesting and conjecturally contains the information about the Riemann zeros. For a short exact sequence of sheaves \(0 \to \mathcal{F}_0 \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{F}_1 \to 0\), the first hypercohomology group can be computed using the connecting homomorphism in the long exact sequence of cohomology.

More concretely, \(H^1(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1)\) is the cohomology of the complex:

\[\Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0) \xrightarrow{d} \Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_1)\]

Since \(\mathcal{F}_0\) has few global sections (essentially just constants), the kernel of \(d\) on global sections is trivial. The first cohomology is therefore the cokernel of the global differential map:

\[H^1 := \coker\left( \Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0) \xrightarrow{d} \Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_1) \right)\]

In other words, \(H^1\) measures the "failure" of the image of \(d\) to fill the entire space of global sections of \(\mathcal{F}_1\). Each element of \(H^1\) corresponds to a global section of \(\mathcal{F}_1\) that cannot be written as \(df\) for some global section \(f\) of \(\mathcal{F}_0\).

(Here \(\operatorname{coker} d = \mathcal{F}_1 / \operatorname{im} d\) is the cokernel of the differential \(d\), i.e., the quotient of the target sheaf by the image of \(d\).)

6.3 Connection to the Riemann Hypothesis

The central conjecture is that the dimension of \(H^1\) is controlled by the location of the complex parameter \(s\) relative to the critical line \(\Re(s) = 1/2\):

\[ \dim_\mathbb{C} H^1(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_0 \to \mathcal{F}_1) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \Re(s) \neq 1/2 \\ r & \text{if } \Re(s) = 1/2 \text{ and } s \text{ is a non-trivial zero (multiplicity } r\text{)} \end{cases} \]

This geometrizes the Riemann Hypothesis in the following way: the functional equation \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\) is encoded in the anti-involution \(\tau\) on the complex, and the critical line property emerges as the condition under which this involution squares to the identity. When \(\tau^2 \neq \mathrm{id}\), the complex is forced to be acyclic (no cohomology), while when \(\tau^2 = \mathrm{id}\), the cohomology captures exactly the zeros.

The intuition is that off the critical line, the "twisting" introduced by the functional equation creates a mismatch that prevents any non-trivial cohomology from existing. On the critical line, this mismatch resolves, and each zero corresponds to a single degree of freedom in the cohomology.

7. Central Conjectures

  1. The differential \(d\) is well-defined (after suitable regularization) and the complex is bounded.
  2. The anti-involution \(\tau\) satisfies \(\tau^2 = \mathrm{id}\) if and only if \(\Re(s) = 1/2\).
  3. Off the critical line the complex is acyclic (\(H^1 = 0\)) due to a maximum principle on adelic height.
  4. On the critical line, the cohomology is exactly one-dimensional per simple zero (or multiplicity \(r\)).
  5. The Euler characteristic (suitably defined) is equal to \(\xi(s)\).

8. Sketch of Possible Proofs

While this entire construction is conjectural, we can sketch what a rigorous proof of the central conjectures might look like. The proof would likely proceed in several stages, combining techniques from number theory, harmonic analysis, and algebraic geometry.

8.1 Establishing the Differential Operator

The first step would be to make the differential operator \(d\) mathematically rigorous. For the preferred dyadic difference operator:

\[(df)(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} 2^{-n} \sum_{k=0}^{2^n - 1} \bigl( f(2^{-n} \omega^k \, x) - f(x) \bigr) \cdot \chi_{\mathrm{dyadic}}(\omega^k x)\]

Possible approach: Show that this limit converges in the topology of the adele ring, using the fact that \(\mathcal{F}_0\) consists of functions that vary slowly on dyadic scales. The convergence could be established using:

  • Properties of the dyadic filtration and the restricted product topology
  • Harmonic analysis on the adele ring (Fourier analysis with respect to the dyadic character)
  • Regularization techniques similar to those used in p-adic cohomology

8.2 Proving the Anti-Involution Property

The key insight is that the anti-involution \(\tau\) should encode the functional equation \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\). A proof might proceed by:

  1. Constructing \(\tau\) explicitly using the reflection \(x \mapsto 1/\overline{x}\) on each local component
  2. Showing that \(\tau\) commutes with the differential \(d\) up to the functional equation factor
  3. Demonstrating that \(\tau^2 = \mathrm{id}\) precisely when the functional equation holds, using the fact that \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\) only when \(\Re(s) = 1/2\)

This would likely involve detailed calculations with the completed zeta function and its functional equation.

8.3 Establishing Acyclicity Off the Critical Line

To prove that \(H^1 = 0\) when \(\Re(s) \neq 1/2\), one could use a maximum principle argument:

  • Define an "adelic height function" or energy functional on the cohomology classes
  • Show that when \(\tau^2 \neq \mathrm{id}\), this functional achieves a maximum on the boundary of the fundamental domain
  • Use the maximum principle to force all cohomology classes to be trivial

This approach would be analogous to how maximum principles are used in complex analysis and PDE theory to prove uniqueness results.

8.4 Counting Cohomology on the Critical Line

On the critical line \(\Re(s) = 1/2\), the cohomology should be one-dimensional per zero. A proof might involve:

  • Showing that when \(\tau^2 = \mathrm{id}\), the complex becomes self-dual in some sense
  • Using the explicit formula for the zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) to construct a basis for the cohomology
  • Establishing a correspondence between non-trivial zeros and cohomology classes via the Mellin transform or Fourier analysis

The dimension count would follow from the fact that each simple zero contributes exactly one degree of freedom to the cohomology space.

8.5 Relating to the Completed Zeta Function

The final and most crucial step is establishing that the Euler characteristic of our dyadic sheaf complex equals the completed Riemann zeta function \(\xi(s)\). This would provide the bridge between the sheaf-theoretic construction and classical analytic number theory.

For a complex of sheaves \(0 \to \mathcal{F}_0 \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{F}_1 \to 0\), the Euler characteristic is defined as:

\[\chi(\mathcal{F}_\bullet) = \sum_{q=0}^\infty (-1)^q \dim H^q(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_\bullet)\]

Given our conjectures about the cohomology dimensions, this would simplify to:

\[\chi = \dim H^0 - \dim H^1 = \begin{cases} 1 - 0 = 1 & \text{if } \Re(s) \neq 1/2 \\ 1 - r & \text{if } \Re(s) = 1/2 \text{ and there are } r \text{ zeros at } s \end{cases}\]

The completed zeta function \(\xi(s)\) has the property that \(\xi(s) = 0\) precisely when \(s\) is a non-trivial zero of \(\zeta(s)\), and \(\xi(s) \neq 0\) otherwise. However, \(\xi(s)\) is never zero off the critical line (by the functional equation), and on the critical line it vanishes exactly at the zeros with appropriate multiplicity.

Sketch of proof: The key insight is that the Euler characteristic should be related to the zeta function through the Lefschetz fixed-point formula or an analogue in adele geometry. Consider the following approach:

8.5.1 The Trace Formula Connection

One possible route is through the Arthur-Selberg trace formula adapted to our sheaf-theoretic setting. The trace formula relates the spectral side (automorphic forms) to the geometric side (orbital integrals) of the adele group.

For our dyadic complex, we could define a "trace" operation that counts fixed points of the scaling operator \(x \mapsto 2x\) on the cohomology groups. The Lefschetz number would then be:

\[L(s) = \sum_{q=0}^\infty (-1)^q \mathrm{Tr}\left( (2x)^* \mid H^q(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_\bullet) \right)\]

By conjectures 1-4, this Lefschetz number should equal the Euler characteristic when \(\Re(s) = 1/2\), and be related to \(\xi(s)\) through the functional equation.

8.5.2 Mellin Transform Approach

Alternatively, consider the Mellin transform of the dyadic measure. The differential operator \(d\) can be viewed as a discrete analogue of the logarithmic derivative. The space of global sections \(\Gamma(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times, \mathcal{F}_1)\) contains functions that are "harmonic" with respect to the dyadic Laplacian.

The dimension of the cokernel \(H^1\) could be related to the growth rate of the number of dyadic harmonic functions, which in turn connects to the analytic properties of \(\zeta(s)\) via the explicit formula:

\[\psi(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} \Lambda(n) = \frac{x^{1/2}}{\log x} \sum_\rho \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} + \cdots\]

where \(\Lambda(n)\) is the von Mangoldt function. The dyadic analogue would count the number of "dyadic primes" or scaling orbits, leading to a similar explicit formula.

8.5.3 Functional Equation Compatibility

The functional equation \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\) should be reflected in the behavior of the Euler characteristic under the involution \(\tau\). Since \(\tau\) squares to the identity precisely on the critical line, the Euler characteristic should satisfy:

\[\chi(s) = \chi(1-s) \cdot \epsilon(s)\]

where \(\epsilon(s)\) is some correction factor related to the root number or functional equation factor. This would ensure that \(\chi(s)\) vanishes precisely when \(\xi(s) = 0\), i.e., at the non-trivial zeros.

8.5.4 Analytic Continuation Argument

Finally, one could establish the equality \(\chi(s) = \xi(s)\) by analytic continuation. Since both \(\chi(s)\) and \(\xi(s)\) are meromorphic functions with the same functional equation and zeros/poles, they must be equal up to a constant factor.

More precisely:

  1. Show that \(\chi(s)\) is meromorphic with the same poles as \(\xi(s)\) (at s=0,1)
  2. Establish that \(\chi(s) = \chi(1-s)\) times the appropriate gamma factor
  3. Verify that \(\chi(s)\) vanishes at the known zeros of \(\zeta(s)\)
  4. Use the fact that both functions are determined by their functional equation and zeros

This analytic continuation argument would provide the rigorous link between the sheaf-theoretic Euler characteristic and the completed Riemann zeta function, completing the geometrization of the Riemann Hypothesis.

This final step represents the most ambitious part of the program. Establishing the precise relationship between the sheaf cohomology and the zeta function would require deep insights into the arithmetic geometry of the adele ring and the analytic theory of L-functions. Success here would not only prove the Riemann Hypothesis but also provide a new geometric interpretation of zeta functions in terms of sheaf cohomology on arithmetic spaces.
