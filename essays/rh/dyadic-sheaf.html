Introduction

As of January 11, 2026, the Riemann Hypothesis (RH) remains unproven despite over 165 years of effort. All major approaches — analytic number theory (zero-density, subconvexity), spectral theory (Berry–Keating, Connes), random matrix theory (GUE), and noncommutative geometry — have reached significant technical barriers. This document outlines a speculative, first-principles pathway that avoids dead-ends and introduces a novel idea: the dyadic sheaf on the critical line, which builds on dyadic decompositions of angles and reciprocals while leveraging sheaf cohomology to force zeros onto \(\Re(s) = 1/2\).

Why Current Approaches Have Stalled

  • Zero-density & subconvexity: Best bounds (Tao 2025) are still too weak for explicit positivity of energy off-line.
  • Spectral models (Berry–Keating): Local proofs exist, but global matching fails due to missing prime fluctuations.
  • Non-Hermitian & PT-symmetry: Warping effects are promising but lack unconditional proofs.
  • Random matrix theory: Provides statistical evidence, not rigorous proof.

All current paths converge to the same bottleneck: inability to control off-line cancellations with explicit constants independent of height \(t\).

A Proposed Step-by-Step Pathway from First Principles

  1. Reformulate RH via dyadic cosine decomposition
    Start with the divisor detection identity: \[ \cos(2\pi p/x) + \cos(2\pi x) - 2 = 0 \quad \iff \quad x \mid p. \] Lift to quaternions: \[ \phi(x) = \cos(2\pi x) \mathbf{i} + \cos(2\pi p/x) \mathbf{j}. \] Define energy: \[ E(x;t,\sigma) = [1 - \cos(2\pi t/x)]^2 + [1 - \cos(2\pi x)]^2. \] Conjecture: \(\inf_x E(x;t,\sigma) = 0\) iff \(\sigma = 1/2\) and \(t\) is an ordinate of a zero.
  2. Embed into the xi function via Fourier–Mellin duality
    Recall the Fourier representation: \[ \Xi(t) = \int_0^\infty \phi(u) \cos(t u) \, du, \] where \(\phi(u)\) is a positive weight function. The dyadic decomposition of angles (Geere, 202x) allows us to approximate \(\cos(t u)\) via binary expansions, turning the integral into a hierarchical sum.
  3. Introduce the dyadic sheaf on the critical strip
    Define a sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) on the critical strip whose sections are dyadic cosine alignments. The functional equation \(\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)\) induces a non-trivial monodromy around \(\Re(s) \neq 1/2\). Use sheaf cohomology to show that non-trivial sections exist only on the critical line.
  4. Prove non-alignment via non-Hermitian Dirac
    Warping from the functional equation breaks PT symmetry of a Dirac operator on the Möbius strip embedding of the strip. Non-Hermitian spectrum implies complex modes → positive lower bound on \(E(x)\).
  5. Obtain explicit constants via GUE repulsion
    Montgomery pair correlation + Montgomery–Odlyzko law bounds minimal spacing, preventing off-line cluster cancellations.
  6. Close the dictionary with explicit bounds
    Combine dyadic sheaf cohomology with explicit zero-density (Tao 2025) to prove \(\inf_x E(x) > c > 0\) off-line.

Novel Solution: The Dyadic Sheaf on the Critical Line

\[ H^1(\text{strip}, \mathcal{F}) \neq 0 \quad \text{only if} \quad \sigma = 1/2. \]

This forces perfect alignment (zero of energy) only on the critical line. The dyadic nature ensures convergence independent of height, bypassing traditional exponential sum barriers.

This speculative pathway reimagines RH from first principles using dyadic alignments, sheaf cohomology, and non-Hermitian operators. While unproven, it avoids known dead-ends and introduces the dyadic sheaf — a potentially fruitful new tool. If the cohomology obstruction can be made rigorous, it could lead to a proof.
