1. Introduction

Here we connect periodic trigonometric functions with theta functions, number theory, and the Riemann zeta function. Our starting point is a curious function defined as \( f(x) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) + \cos(2\pi x) - 2 \), where \( k \) is a positive integer. This function is special because its roots occur precisely at the integer factors of \( k \). Our goal is to explore this function, use it as a stepping stone to introduce the theta function, and ultimately connect these ideas to the Riemann zeta function—a pivotal entity in analytic number theory. Along the way, we'll uncover the deep relationships that tie these mathematical objects together.

2. Exploring the Function \( f(x) \)

Let’s begin by examining the function at the heart of our exploration:

\[ f(x) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) + \cos(2\pi x) - 2, \]

where \( k \) is a fixed positive integer. The key property of this function is that it equals zero when \( x \) is an integer divisor of \( k \), including both positive and negative integers. To understand why, consider \( x = d \), where \( d \) is an integer such that \( d \mid k \). This means \( k = d \cdot m \) for some integer \( m \).

Substituting into the function:

\[ f(d) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{d}\right) + \cos(2\pi d) - 2. \]

Since \( \frac{k}{d} = m \) is an integer, we have:

\[ \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{d}\right) = \cos(2\pi m) = 1, \]

and because \( d \) is an integer:

\[ \cos(2\pi d) = 1. \]

Thus:

\[ f(d) = 1 + 1 - 2 = 0. \]

Since the cosine function is even, \( f(-d) = f(d) = 0 \), so the roots include both \( d \) and \( -d \). For example, if \( k = 6 \), the integer divisors are \( \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 6 \), and \( f(x) = 0 \) at each of these points.

Are these the only roots? For \( f(x) = 0 \), we need \( \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) = 1 \) and \( \cos(2\pi x) = 1 \) simultaneously (since their sum must be 2). This occurs when \( \frac{k}{x} \) and \( x \) are both integers, which is exactly the condition for \( x \) to be an integer divisor of \( k \). Thus, the roots of \( f(x) \) are precisely the integer factors of \( k \).

Moreover, these zeros are double zeros. The derivative is:

\[ f'(x) = \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) + \cos(2\pi x) - 2 \right] = -\sin\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) \cdot \left(2\pi \frac{k}{x^2}\right) - 2\pi \sin(2\pi x). \]

At \( x = d \), since \( \frac{k}{d} \) and \( d \) are integers, \( \sin\left(2\pi \frac{k}{d}\right) = 0 \) and \( \sin(2\pi d) = 0 \), so \( f'(d) = 0 \). This indicates that each root has multiplicity two, a property that may hint at deeper structural features.

3. Divisors and Number Theory

The roots of \( f(x) \) spotlight the concept of divisors, a fundamental idea in number theory. For a positive integer \( k \), its integer divisors are all integers \( d \) (positive and negative) such that \( k = d \cdot m \) for some integer \( m \). The number of positive divisors, denoted \( \tau(k) \), and their sum, \( \sigma(k) \), are arithmetic functions that reveal much about the structure of \( k \).

Divisors connect to many areas of mathematics. For instance, the generating function for \( \tau(k) \) is related to the square of the Riemann zeta function:

\[ \zeta(s)^2 = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\tau(n)}{n^s}, \quad \Re(s) > 1. \]

This suggests a potential bridge to our later topics. For now, \( f(x) \)’s dependence on divisors motivates us to explore functions defined over integers, such as the theta function, which we introduce next.

4. Understanding the Theta Function

Theta functions are elegant sums that arise in number theory, elliptic functions, and beyond. The theta function relevant to our discussion is:

\[ \theta(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\pi n^2 t}, \]

defined for \( t > 0 \). This infinite series converges rapidly due to the exponential decay of \( e^{-\pi n^2 t} \). One of its most striking properties is its functional equation, which relates \( \theta(t) \) to \( \theta(1/t) \):

\[ \theta\left(\frac{1}{t}\right) = \sqrt{t} \, \theta(t). \]

This can be proven using the Poisson summation formula, reflecting the theta function’s symmetry under the transformation \( t \to 1/t \). The function is positive for all \( t > 0 \) and does not have real zeros, but its generalizations (e.g., Jacobi theta functions) can have zeros at specific points in the complex plane.

Theta functions are deeply tied to lattices and quadratic forms, making them a natural tool for studying sums over integers—similar to how \( f(x) \) involves integers via its roots. This sets the stage for connecting to the Riemann zeta function.

5. Connecting Theta Functions to the Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function is defined as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}, \quad \Re(s) > 1, \]

and extends to the entire complex plane via analytic continuation. Its functional equation is:

\[ \zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1 - s) \zeta(1 - s). \]

The theta function provides a pathway to this equation. Consider:

\[ \frac{\theta(t) - 1}{2} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} e^{-\pi n^2 t}. \]

Taking the Mellin transform:

\[ \int_0^{\infty} \left( \frac{\theta(t) - 1}{2} \right) t^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \, dt = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\pi n^2 t} t^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \, dt. \]

For each term, let \( u = \pi n^2 t \), so \( t = \frac{u}{\pi n^2} \), \( dt = \frac{du}{\pi n^2} \):

\[ \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\pi n^2 t} t^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \, dt = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u} \left( \frac{u}{\pi n^2} \right)^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \frac{du}{\pi n^2} = (\pi n^2)^{-\frac{s}{2}} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u} u^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \, du = \pi^{-\frac{s}{2}} n^{-s} \Gamma\left(\frac{s}{2}\right). \]

Thus:

\[ \int_0^{\infty} \left( \frac{\theta(t) - 1}{2} \right) t^{\frac{s}{2} - 1} \, dt = \pi^{-\frac{s}{2}} \Gamma\left(\frac{s}{2}\right) \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^{-s} = \pi^{-\frac{s}{2}} \Gamma\left(\frac{s}{2}\right) \zeta(s). \]

Splitting the integral from 0 to 1 and 1 to ∞, and applying \( \theta(1/t) = \sqrt{t} \theta(t) \), yields the functional equation, showcasing the theta function’s role in number theory.

6. Relating \( f(x) \) to Theta Functions

Can we directly link \( f(x) \) to \( \theta(t) \)? Since \( f(x) \) has double zeros at the integer divisors of \( k \), and \( \theta(t) \) is a sum over all integers, a direct expression is elusive. However, consider a sum over divisors inspired by \( f(x) \)’s roots:

\[ s(t) = \sum_{\substack{d \mid k \\ d \in \mathbb{Z}}} e^{-\pi d^2 t}. \]

For \( k > 0 \), since \( e^{-\pi d^2 t} = e^{-\pi (-d)^2 t} \), this is:

\[ s(t) = 2 \sum_{\substack{d \mid k \\ d > 0}} e^{-\pi d^2 t}, \]

a finite sum resembling a restricted theta function. While not equal to \( \theta(t) \), it connects the divisors of \( k \) to exponential sums, echoing theta function forms. Alternatively, \( f(x) \)’s terms \( \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) \) and \( \cos(2\pi x) \) suggest a duality between \( x \) and \( \frac{k}{x} \), reminiscent of \( \theta(t) \)’s \( t \) and \( 1/t \), though a precise mapping remains indirect.

Thus, \( f(x) \) motivates studying sums over divisors, which theta functions generalize over all integers, bridging to the zeta function.

7. Conclusion

This ebook began with \( f(x) \), whose roots at the integer divisors of \( k \) opened a window into number theory. We explored the theta function \( \theta(t) \), with its beautiful functional equation, and linked it to the Riemann zeta function via integral transforms. While a direct tie between \( f(x) \) and \( \theta(t) \) is subtle, the journey illustrates how specific functions can lead to universal concepts, weaving a rich mathematical narrative from divisors to infinite series.
