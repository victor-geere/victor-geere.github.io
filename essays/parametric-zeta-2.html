Exploring Parametric Functions and the Riemann Zeta Function

In this eBook, we delve into the fascinating world of parametric functions and their connection to the Riemann zeta function. We will explore how to convert trigonometric functions into their exponential forms using Euler's formulas and analyze the resulting expressions to gain insights into the behavior of these functions.

Introduction

We are given two parametric functions:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x)] \]

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} [a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x)] \]

where \( s = \sigma + it \) is a complex parameter, with \( \sigma \) being the real part and \( t \) the imaginary part, and \( a \) and \( b \) are real constants. These functions exhibit oscillatory behavior due to the presence of sine and cosine terms, and they are related to the Riemann zeta function through their structure and properties. Our goal is to transform the trigonometric components into exponential forms and explore their significance.

Section 1: Converting Sine and Cosine to Exponential Forms

To analyze the oscillatory nature of \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \), we begin by converting the sine and cosine functions into their exponential equivalents using Euler's formulas. These formulas bridge the gap between trigonometric and exponential functions and are defined as:

\[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2} \]

\[ \sin(\theta) = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i} \]

In our functions, the argument of the trigonometric terms is \( \theta = t \log x \). Substituting this into Euler's formulas, we get:

\[ \cos(t \log x) = \frac{e^{i t \log x} + e^{-i t \log x}}{2} \]

\[ \sin(t \log x) = \frac{e^{i t \log x} - e^{-i t \log x}}{2i} \]

Since \( e^{i t \log x} = x^{it} \) and \( e^{-i t \log x} = x^{-it} \) (because \( e^{i \alpha \log x} = x^{i \alpha} \)), we can rewrite these expressions as:

\[ \cos(t \log x) = \frac{x^{it} + x^{-it}}{2} \]

\[ \sin(t \log x) = \frac{x^{it} - x^{-it}}{2i} \]

Applying to \( f(x, s) \)

The oscillatory component of \( f(x, s) \) is \( a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x) \). Substituting the exponential forms:

\[ a \cos(t \log x) - b \sin(t \log x) = a \left( \frac{x^{it} + x^{-it}}{2} \right) - b \left( \frac{x^{it} - x^{-it}}{2i} \right) \]

To combine these, we need a common denominator. Rewrite \( \sin(t \log x) \) noting that \( \frac{1}{2i} = -\frac{i}{2} \), but let's compute directly:

\[ = \frac{a (x^{it} + x^{-it})}{2} - \frac{b (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{2i} \]

Multiply the second term by \( \frac{i}{i} \) to simplify:

\[ -\frac{b (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{2i} = -\frac{b (x^{it} - x^{-it}) i}{2i \cdot i} = \frac{b i (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{2} \]

So:

\[ = \frac{a (x^{it} + x^{-it}) + b i (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{2} \]

Distribute and combine like terms:

\[ = \frac{a x^{it} + a x^{-it} + b i x^{it} - b i x^{-it}}{2} = \frac{(a + b i) x^{it} + (a - b i) x^{-it}}{2} \]

Thus, \( f(x, s) \) becomes:

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + b i) x^{it} + (a - b i) x^{-it} \right] \]

Applying to \( g(x, s) \)

For \( g(x, s) \), the oscillatory part is \( a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x) \):

\[ a \sin(t \log x) + b \cos(t \log x) = a \left( \frac{x^{it} - x^{-it}}{2i} \right) + b \left( \frac{x^{it} + x^{-it}}{2} \right) \]

Adjust the first term:

\[ \frac{x^{it} - x^{-it}}{2i} = \frac{x^{it} - x^{-it}}{2i} \cdot \frac{i}{i} = \frac{i (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{2i^2} = \frac{i (x^{it} - x^{-it})}{-2} = \frac{-i x^{it} + i x^{-it}}{2} \]

So:

\[ = a \left( \frac{-i x^{it} + i x^{-it}}{2} \right) + b \left( \frac{x^{it} + x^{-it}}{2} \right) = \frac{-a i x^{it} + a i x^{-it} + b x^{it} + b x^{-it}}{2} \]

Combine:

\[ = \frac{(b - a i) x^{it} + (b + a i) x^{-it}}{2} \]

Thus:

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \left[ (b - a i) x^{it} + (b + a i) x^{-it} \right] \]

Section 2: Simplifying the Functions

Using properties of exponents, we can further simplify these expressions. For \( f(x, s) \):

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{x^{\sigma - 1}}{e^x - 1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + b i) x^{it} + (a - b i) x^{-it} \right] \]

Since \( x^{\sigma - 1} x^{it} = x^{(\sigma - 1) + it} = x^{s - 1} \) and \( x^{\sigma - 1} x^{-it} = x^{(\sigma - 1) - it} = x^{\overline{s} - 1} \) (where \( \overline{s} = \sigma - it \)):

\[ f(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (a + b i) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (a - b i) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \]

For \( g(x, s) \):

\[ g(x, s) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ (b - a i) \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} + (b + a i) \frac{x^{\overline{s} - 1}}{e^x - 1} \right] \]

This form highlights the symmetry between \( s \) and its conjugate \( \overline{s} \).

Section 3: Discussing the Nature of the Functions

Oscillatory Behavior

The terms \( x^{it} \) and \( x^{-it} \) are complex exponentials with magnitude 1, since \( |x^{it}| = |e^{i t \log x}| = 1 \). Their phases depend on \( t \log x \), causing oscillations that increase in frequency as \( x \) grows, mirroring patterns in the Riemann zeta function.

Connection to the Riemann Zeta Function

The expression \( \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \) resembles the integrand of the zeta function’s integral representation:

\[ \Gamma(s) \zeta(s) = \int_0^\infty \frac{x^{s - 1}}{e^x - 1} \, dx \quad (\text{Re}(s) > 1) \]

Integrating \( f(x, s) \) or \( g(x, s) \) over \( x \) from 0 to \( \infty \) would produce linear combinations of \( \zeta(s) \) and \( \zeta(\overline{s}) \), linking them directly to the zeta function.

Real and Imaginary Components

In \( \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s} \), we have \( n^{-s} = n^{-\sigma} e^{-i t \log n} \), and the real and imaginary parts involve \( \cos(t \log n) \) and \( \sin(t \log n) \). Similarly, \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \) act as continuous analogs, with coefficients \( a \) and \( b \) modulating their real and imaginary contributions.

Conclusion

By converting sine and cosine to exponential forms, we’ve uncovered the oscillatory structure of \( f(x, s) \) and \( g(x, s) \), revealing their deep connection to the Riemann zeta function. This transformation and analysis enhance our understanding of complex functions and their mathematical significance.
