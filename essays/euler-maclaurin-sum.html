1. Introduction to the Euler-Maclaurin Sum Formula

The Euler-Maclaurin Sum Formula is a remarkable tool in mathematics that connects discrete sums to continuous integrals. Named after Leonhard Euler and Colin Maclaurin, this formula provides a way to approximate a sum \( \sum_{n=a}^{b} f(n) \) with an integral, plus a series of correction terms that account for the difference. It’s widely used in number theory, numerical analysis, and the study of special functions like the Riemann zeta function. This eBook explores its formulation, significance, and applications.

2. Derivation of the Formula

The formula arises from the idea of approximating a sum by an integral and correcting the error using Taylor series and Bernoulli numbers. Consider a sum \( S = \sum_{n=a}^{b} f(n) \). The integral \( \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \) gives a continuous approximation, but misses the discrete nature of the sum. To refine this, we use the trapezoidal rule and expand the error terms.

Start with the difference between \( f(n) \) and the integral over \([n, n+1]\):

\[ f(n) = \int_n^{n+1} f(n) \, dx \]

Using a Taylor expansion around \( x = n \), and integrating term by term with Bernoulli polynomials, we derive:

\[ \sum_{n=a}^{b} f(n) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx + \frac{f(a) + f(b)}{2} + \sum_{k=1}^{p} \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!} \left[ f^{(2k-1)}(b) - f^{(2k-1)}(a) \right] + R_p \]

3. Components of the Formula

The Euler-Maclaurin formula consists of several key parts:

  • Integral Term: \( \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \), the continuous approximation of the sum.
  • Endpoint Correction: \( \frac{f(a) + f(b)}{2} \), adjusting for boundary values (trapezoidal rule).
  • Correction Series: \( \sum_{k=1}^{p} \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!} [f^{(2k-1)}(b) - f^{(2k-1)}(a)] \), where \( B_{2k} \) are Bernoulli numbers (e.g., \( B_2 = \frac{1}{6} \), \( B_4 = -\frac{1}{30} \)), and \( f^{(2k-1)} \) is the \((2k-1)\)-th derivative.
  • Remainder: \( R_p = -\frac{1}{(2p)!} \int_a^b B_{2p}(x - \lfloor x \rfloor) f^{(2p)}(x) \, dx \), capturing the residual error.

These terms refine the sum by accounting for the function’s behavior at and between integers.

4. Example: Harmonic Numbers

Consider the harmonic number \( H_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{1}{n} \) with \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \):

  • Integral: \( \int_1^N \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln N \)
  • Endpoint: \( \frac{f(1) + f(N)}{2} = \frac{1 + \frac{1}{N}}{2} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2N} \)
  • First Correction (\( k = 1 \)): \( f'(x) = -\frac{1}{x^2} \), \( \frac{B_2}{2!} [f'(N) - f'(1)] = \frac{1}{12} (1 - \frac{1}{N^2}) \)

Thus: \( H_N \approx \ln N + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{12} (1 - \frac{1}{N^2}) + R_1 \), approaching \( \ln N + \gamma \) as \( N \to \infty \), where \( \gamma \approx 0.577 \) is the Euler-Mascheroni constant.

5. Connection to the Riemann Zeta Function

The formula is crucial for the analytic continuation of \( \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} \). Apply it to \( f(x) = x^{-s} \), \( a = 1 \), \( b = \infty \):

\[ \zeta(s) = \int_1^\infty x^{-s} \, dx + \frac{1}{2} + \sum_{k=1}^p \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!} [0 - f^{(2k-1)}(1)] + R_p \]

With \( \int_1^\infty x^{-s} \, dx = \frac{1}{s-1} \), and derivatives adjusting the domain, this extends \( \zeta(s) \) beyond \( \sigma > 1 \), linking sums to integrals.
