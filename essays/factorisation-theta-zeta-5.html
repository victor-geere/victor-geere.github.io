Analysis of the Divisor Function \( f(x) \)

We explore the function \( f(x) = -2 \left[ \sin^2(\pi x) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{x} \right) \right] \), where \( k \) is a positive integer, with roots at the positive divisors of \( k \). This analysis connects \( f(x) \) to advanced mathematical frameworks, providing clarity and depth.

1. Introduction

The function \( f(x) = -2 \left[ \sin^2(\pi x) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{x} \right) \right] \) has roots at \( x = d \), where \( d \) divides \( k \), due to \( \sin(\pi d) = 0 \) and \( \sin\left( \pi \frac{k}{d} \right) = 0 \) when \( d \) and \( \frac{k}{d} \) are integers. Its symmetry, \( f(x) = f(k/x) \), and oscillatory nature make it a rich subject for connecting to number theory and analysis.

2. Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function is \( \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \) for \( \Re(s) > 1 \), extended analytically with a pole at \( s = 1 \). We explore its connections to \( f(x) \).

Series Form

The divisor function \( d(k) = \sum_{d \mid k} 1 \) relates to \( \zeta(s)^2 = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{d(k)}{k^s} \), but since \( f(d) = 0 \) at divisors, direct sums are trivial. Instead, transforms may reveal deeper links.

Integral Form

Zeta’s integral representations, like \( \zeta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^{\infty} \frac{x^{s-1}}{e^x - 1} \, dx \), involve oscillatory terms in complex \( s \). The sine in \( f(x) \) suggests a potential integral relation.

Functional Equation

The zeta functional equation, \( \zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left( \frac{\pi s}{2} \right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s) \), mirrors the symmetry \( f(x) = f(k/x) \), hinting at a duality akin to \( s \to 1-s \).

Prime-Related Oscillations

The explicit formula ties prime distribution to zeta zeros via oscillatory terms. The sine oscillations in \( f(x) \) may analogously reflect divisor distribution tied to \( k \)’s prime factors.

Hurwitz Zeta

The Hurwitz zeta function, \( \zeta(s, a) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(n+a)^s} \), has real and imaginary parts with \( \cos(t \log n) \) and \( \sin(t \log n) \) for \( s = \sigma + it \). While \( f(x) \) uses periodic sine, a logarithmic transform might align these.

3. Sinc Function and Sine Kernel

The sinc function is \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \), prominent in signal processing and random matrix theory’s sine kernel.

Near a divisor \( d \), let \( x = d + \epsilon \). Then, \( f(x) \approx -2 \pi^2 \epsilon^2 \left( 1 + \left( \frac{k}{d^2} \right)^2 \right) \), a quadratic behavior unlike sinc’s linear decay. However, Fourier analysis of \( f(x) \)’s periodicity may yield sinc-like kernels.

4. Mellin Transform

The Mellin transform is \( \mathcal{M}\{g\}(s) = \int_0^{\infty} x^{s-1} g(x) \, dx \). For \( f(x) = -2 + \cos(2\pi x) + \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) \), direct computation is tricky due to oscillation, but symmetry helps:

\[ \mathcal{M}\{f\}(s) = k^s \mathcal{M}\{f\}(2 - s) \]

This functional equation suggests \( f(x) \) encodes divisor properties, possibly linking to zeta-like series via residues.

5. Euler-Maclaurin Sum Formula

For \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f(n) \), where \( f(n) = -2 \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{n} \right) \), the sum is zero at divisors and converges as \( \approx -2 \pi^2 k^2 \zeta(2) \) with divisor terms excluded. The formula approximates this via integrals and derivatives, connecting to \( \zeta(2) \).

6. p-adic Analysis

Defining \( f(x) \) p-adically is challenging since \( \pi \) is real, but the exponential \( e^{ix} \) exists p-adically for small \( x \). Zeros at divisors suggest a p-adic interpolation, potentially tying to p-adic zeta functions, though this is non-standard.

7. Dirichlet Series

Construct \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{f(n)}{n^s} = -\zeta(s) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right)}{n^s} \). The cosine series may relate to arithmetic functions, but \( \sin(x \log n) \) requires a transform to appear, possibly via Fourier methods.

8. Convergent Infinite Series

Using \( f(x) = -2 + \cos(2\pi x) + \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) \), a Fourier expansion or regularization (e.g., damping) could yield a convergent series, reflecting divisor zeros indirectly.

9. Poisson Summation and Theta Function

Poisson summation requires extending \( f(x) \) (e.g., \( f(|x|) \) or zero for \( x \leq 0 \)), but symmetry \( f(x) = f(k/x) \) suggests a Fourier transform relation. Theta functions, linked to divisors, may connect via this symmetry after transformation.

10. Weierstrass Factorization

Since \( f(x) \) isn’t entire (oscillates at infinity), standard factorization doesn’t apply. A trigonometric form leveraging sine zeros at divisors is possible but complex due to the sum structure.
