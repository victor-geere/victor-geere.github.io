1. Introduction

This ebook delves into the sine version of the divisor function, defined as \( f(x) = -2 \left[ \sin^2(\pi x) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{x} \right) \right] \), where \( k \) is a positive integer. This function is remarkable for having roots at the positive divisors of \( k \) when \( x > 1 \), making it a number-theoretic object of interest. Our goal is to explore its relationships with advanced mathematical concepts, including the Mellin transform, Riemann zeta function, sinc function, Euler-Maclaurin formula, parametric zeta functions, p-adic analysis, Dirichlet series, infinite series convergence, Poisson summation, theta functions, integral representations of the zeta function, and Weierstrass's contributions. We will hypothesize where appropriate, clearly marking such leaps, and provide a comprehensive analysis suitable for readers comfortable with complex topics.

2. The Sine Version of the Divisor Function

Consider the function: \[ f(x) = -2 \left[ \sin^2(\pi x) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{x} \right) \right], \] where \( k \) is a fixed positive integer and typically \( x > 1 \). This function is "sine-based" and exhibits symmetry: \( f(x) = f(k/x) \). Its key property is that \( f(x) = 0 \) when \( x \) is a positive divisor of \( k \). For integer \( n > 1 \): \[ f(n) = -2 \left[ \sin^2(\pi n) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{n} \right) \right]. \] Since \( \sin(\pi n) = 0 \) for integer \( n \), this simplifies to: \[ f(n) = -2 \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{n} \right). \] Using the identity \( \sin^2(\theta) = \frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2} \): \[ f(n) = -2 \left( \frac{1 - \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right)}{2} \right) = -1 + \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right). \] When \( n \) divides \( k \), \( \frac{k}{n} \) is an integer, so \( \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right) = 1 \), and \( f(n) = 0 \). Otherwise, \( f(n) < 0 \), oscillating between \(-2\) and \(0\).

3. Mellin Transform and Its Application

The Mellin transform of \( f(x) \) is: \[ F(s) = \int_0^\infty f(x) x^{s-1} \, dx. \] Since \( f(x) \) oscillates and is bounded (\(|f(x)| \leq 4\)), the integral may not converge over \( (0, \infty) \). Given the context \( x > 1 \), we consider: \[ F(s) = \int_1^\infty f(x) x^{-s} \, dx, \] for \( \Re(s) > 1 \), where the negative exponent ensures decay. Substituting \( f(x) \): \[ F(s) = -2 \int_1^\infty \left[ \sin^2(\pi x) + \sin^2\left( \pi \frac{k}{x} \right) \right] x^{-s} \, dx. \] Using \( \sin^2(\theta) = \frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2} \): \[ F(s) = - \int_1^\infty \left[ 2 - \cos(2\pi x) - \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) \right] x^{-s} \, dx. \] This splits into: \[ F(s) = -2 \int_1^\infty x^{-s} \, dx + \int_1^\infty \cos(2\pi x) x^{-s} \, dx + \int_1^\infty \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{x}\right) x^{-s} \, dx. \] The first term is: \[ -2 \int_1^\infty x^{-s} \, dx = -2 \left[ \frac{x^{-s+1}}{-s+1} \right]_1^\infty = \frac{2}{s-1}, \] for \( \Re(s) > 1 \). The cosine integrals are more complex, often requiring special functions or numerical methods, and do not directly yield the Riemann zeta function.

4. Connection to the Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function is: \[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s}, \quad \Re(s) > 1. \] Consider the Dirichlet series: \[ \sum_{n=2}^\infty f(n) n^{-s} = \sum_{n=2}^\infty \left[ \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right) - 1 \right] n^{-s}. \] Since \( f(n) = 0 \) when \( n \) divides \( k \), the sum excludes divisors. For large \( n \), \( \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right) \approx 1 - \frac{(2\pi k / n)^2}{2} \), so: \[ f(n) \approx -\frac{(2\pi k)^2}{2 n^2}, \] suggesting: \[ \sum_{n=2}^\infty f(n) n^{-s} \approx -\frac{(2\pi k)^2}{2} \sum_{n=2}^\infty n^{-s-2}. \] Since \( \sum_{n=2}^\infty n^{-s-2} = \zeta(s+2) - 1 \), this approximates a shifted zeta function, but the exact sum involves oscillatory terms not directly reducible to \( \zeta(s) \).

5. Exploration of the Sinc Function

The sinc function, \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \), differs from \( f(x) \), which uses \( \sin^2(\pi x) \). While both involve sine, the squared term and lack of denominator in \( f(x) \) limit direct connections. The sinc function’s integral properties do not readily apply here.

6. Euler-Maclaurin Sum Formula

The Euler-Maclaurin formula approximates sums via integrals: \[ \sum_{n=a}^b g(n) \approx \int_a^b g(x) \, dx + \frac{g(a) + g(b)}{2} + \text{correction terms}. \] For \( \sum_{n=2}^\infty f(n) e^{-t n} \), it could estimate behavior, but \( f(x) \)’s oscillations complicate higher-order terms, and no specific zeta connection emerges without a target sum.

7. Parametric Versions of the Riemann Zeta Function

The Hurwitz zeta function, \( \zeta(s, a) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(n+a)^s} \), generalizes \( \zeta(s) \). If \( f(n) \) could parameterize shifts, a link might exist, but its trigonometric form resists this framework.

8. p-adic Conversion and p-adic Zeta Function

In p-adic analysis, numbers are evaluated via their p-adic valuations. The p-adic zeta function interpolates \( \zeta(s) \) in this topology. Since \( f(x) \) zeros at divisors, a p-adic interpretation might count divisors modulo \( p \), but this requires advanced reformulation beyond our scope.

9. Dirichlet Series

A Dirichlet series is: \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n^s}. \] With \( a_n = f(n) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{k}{n}\right) - 1 \) for \( n \geq 2 \), \( a_1 \) undefined (assume \( f(1) = 0 \) if \( k=1 \)), we get a series distinct from \( \zeta(s)^2 = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\tau(n)}{n^s} \) (where \( \tau(n) \) is the divisor count), lacking a standard form.

10. Infinite Series Convergence

The series \( \sum_{n=2}^\infty f(n) n^{-s} \) converges for \( \Re(s) > 1 \), as \( |f(n)| \leq 2 \). To ensure convergence otherwise, consider: \[ \sum_{n=2}^\infty r^n f(n), \] for \( 0 < r < 1 \). As \( r \to 1^- \), it may reflect divisor properties, though not directly zeta-related.

11. Poisson Summation and Theta Function

Poisson summation states: \[ \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty g(n) = \sum_{m=-\infty}^\infty \hat{g}(m), \] where \( \hat{g}(m) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x) e^{-i 2\pi m x} \, dx \). For \( g(x) = f(x) \cdot \mathbf{1}_{x > 1} \), the transform is complex due to the cutoff, and theta functions (\( \theta(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi n^2 t} \)) do not directly align with \( f(x) \)’s form.

12. Integral Versions of the Riemann Zeta Function

An integral form is: \[ \zeta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t - 1} \, dt. \] Linking \( f(x) \) to this kernel is challenging, though custom integrals with \( f(x) \) might be devised. Another form: \[ \pi^{-s/2} \Gamma\left( \frac{s}{2} \right) \zeta(s) = \int_0^\infty t^{s/2 - 1} (\theta(it) - 1) \, dt, \] remains distant from \( f(x) \).

13. Weierstrass and Other Advanced Topics

Weierstrass’s factorization might express \( f(x) \) as a product over its zeros, but it’s not entire. Hypothesis: \( f(x) \) could relate to modular forms via its symmetry, though this requires transforming \( x \to \tau \) in the upper half-plane, an uncharted leap.

14. Glossary of Terms

  • Mellin Transform: \( \int_0^\infty f(x) x^{s-1} \, dx \), transforms functions into the complex plane.
  • Riemann Zeta Function: \( \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} \), key in number theory.
  • Sinc Function: \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \), used in signal processing.
  • Euler-Maclaurin Sum Formula: Approximates sums with integrals plus corrections.
  • Dirichlet Series: \( \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n^s} \), generalizes zeta functions.
  • Poisson Summation: Relates sums to Fourier transforms.
  • Theta Function: \( \theta(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi n^2 t} \), appears in number theory.
  • p-adic Numbers: Numbers analyzed via prime-based valuations.
