Rigour

Rigour refers to a process of adhering absolutely to certain constraints. It is the practice of maintaining strict consistency with certain predefined parameters. The mathematical definitions of conjecture, proof and theorem is used i.e. a conjecture withstood the scrutiny of existing knowledge, a proof is unfalsifiable, a theorem has been proven, and a law defines the boundaries and is beyond scrutiny. Additionally, a truth is an abstraction that has remained standing in the face of the aforementioned. The application of a theorem or a truth is itself subject to rigour.

Intellectual Rigour

Intellectual rigour is a process of thought which is consistent, does not contain self-contradiction, and takes into account the entire scope of available knowledge on the topic. It actively avoids logical fallacy. Furthermore, it requires a sceptical assessment of the available knowledge. If a topic or case is dealt with in a rigorous way, it typically means that it is dealt with in a comprehensive, thorough and complete way, leaving no room for inconsistencies.

Mathematical Proof

A mathematical proof is an inferential argument for a mathematical statement, showing that the stated assumptions logically guarantee the conclusion. The argument may use other previously established statements, such as theorems; but every proof can, in principle, be constructed using only certain basic or original assumptions known as axioms, along with the accepted rules of inference. Proofs are examples of exhaustive deductive reasoning which establish logical certainty, to be distinguished from empirical arguments or non-exhaustive inductive reasoning which establish "reasonable expectation".

Practical Considerations

Where it is impossible to consider all the possible values of all the variables that are or might become relevant in future it is useful to consider the variables at their limits. Limits are not necessarily encountered at the extremities as periodic functions might encounter their limits at regular intervals. Timelines might never reach their extremities as an infinite timeline is not a practical consideration. The application of practical considerations should in itself be subjected to rigour. Where practical considerations limit the provability, the application of the theorem should be limited by the same measure.

Scrutinizing and defending a conjecture

A criticism does not oppose a truth but is itself scrutinised separate to the conjecture. If it is a truth itself it considered as part of the conjecture under the maxim of Total Evidence. The conjecture is then scrutinised again with the new knowledge included. If the original conjecture remains unchanged for all values of the criticism, the criticism may be archived but noted. If additional criticism is considered at a later staged, archived criticism should be considered along with it in case the two criticisms interact with one another.
