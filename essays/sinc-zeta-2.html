Relationships in Mathematics: Sinc, Sine Kernel, RMT, and Zeta

The Question

What is the relationship between the sinc function, sine kernel, random matrix theory, and the Riemann zeta function?

The Answer

The Sinc Function

The sinc function is a well-known mathematical function defined as:

  • \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{x} \) for \( x \neq 0 \),
  • \( \text{sinc}(0) = 1 \) (by taking the limit as \( x \to 0 \)).

This function is fundamental in areas like signal processing and Fourier analysis due to its role in representing ideal low-pass filters and its appearance in convolution operations.

The Sine Kernel

The sine kernel is closely related to the sinc function. It typically appears in the form:

  • \( K(x, y) = \frac{\sin(x - y)}{x - y} \).

If we define \( z = x - y \), this becomes:

  • \( K(x, y) = \text{sinc}(z) = \text{sinc}(x - y) \).

Thus, the sine kernel is essentially the sinc function applied to the difference between two variables. It quantifies similarity or correlation between points \( x \) and \( y \) and arises naturally in contexts involving oscillatory phenomena.

Random Matrix Theory and the Sine Kernel

Random matrix theory (RMT) studies matrices with random entries, focusing on properties like the distribution of their eigenvalues. A key example is the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE), a set of Hermitian matrices with complex Gaussian entries. As the size of these matrices grows large, the eigenvalues exhibit specific statistical behaviors.

One striking result in RMT is that the two-point correlation function of the eigenvalues in the GUE is governed by the sine kernel. After appropriate scaling (e.g., unfolding the spectrum to normalize the mean eigenvalue spacing to 1), the probability density of finding eigenvalues at positions \( x \) and \( y \) includes the term:

\[ \frac{\sin(\pi (x - y))}{\pi (x - y)} \],

which is a scaled version of the sine kernel (or sinc function). This indicates that the sine kernel describes the repulsion between eigenvalues: they tend not to cluster too closely, and their spacing follows a pattern dictated by this oscillatory function.

The Riemann Zeta Function and Its Zeros

The Riemann zeta function is defined as:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} \] for complex \( s \) with \( \text{Re}(s) > 1 \),

and it extends to the entire complex plane (except for a simple pole at \( s = 1 \)) via analytic continuation. The non-trivial zeros of \( \zeta(s) \), conjectured to lie on the critical line \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \) (the Riemann Hypothesis), are central to number theory, as their distribution relates to the primes.

Connecting the Zeta Function to Random Matrix Theory

A profound link emerges when examining the spacings between the non-trivial zeros of the zeta function along the critical line. Numerical computations and theoretical insights reveal that these spacings statistically resemble the eigenvalue spacings of large GUE matrices. Specifically:

  • The distribution of normalized spacings between consecutive zeta zeros matches the distribution predicted by the GUE, where the sine kernel governs the correlations.

This observation supports the Hilbert-Pólya conjecture, which posits that the zeros of \( \zeta(s) \) might be the eigenvalues of some Hermitian operator—a concept aligned with RMT, where Hermitian matrices like those in the GUE have real eigenvalues.

Tying It All Together

The relationships between these concepts can be summarized as follows:

  1. Sinc Function and Sine Kernel: The sinc function, \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{x} \), directly relates to the sine kernel, \( \frac{\sin(x - y)}{x - y} = \text{sinc}(x - y) \), differing only by the argument.
  2. Sine Kernel in Random Matrix Theory: In the GUE, the sine kernel describes the two-point correlation of eigenvalues, reflecting their statistical repulsion and spacing distribution.
  3. Random Matrix Theory and the Riemann Zeta Function: The spacings of the zeta function’s non-trivial zeros along the critical line exhibit the same statistical properties as GUE eigenvalue spacings, with the sine kernel playing a key role in both.

Thus, the sinc function, through its manifestation as the sine kernel, bridges signal processing and RMT, while RMT provides a framework to understand the mysterious distribution of the Riemann zeta function’s zeros. This interplay illustrates a remarkable convergence of pure mathematics, statistical physics, and number theory.
