1. Introduction

This ebook delves into the analysis of a trigonometric equation whose roots are tied to the divisors of a given integer \( k \). We explore its mathematical structure, manipulate it to study convergence, and investigate its connections to fundamental number-theoretic constructs such as the Riemann zeta function, theta function, Dirichlet series, and the Mellin transform. Each section offers a detailed explanation, supported by mathematical rigor, and where direct connections are not fully established, we propose hypotheses and open questions. This work is designed for readers with a solid foundation in analysis and number theory, aiming to bridge trigonometric properties with arithmetic functions.

2. The Trigonometric Equation and Its Roots

Consider the following trigonometric equation:

\[ \frac{1 - \cos(2 \pi x)}{x} - \frac{\cos(2 \pi k / x) - 1}{z} = 0, \quad x > 1 \]

It is claimed that the roots of this equation correspond to the divisors of \( k \) greater than 1. However, the variable \( z \) introduces ambiguity. To test this, let \( x = d \), where \( d \) is a divisor of \( k \). Since \( d \) is an integer, \( \cos(2 \pi d) = 1 \), so the left term becomes:

\[ \frac{1 - 1}{d} = 0 \]

Similarly, \( k / d \) is an integer, so \( \cos(2 \pi k / d) = 1 \), and the right term is:

\[ \frac{1 - 1}{z} = 0 \]

The equation holds trivially for any \( z \neq 0 \), suggesting a possible error in the original form. Assuming \( z = x \) corrects this, yielding:

\[ \frac{1 - \cos(2 \pi x)}{x} - \frac{\cos(2 \pi k / x) - 1}{x} = 0 \]

Multiply through by \( x \) (since \( x > 1 \)):

\[ 1 - \cos(2 \pi x) = \cos(2 \pi k / x) - 1 \]

Rearrange:

\[ \cos(2 \pi x) + \cos(2 \pi k / x) - 2 = 0 \]

Define the function:

\[ f(x) = \cos(2 \pi x) + \cos(2 \pi k / x) - 2 \]

For \( x = d \), a divisor of \( k \), \( 2 \pi d \) and \( 2 \pi k / d \) are integer multiples of \( 2 \pi \), so:

\[ f(d) = \cos(2 \pi d) + \cos(2 \pi k / d) - 2 = 1 + 1 - 2 = 0 \]

For \( k = 6 \), divisors are 1, 2, 3, 6. Since \( x > 1 \), roots are at \( x = 2, 3, 6 \), which satisfy \( f(x) = 0 \), confirming the claim.

3. Ensuring Convergence of the Function

The function \( f(x) \) ranges from \(-2\) to 0, with zeros at divisors of \( k \). Summing over integers, \( \sum_{n=2}^\infty f(n) \), diverges because \( f(n) \) decays slowly (e.g., \( f(n) \approx -2 (\pi k / n)^2 \) for large \( n \)). To achieve convergence, introduce a damping factor \( r^x \), where \( 0 < r < 1 \):

\[ f_r(x) = r^x f(x) \]

As \( x \to \infty \), \( r^x \to 0 \) exponentially, ensuring convergence of:

\[ S(r) = \sum_{n=2}^\infty r^n f(n) \]

At divisors \( d \), \( f(d) = 0 \), so \( f_r(d) = 0 \), preserving the roots. For large \( n \), \( f(n) \approx -2 (\pi k / n)^2 \), so:

\[ S(r) \approx -2 \pi^2 k^2 \sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{r^n}{n^2} \]

This resembles the polylogarithm \( \text{Li}_2(r) \), convergent for \( |r| < 1 \). As \( r \to 1^- \), \( S(r) \) may reflect divisor properties.

4. Link to the Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function is:

\[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s}, \quad \Re(s) > 1 \]

Define a zeta-like series for \( f(x) \):

\[ Z(s) = \sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{f(n)}{n^s} \]

For large \( n \), \( f(n) \sim -2 (\pi k / n)^2 \), so \( Z(s) \) converges for \( \Re(s) > -1 \). Since \( f(n) = 0 \) at divisors, the sum excludes their contributions. Approximate:

\[ Z(s) \approx -2 \pi^2 k^2 \sum_{n \neq d | k} \frac{1}{n^{s+2}} \]

This suggests:

\[ Z(s) + 2 \pi^2 k^2 \zeta(s + 2) \approx \text{a modified sum} \]

While not exact, this hints at \( f(x) \) modulating zeta sums by nullifying divisor terms.

5. Relevance of the Theta Function

The theta function is:

\[ \theta(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi n^2 t}, \quad t > 0 \]

Propose a related sum:

\[ \theta_f(t) = \sum_{n=2}^\infty e^{-t n} f(n) \]

With \( f(n) = 0 \) at divisors, \( \theta_f(t) \) skips these terms, creating a modified theta function tied to \( k \)’s divisor structure. It lacks \( \theta(t) \)’s symmetry but may reveal divisor distribution patterns.

6. Dirichlet Series in Number Theory

The divisor function’s Dirichlet series is:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{d(n)}{n^s} = \zeta(s)^2 \]

For divisors of \( k \) greater than 1:

\[ D_k(s) = \sum_{\substack{d | k \\ d > 1}} d^{-s} \]

Since \( f(x) = 0 \) at divisors, \( 1 / f(x) \) has poles there, but its double zeros complicate direct use. Instead, \( S(r) \) as \( r \to 1^- \) may approximate divisor-related sums after adjustment.

7. The Mellin Transform Application

The Mellin transform is:

\[ \mathcal{M}\{g(t)\}(s) = \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} g(t) \, dt \]

For \( f(t) \) with \( t > 1 \):

\[ F(s) = \int_1^\infty t^{s-1} f(t) \, dt \]

Split as:

\[ F(s) = \int_1^\infty t^{s-1} \cos(2 \pi t) \, dt + \int_1^\infty t^{s-1} \cos\left(2 \pi \frac{k}{t}\right) \, dt - 2 \int_1^\infty t^{s-1} \, dt \]

The last term is \( -\frac{1}{s} \) for \( \Re(s) < 0 \). The oscillatory integrals need regularization, but \( F(s) \) may encode divisor information via analytic properties.

8. Number-Theoretic Properties

Note \( f(x) = f(k / x) \), reflecting divisor pairs \( (d, k/d) \). Near \( x = d \), \( f(x) \approx -2 \pi^2 (1 + k^2 / d^4) (x - d)^2 \), a double zero. This symmetry and behavior suggest applications in divisor sum identities.

9. Hypotheses and Open Problems

  • Hypothesis 1: \( F(s) \) or \( Z(s) \) encodes \( D_k(s) \) via analytic continuation.
  • Hypothesis 2: \( \theta_f(t) \) has modular properties tied to \( k \).
  • Open Question: Can \( f(x) \) generate \( \sigma(k) \) directly?
