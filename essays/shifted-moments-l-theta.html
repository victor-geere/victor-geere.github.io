Introduction to Shifted Moments and Theta Functions

Shifted moments of L functions and moments of theta functions are advanced topics in number theory, exploring how these mathematical objects behave under specific conditions. L functions, like the Riemann zeta function, are crucial for understanding prime number distributions, while theta functions relate to quadratic forms and modular forms. This section simplifies their shifted moments and theta function moments for a general audience.

Shifted Moments of L Functions

Shifted moments look at how L functions, particularly Dirichlet L functions, behave when evaluated at points slightly offset from the critical line (where the real part of the complex variable is 1/2). Imagine summing up products of these functions over different characters (mathematical functions tied to number patterns) with small shifts. Research suggests this helps study how these functions correlate at different points, which is vital for understanding their distribution and zeros.

Under the GRH, a hypothesis about where the zeros of L functions lie, there are upper bounds for these shifted moments. For example, the sum is bounded by terms involving the Euler totient function and logarithms, with additional factors depending on the shifts, indicating how close or far apart the evaluation points are.

Moments of Theta Functions

Moments of theta functions involve averaging the \(2k\)-th power of theta functions, which are special sums associated with Dirichlet characters, evaluated at specific points like \(x=1\). For instance, for even characters, it's a sum over characters excluding the principal one, of the absolute value of theta at 1 raised to \(2k\). Conjectures suggest their behavior as the modulus \(q\) grows, like scaling with \(q\) and logarithms, but these are still under active research.

Connection Between the Two

The evidence leans toward using shifted moments of L functions to derive bounds for theta function moments, often through expressing theta functions in terms of L functions using transforms like Mellin. This connection is complex and relies on GRH, highlighting the interplay between these areas in number theory.

Background and Context

Number theory, a branch of pure mathematics, deals with properties of integers and related structures. L functions, generalizing the Riemann zeta function, are central to studying prime distributions, particularly through Dirichlet L functions defined as:

\[ L(s, \chi) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\chi(n)}{n^s} \]

for a Dirichlet character \(\chi\) modulo \(q\), initially convergent for Re(\(s\)) > 1 and extended by analytic continuation. Theta functions, historically linked to elliptic functions and modular forms, are special functions with quasiperiodic properties, often expressed as sums like the Jacobi theta function:

\[ \vartheta(z; \tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \exp(\pi i n^2 \tau + 2\pi i n z) \]

where \(z\) and \(\tau\) (with \(\tau\) in the upper half-plane) are complex variables. In number theory, theta functions associated with Dirichlet characters take the form:

\[ \theta(x, \chi) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \chi(n) n^{\eta} e^{-\pi n x / q} \]

where \(\eta = 0\) for even characters (\(\chi(-1) = 1\)) and \(\eta = 1\) for odd characters (\(\chi(-1) = -1\)), reflecting their parity.

Shifted Moments of L Functions: Definition and Significance

Shifted moments of L functions involve expressions summing over primitive characters \(\chi \in X_q^*\) (modulo \(q\)) of products of L functions evaluated at shifted points, typically:

\[ \sum_{\chi \in X_q^*} \left| L\left(\frac{1}{2} + it_1, \chi\right) \cdots L\left(\frac{1}{2} + it_{2k}, \chi\right) \right| \]

where \(t_1, \dots, t_{2k}\) are small real numbers, often \(t_i \ll \log q\). This formulation generalizes standard moments by incorporating shifts, aiming to capture correlations between L function values at different points on the critical line (Re(\(s\)) = 1/2). These correlations are crucial for understanding the distribution of L function zeros and values, with applications in random matrix theory modeling.

Under the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis (GRH), which posits all non-trivial zeros of L functions have real part 1/2, research has established upper bounds. For instance, a key result from Munsch and Shparlinski (2015) states, for large \(q\) and \(t_i \ll \log q\):

\[ \sum_{\chi \in X_q^*} \left| L\left(\frac{1}{2} + it_1, \chi\right) \cdots L\left(\frac{1}{2} + it_{2k}, \chi\right) \right| \ll \phi(q) (\log q)^{k/2 + \epsilon} \prod_{iwhere \(\phi(q)\) is the Euler totient function, \(\epsilon > 0\) is arbitrary, and \(E_{i,j}\) depends on \(|t_i - t_j|\):

  • If \(|t_i - t_j| \leq \frac{1}{100}\), \(E_{i,j} = \min\left(\frac{1}{|t_i - t_j|^{1/2}}, \log q\right)\).
  • If \(|t_i - t_j| \geq \frac{1}{100}\), \(E_{i,j} = \sqrt{\log \log q}\).

This bound reflects the correlation structure: L functions are "essentially correlated" when \(|t_i - t_j| \approx \frac{1}{2 \log q}\) and "independent" for larger differences.

Moments of Theta Functions: Definition and Conjectures

Moments of theta functions involve summing powers of theta functions evaluated at specific points, typically at the central point \(x=1\). For instance, for even primitive characters (excluding the principal character \(\chi_0\)):

\[ S_{2k}^+(q) = \sum_{\chi \in X_q^+ \setminus \chi_0} \left| \theta(1, \chi) \right|^{2k} \]

and for odd characters:

\[ S_{2k}^-(q) = \sum_{\chi \in X_q^-} \left| \theta(1, \chi) \right|^{2k} \]

where \(X_q^+\) and \(X_q^-\) are sets of primitive even and odd characters, respectively. Here, \(\theta(1, \chi)\) is derived from \(\theta(x, \chi) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \chi(n) n^{\eta} e^{-\pi n x / q}\), evaluated at \(x=1\), with \(\eta\) depending on character parity.

Research provides upper bounds under GRH:

  • For even characters: \( S_{2k}^+(q) \ll \phi(q) q^{k/2 (k-1) + \epsilon} (\log q)^{2(k-1) + \epsilon} \)
  • For odd characters: \( S_{2k}^-(q) \ll \phi(q) q^{3k/2 (k-1) + \epsilon} (\log q)^{2(k-1) + \epsilon} \)

Conjectures propose asymptotic formulas:

  • \( S_{2k}^+(q) \sim a_k \phi(q) q^{k/2} (\log q)^{2(k-1)} \)
  • \( S_{2k}^-(q) \sim b_k \phi(q) q^{3k/2} (\log q)^{2(k-1)} \)

for positive constants \(a_k, b_k\).

Conclusion and Future Directions

The study of shifted moments of L functions and moments of theta functions reveals a deep interplay in number theory, with applications in prime distribution, modular forms, and beyond. The reliance on GRH for many results underscores the need for its resolution, while conjectures on theta function moments suggest fertile ground for future research. Ongoing efforts aim to prove these asymptotics and explore new connections, potentially advancing our understanding of arithmetic structures.
