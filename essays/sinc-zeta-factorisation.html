1. Sinc Function and Zeta Zeros

The sinc function, defined as \( \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \), is pivotal in understanding the Riemann zeta function, \( \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s} \) (for \( \text{Re}(s) > 1 \)). The zeta function’s non-trivial zeros, conjectured to lie on the critical line \( \text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2} \) per the Riemann Hypothesis, are linked to prime number distributions. Their pair correlation, describing normalized spacings, reveals a striking pattern.

As noted in arXiv:0803.1141, the sine kernel \( \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \) emerges in the two-point correlation function of these zeros. For a height \( t \) on the critical line, the average spacing is \( \frac{2\pi}{\log t} \), and the pair correlation approximates:

\[ R_2(\delta) \approx 1 - \left( \frac{\sin(\pi \delta)}{\pi \delta} \right)^2 \]

Here, \( \delta \) is the normalized spacing between zeros. This form mirrors the eigenvalue correlations in the Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE) from random matrix theory, with \( \text{sinc}(\delta) \) arising as the Fourier transform of a rectangular function. This connection ties the oscillatory sinc function to prime distributions via the explicit formula, reinforcing its number-theoretic significance.

2. Sine Equation and Integer Factors

We investigate the equation \( y = \sin\left(\pi \frac{k}{y}\right) \), derived from \( k = x y \) and \( y = \sin(\pi x) \) by substituting \( x = \frac{k}{y} \), to see if its solutions \( y \) identify integer divisors of \( k \).

Analysis

If \( y = d \) is a divisor of \( k \), then \( \frac{k}{d} = m \) is an integer, so:

\[ d = \sin(\pi m) \]

Since \( \sin(\pi m) = 0 \) for integer \( m \), \( d = 0 \), which is trivial unless \( k = 0 \). Non-zero divisors (e.g., \( d = 1, 2 \)) yield contradictions (e.g., \( 1 \neq 0 \)).

Challenges

The sine oscillates between \(-1\) and \(1\), while divisors are positive integers. Solutions tend to cluster near \( y = 0+ \) due to rapid oscillations, not at divisors.

Approximations

For \( y = d + \epsilon \), approximate:

\[ \sin\left(\pi \frac{k}{d + \epsilon}\right) \approx (-1)^m \frac{\pi m \epsilon}{d} \]

But \( d + \epsilon \approx 0 \) is inconsistent for large \( d \). Numerically, solutions exist (e.g., \( k = 6 \), \( y \approx 0.318 \)), but they don’t align with divisors 1, 2, 3, 6.

3. Sinc Function in Factorization

Consider \( y = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \) with \( k = x y \), so:

\[ k = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi} \]

Can \( \text{sinc}(x) \) approximate divisors of \( k \)?

Solutions

Solving \( \sin(\pi x) = \pi k \), solutions exist only for \( |\pi k| \leq 1 \) (i.e., \( |k| \leq \frac{1}{\pi} \)), with \( x = \frac{1}{\pi} (\arcsin(\pi k) + 2\pi n) \). Integer \( k > 1 \) has no solutions.

Kernel Role

Summing \( \text{sinc}(d) \) over divisors \( d|k \) gives 0 (since \( \sin(\pi d) = 0 \) for \( d \neq 0 \)), offering no direct divisor insight. Its interpolation properties suggest potential in continuous approximations, but not here.

4. Linking Oscillatory Functions to Zeta

We explore how \( \sin(\pi x) \) and \( \text{sinc}(x) \) relate to \( \zeta(s) \)’s integral form:

\[ \zeta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{x^{s-1}}{e^x - 1} dx \]

Oscillatory Properties

\( \sin(\pi x) = 0 \) at integers; \( \text{sinc}(x) = \prod_{n=1}^\infty (1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2}) \) resembles \( \zeta(2) = \frac{\pi^2}{6} \).

Zeta and Primes

The explicit formula links primes to zeros, with \( \text{sinc}(x) \) in zero correlations. The integral form bridges discrete sums to continuous integrals.

Unified Perspective

Both functions connect discrete and continuous domains, suggesting oscillatory methods might approximate divisor or prime properties via zeta’s analytic framework.

Conclusion

The sinc function ties zeta zeros to prime statistics via the sine kernel. The equations \( y = \sin(\pi k / y) \) and \( k = x \cdot \text{sinc}(x) \) don’t directly yield divisors, but their oscillatory nature hints at potential analytic factorization methods, aligned with zeta’s discrete-to-continuous bridge. Future research could explore sinc-based sums for divisor approximations.
