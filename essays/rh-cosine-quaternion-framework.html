Abstract

We explore a speculative geometric framework that begins with a continuous cosine-based detector of integer divisors and attempts to generalize it toward a characterization of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. By lifting the divisor detection problem into quaternion space and interpreting alignment conditions as phase interference, we propose that the functional equation of the Riemann xi function imposes a symmetry constraint that may force zeros onto the critical line Re(s) = 1/2. Although the approach remains highly conjectural and faces significant analytic obstacles, it offers an unusual geometric perspective linking arithmetic divisibility, dyadic decompositions, quaternion geometry, and the oscillatory landscape of the zeta function.

1. Introduction

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH), first stated by Bernhard Riemann in 1859, asserts that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function lie on the critical line Re(s) = 1/2. Despite more than 160 years of effort and enormous computational verification, no proof exists.

This note presents a speculative geometric intuition derived from an extended exploratory discussion. The core idea starts with a surprisingly simple trigonometric expression that vanishes precisely at the divisors of an integer — a continuous detector of a discrete arithmetic relation. We lift this detector into quaternion space, then ask whether a similar phase-alignment condition, when generalized to the imaginary parts of potential zeta zeros and combined with the functional equation, might force zeros onto the critical line.

While far from rigorous, the framework connects several seemingly distant domains: geometric trigonometry, hardware-oriented rotation algorithms, divisor theory, and the spectral theory of the zeta function.

2. Mathematical Background

  • Riemann zeta function:
    \[ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} \quad (\Re(s)>1) \]
    continued meromorphically to ℂ with simple pole at s=1.
  • Riemann xi function (entire, symmetric):
    \[ \xi(s) = \frac{1}{2} s(s-1) \pi^{-s/2} \Gamma(s/2) \zeta(s), \qquad \xi(s)=\xi(1-s) \]
  • Explicit formula (von Mangoldt–Riemann): connects primes to zeros via oscillatory sums.
  • Euler product:
    \[ \zeta(s) = \prod_p \left(1 - p^{-s}\right)^{-1} \]

3. Cosine-based Divisor Detection

The starting observation is the function

\[ f(x) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{p}{x}\right) + \cos(2\pi x) - 2 \]

which equals zero if and only if both arguments are integers, i.e., precisely when x divides p (and p/x is integer).

This provides a continuous probe of the discrete divisor relation through the interference of two periodic waves whose frequencies are reciprocals on the hyperbolic branch xy = p.

4. Dyadic Geometric Construction & Connection to Divisors

Victor Geere’s purely geometric construction of the sine function via greedy dyadic angle halving provides a deep parallel. The target angle θ is approximated as a sum of dyadic fractions of π/2 using only Pythagorean relations.

Similarly, the reciprocal of any integer 1/d admits a greedy dyadic expansion:

\[ \frac{1}{d} \approx \sum_{j=0}^{m} c_j \, 2^{-j} \qquad (c_j \in \{0,1\}) \]

with exponential convergence. This suggests that both angle reconstruction and divisor reciprocals can be governed by the same hierarchical binary decomposition strategy.

5. Quaternion Lifting and Geometric Interpretation

We separate the two cosine components into orthogonal directions using the quaternion algebra ℍ:

\[ \phi(x) = \cos(2\pi x) \, \mathbf{i} + \cos\left(2\pi \frac{p}{x}\right) \, \mathbf{j} \]

Divisors then correspond to trajectory points where ϕ(x) passes near i + j (or scaled equivalents). The higher-dimensional geometry transforms a scalar zero-finding problem into a vector alignment question.

6. Extension Toward the Riemann Hypothesis

Generalize the construction by replacing the integer p with the imaginary part t of a potential zero s = σ + it:

\[ \phi_t(x) = \cos\left(2\pi \frac{t}{x}\right) \mathbf{i} + \cos(2\pi x)\, \mathbf{j} \]

We conjecture that perfect (or near-perfect) alignment is possible only when the functional equation symmetry is preserved, i.e., only when σ = 1/2.

7. Energy Functional and Warping under Functional Equation

Define the alignment energy

\[ E(x;\,t,\sigma) = \bigl[1 - \cos(2\pi t/x)\bigr]^2 + \bigl[1 - \cos(2\pi x)\bigr]^2 \]

Under the functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s), moving away from σ = 1/2 induces an effective frequency warp

\[ t \;\longrightarrow\; t' \sim \frac{t}{2\sigma - 1} \]

This breaks the natural frequency balance between t/x and x, preventing both cosines from simultaneously reaching their maxima.

8. Central Conjecture & Geometric Reformulation of RH

Conjecture (geometric version). Let ρ = σ + it be a non-trivial zero of ξ(s). Then inf_{x>0} E(x; t, σ) = 0 if and only if σ = 1/2.

In other words: perfect phase alignment between the two cosine terms is possible only on the critical line, where the functional equation induces no frequency distortion.

9. Major Challenges and Open Problems

  • No proven link between the energy functional E(x) and actual vanishing of ξ(s)
  • Lack of sharp uniform bounds on nonlinear exponential sums over primes of the form ∑ cos(2π α log p) or ∑ cos(2π α / u)
  • Justification of logarithmic → geometric phase substitution remains heuristic
  • Controlling error terms and constants to obtain a strict positive lower bound off the line

Any rigorous proof along this path would likely require major advances in exponential sum theory, moment estimates, and possibly spectral interpretations of ζ(s).

11. Conclusion and Outlook

This document presents an imaginative geometric reinterpretation that begins with a continuous cosine detector of integer divisors, lifts it into quaternion space, and speculatively extends the alignment condition toward the critical line of the Riemann zeta function.

While currently far from a proof, the framework offers a novel perspective that intertwines computational geometry, arithmetic phase interference, and the deepest unsolved problem in analytic number theory.
