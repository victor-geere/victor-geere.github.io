Chapter 1: Introduction to the Dirichlet Eta Function

The Dirichlet eta function, denoted as \(\eta(s)\), is a special function closely related to the Riemann zeta function. It is defined by the alternating series:

\[ \eta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^s} \]

This series converges for all complex numbers \(s\) where the real part satisfies \(\text{Re}(s) > 0\). The eta function can also be expressed in terms of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) as:

\[ \eta(s) = \left(1 - 2^{1 - s}\right) \zeta(s) \]

The purpose of this eBook is to explore the integral representation of the Dirichlet eta function, break it down into its real (cosine) and imaginary (sine) components, and use contour integration to identify values of \(s\) where both components simultaneously converge to zero.

Chapter 2: Integral Representation of the Eta Function

To obtain the integral representation of \(\eta(s)\), we start with its series definition and apply an integral identity. For \(\text{Re}(s) > 0\), the term \(\frac{1}{n^s}\) can be written as:

\[ \frac{1}{n^s} = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} e^{-nt} \, dt \]

where \(\Gamma(s)\) is the Gamma function. Substituting this into the eta function’s series, we get:

\[ \eta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} e^{-nt} \, dt \]

Assuming the interchange of the sum and integral is valid (which holds for \(\text{Re}(s) > 0\)), this becomes:

\[ \eta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} \left( \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} e^{-nt} \right) dt \]

The inner sum is an infinite geometric series with first term \(e^{-t}\) and common ratio \(-e^{-t}\). Summing it gives:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1} e^{-nt} = e^{-t} - e^{-2t} + e^{-3t} - \cdots = \frac{e^{-t}}{1 + e^{-t}} = \frac{1}{e^t + 1} \]

Thus, the integral representation of the Dirichlet eta function is:

\[ \eta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t + 1} \, dt \]

Chapter 3: Deconstructing the Integral into Sine and Cosine Components

Let’s express \(s\) as a complex number, \(s = \sigma + i\tau\), where \(\sigma = \text{Re}(s)\) and \(\tau = \text{Im}(s)\). The term \(t^{s-1}\) in the integral can be rewritten using Euler’s formula:

\[ t^{s-1} = t^{\sigma-1} e^{i\tau \ln t} = t^{\sigma-1} \left( \cos(\tau \ln t) + i \sin(\tau \ln t) \right) \]

Substituting this into the integral representation, we obtain:

\[ \eta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{\sigma-1} \left( \cos(\tau \ln t) + i \sin(\tau \ln t) \right)}{e^t + 1} \, dt \]

This integral can be split into its real and imaginary parts:

\[ \eta(s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \left[ \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{\sigma-1} \cos(\tau \ln t)}{e^t + 1} \, dt + i \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{\sigma-1} \sin(\tau \ln t)}{e^t + 1} \, dt \right] \]

Define the cosine and sine components as follows:

- \(I_c = \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{\sigma-1} \cos(\tau \ln t)}{e^t + 1} \, dt\) (real part) - \(I_s = \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{\sigma-1} \sin(\tau \ln t)}{e^t + 1} \, dt\) (imaginary part)

Thus, the eta function can be expressed as:

\[ \eta(s) = \frac{I_c + i I_s}{\Gamma(s)} \]

Here, \(I_c\) and \(I_s\) represent the real and imaginary contributions to the numerator of \(\eta(s)\).

Chapter 4: Using Contour Integration to Evaluate the Integral

To evaluate the integral \(\int_0^\infty \frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t + 1} \, dt\), we can use contour integration in the complex plane. Consider the function \(f(z) = \frac{z^{s-1}}{e^z + 1}\) with a branch cut along the positive real axis due to the multi-valued nature of \(z^{s-1}\). The poles of \(f(z)\) occur where \(e^z + 1 = 0\), i.e., \(e^z = -1\), which happens at:

\[ z = i\pi (2k + 1), \quad k \in \mathbb{Z} \]

A common approach is to use a keyhole contour to account for the branch cut. The contour integral around this path relates the real-axis integral to the sum of residues at the poles. The result is:

\[ \left(1 - e^{i2\pi (s-1)}\right) \int_0^\infty \frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t + 1} \, dt = 2\pi i \sum_{\text{poles}} \text{Res} f(z) \]

Computing the residue at a pole \(z_k = i\pi (2k + 1)\), we find:

\[ \text{Res}_{z = z_k} f(z) = \lim_{z \to z_k} (z - z_k) \frac{z^{s-1}}{e^z + 1} = z_k^{s-1} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{z_k}} = - [i\pi (2k + 1)]^{s-1} \]

Summing the residues and solving provides a series form of the integral. However, for our purpose of finding zeros, we can leverage the known relationship \(\eta(s) = (1 - 2^{1 - s}) \zeta(s)\) directly, as explored in the next chapter.

Chapter 5: Finding Inputs Where Both Components Converge to Zero

We seek values of \(s\) where both \(I_c = 0\) and \(I_s = 0\), making \(\eta(s) = 0\) (since \(\Gamma(s) \neq 0\) for \(\text{Re}(s) > 0\)). Using the relation:

\[ \eta(s) = (1 - 2^{1 - s}) \zeta(s) \]

\(\eta(s) = 0\) occurs when either \(\zeta(s) = 0\) (the zeros of the zeta function) or \(1 - 2^{1 - s} = 0\). Solve the latter condition:

\[ 1 - 2^{1 - s} = 0 \implies 2^{1 - s} = 1 \] \[ 1 - s = i \cdot \frac{2\pi k}{\ln 2}, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z} \] \[ s = 1 + i \cdot \frac{2\pi k}{\ln 2} \]

For \(k \neq 0\), these points lie on the line \(\text{Re}(s) = 1\). At these values of \(s\), \(\eta(s) = 0\), implying both the real part \(I_c\) and the imaginary part \(I_s\) of the integral numerator must vanish simultaneously.

Chapter 6: Conclusion and Algebraic Simplification

In this eBook, we’ve deconstructed the integral representation of the Dirichlet eta function into its sine and cosine components and identified inputs where both components converge to zero. These inputs are:

\[ s = 1 + \frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2}, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\} \]

These values correspond to zeros of \(\eta(s)\) along the line \(\text{Re}(s) = 1\), arising from the factor \(1 - 2^{1 - s} = 0\). At these points, the real and imaginary parts of the integral vanish, satisfying \(\eta(s) = 0\). This concludes our exploration of the eta function’s integral representation and its zeros.
