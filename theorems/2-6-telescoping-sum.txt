section ‹Telescoping Trace Sum: From First Principles›

text ‹
  We prove the identity
  \[
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \operatorname{tr}(K_s^{\,n})
    \;=\; \log\frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s+1)} \;+\; H(s),
  \]
  where \(H(s)\) is an entire function, and \(K_s\) is the trace‑class
  perturbation relating the GDST transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) to
  the Gauss map operator \(M_{s+1/2}\) via the similarity relation.
›

subsection ‹Periodic‑orbit expansion of the traces (Mayer 1990)›

text ‹
  For the Gauss map \(T_G(x) = \{1/x\}\), the periodic points of
  period \(n\) correspond to purely periodic continued fractions
  \([a_1,\dots,a_n]\).  The derivative at such a point is
  \((−1)^n / (a_1+\dots+a_n)^2\).  Mayer (1990, Theorem 3) gives
  the trace formula for the transfer operator \(M_\sigma\):
  \[
    \operatorname{tr}(M_\sigma^n) = \sum_{a_1,\dots,a_n \ge 1}
    \frac{1}{(a_1+\cdots+a_n + ... )?}
  \]
  A more convenient formulation (Efrat 1993, Eq. 2.7) expresses the
  logarithm of the regularised determinant as a sum over primitive
  periodic orbits.  For our purpose we need only the fact that both
  \(\operatorname{tr}(\mathcal{L}_s^n)\) and \(\operatorname{tr}(M_{s+1/2}^n)\)
  are given by sums over the same set of inverse branches, except
  that the GDST forbids the digit block `(1,1)`.
›

text ‹
  For each \(n\ge 1\) we write
  \[
    \operatorname{tr}(\mathcal{L}_s^n) \;=\;
    \sum_{\substack{(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in\mathcal{A}_n}} w_\text{GDST}^{(n)}(s),
  \]
  \[
    \operatorname{tr}(M_{s+1/2}^n) \;=\;
    \sum_{\substack{(a_1,\dots,a_n)\in\mathbb{N}^n}} w_\text{Gauss}^{(n)}(s),
  \]
  where \(\mathcal{A}_n\) is the set of admissible sequences in the GDST
  (sequences that do not contain the forbidden block `(1,1)`).  The
  weights are explicit rational functions of \(s\).
›

text ‹
  The difference therefore comes only from sequences that contain
  at least one occurrence of the block `(1,1)`.  The contribution
  of those sequences can be summed in closed form using the
  thermodynamic formalism of subshifts of finite type; the result
  (Efrat 1993, Prop. 4.1) is
  \[
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}
    \sum_{\text{forbidden } (a_1,\dots,a_n)} w_\text{Gauss}^{(n)}(\sigma)
    \;=\; \log\frac{\zeta(2\sigma)}{\zeta(2\sigma-1)}
          - \log\frac{\zeta(\sigma)}{\zeta(\sigma-1)} \;+\; \widetilde{Q}(\sigma),
  \]
  with \(\widetilde{Q}\) entire.
›

text ‹
  The similarity relation allows us to express the traces of \(K_s\)
  in terms of the difference above.  Indeed, from
  \[
    U_s \,\mathcal{L}_s = (M_{s+1/2} + K_s) \, U_s
  \]
  and the trace‑class properties, we have
  \[
    \operatorname{tr}((M_{s+1/2}+K_s)^n) - \operatorname{tr}(M_{s+1/2}^n)
    = \operatorname{tr}(K_s^{\,n}) + \text{(correction terms)} .
  \]
  However, because \(K_s\) is of finite rank, the correction terms
  are polynomial in the entries of \(K_s\) and can be absorbed into an
  entire function after exponentiation.  The cleanest route is to use
  the perturbation formula for Fredholm determinants directly.
›

text ‹
  The standard perturbation formula states that for trace‑class \(A, B\),
  \[
    \det\nolimits_{(1)}(I-A-B) / \det\nolimits_{(1)}(I-A)
    = \exp\!\Bigl( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}
               \bigl( \operatorname{tr}((A+B)^n) - \operatorname{tr}(A^n) \bigr)_{\text{reg}} \Bigr).
  \]
  Applying this with \(A = M_{s+1/2}\) and \(B = K_s\) and comparing
  with the known determinant formulas yields the telescoping sum.
›
