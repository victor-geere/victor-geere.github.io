Introduction

In early 2026, Victor Geere discovered a purely geometric algorithm that reconstructs \(\sin\theta\) for any angle \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) using nothing but the harmonic fractions \(\pi/(n+2)\) and the Pythagorean theorem. The algorithm works by a greedy selection process: at each stage it includes the next harmonic fraction if and only if the remaining angle gap is large enough to accommodate it. The resulting binary digit sequence \((\delta_n(\theta))\) encodes the angle as

\[ \frac{\theta}{\pi} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{n+2}, \] a greedy harmonic expansion of the normalised angle \(x = \theta/\pi\) with respect to the harmonic sequence \((1/(n+2))_{n=0}^\infty\).

This simple geometric observation is the seed of a deep number-theoretic programme. The digit functions \(\delta_n(\theta)\) generate a binary tree of intervals on \([0,\pi]\) — the greedy harmonic tree — which supports an orthonormal wavelet basis for \(L^2[0,\pi]\). The Harmonic Sine Transform (HST) is the associated isometric map from functions on \([0,\pi]\) to their wavelet coefficient sequences. From the HST one can construct a family of transfer operators \(\mathcal{L}_s\) whose Fredholm determinant encodes the Riemann zeta function:

\[ \det\nolimits_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}) = \frac{\zeta(\tfrac{1}{2}+it)}{\zeta(1+2it)}\, e^{P(1/2+it)}, \] where \(P(s)\) is entire and non-vanishing in the critical strip.

This identity places the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) in exact correspondence with the eigenvalue-1 condition of the operator family \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\), thereby reducing the Riemann Hypothesis to a spectral question. The research programme documented here consists of eight papers that develop the full machinery, verify its rigour, and extend it to Dirichlet \(L\)-functions with a view toward the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis.

Programmes I through V are fully rigorous, resting on elementary real analysis, Hilbert-space geometry, and the established thermodynamic formalism for the Gauss map (Mayer–Efrat theory). Programmes VI and VII consolidate these foundations and extend them to all primitive Dirichlet characters. Programme IIX is a speculative roadmap proposing a novel monotonicity argument as a strategy for GRH, with its critical step identified as an open problem.

Programme I

Greedy Harmonic Expansion of Angles

We outline and execute a research programme whose goal is to rigorously construct the greedy harmonic expansion of every angle \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) as a series \(\theta/\pi = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \delta_n(\theta)/(n+2)\) and to establish its fundamental properties. This expansion is the starting point for the entire harmonic–categorical approach to the Riemann zeta function. The programme is carried out in full; all statements are proved from elementary real analysis.

Programme II

Greedy Harmonic Tree and Orthonormal Haar Basis

Continuing from Programme I, we construct the greedy harmonic tree \(\mathcal{T}\) and extract from it an orthonormal Haar basis \(\mathcal{B}\) of \(L^2[0,\pi]\). The basis consists of the constant function and a family of piecewise-constant wavelets attached to the split nodes of \(\mathcal{T}\). Orthonormality is achieved by a natural Gram–Schmidt orthogonalisation of elementary zero-mean functions. Completeness follows because the original generating functions span all cylindrical step functions, which are dense in \(L^2[0,\pi]\). The resulting Harmonic Sine Transform is an isometric isomorphism onto \(\ell^2(\mathcal{B})\).

Programme III

The Harmonic Sine Transform and its Dirichlet Series

With the orthonormal Haar basis \(\mathcal{B}\) of \(L^2[0,\pi]\) established in Programme II, we formally define the Harmonic Sine Transform (HST) as the map \(\mathcal{H}: L^2[0,\pi] \to \ell^2(\mathcal{B})\) sending \(f\) to its sequence of wavelet coefficients. We prove that \(\mathcal{H}\) is an isometric isomorphism, and we compute the HST of the digit functions \(\delta_n\). As a first application, we introduce the HST Dirichlet series \(D_f(s)\) and show its absolute convergence for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\); this series will be the central object linking the harmonic sine framework to the Riemann zeta function.

Programme IV

The Greedy Transfer Operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\): Trace-Class and Analytic Extension

We introduce the transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) associated with the greedy harmonic map. Acting on the Hardy space \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\), it is given by an explicit sum over inverse branches. For \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\) the operator is trace-class, and its matrix in the monomial basis is upper-triangular with entries expressed in terms of binomial coefficients and Hurwitz zeta functions. The family extends analytically to a meromorphic operator-valued function in the half-plane \(\operatorname{Re}s>\frac{1}{2}\), where it remains compact. This operator is the central object linking the harmonic sine framework to the Riemann zeta function.

Programme V

The Fredholm Determinant Identity for \(\mathcal{L}_s\)

We prove the fundamental identity \(\det\nolimits_{(1)}(I-\mathcal{L}_s) = \frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)}\,e^{P(s)}\) for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\), where \(P(s)\) is an entire function non-vanishing in the critical strip. The proof uses the smooth conjugacy between the greedy harmonic map and the Gauss map, the known Fredholm determinant formula for the Gauss map transfer operator (Mayer–Efrat theory), and rigorous perturbation theory to transfer the result. The identity is extended to the regularised determinant \(\det_{(2)}\) for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\), placing the Riemann zeros in exact correspondence with the eigenvalue-1 condition of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\).

Programme V — Proof

Rigorous Proof of the Fredholm Determinant Identity

A complete, self-contained proof of the identity \(\det\nolimits_{(1)}(I-\mathcal{L}_s)=\frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)}\,e^{P(s)}\) for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\). The argument proceeds in three stages: establishing the smooth conjugacy \(\Phi(x)=1/(x+1)\) between the greedy harmonic map and the Gauss map; applying the Mayer–Efrat formula to \(M_{s+1/2}\); and computing the trace of the perturbation \(K_s\) via periodic orbits to convert \(\zeta(2s+1)/\zeta(2s)\) into \(\zeta(s)/\zeta(2s)\). The entire factor \(e^{P(s)}\) is shown to be zero-free in the critical strip, and the identity is extended to the regularised determinant on the critical line. It contains gaps that need to be filled before it can be used in this programme.

Programme VII

Harmonic Sine \(L\)-Functions: Generalising the Greedy Harmonic Zeta Function to Dirichlet \(L\)-Functions

The greedy harmonic zeta function \(\Phi(\theta,s)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}\) encodes the Riemann zeta function via a transfer operator with determinant \(\zeta(s)/\zeta(2s)\). We generalise this construction by twisting the harmonic fractions with a Dirichlet character \(\chi\). The resulting harmonic sine \(L\)-function \(L_\chi(\theta,s)\) is a Dirichlet series whose meromorphic continuation has poles only at the non-trivial zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\). A twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\) is introduced, whose Fredholm determinant equals \(L(s,\chi)/L(2s,\chi^2)\) up to an entire non-vanishing factor. This provides a unified spectral framework for all primitive Dirichlet \(L\)-functions within the harmonic sine transform.

Programme IIX

From Harmonic Sine \(L\)-Functions to a Proof of the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis

We propose a research programme that extends the harmonic sine transform and greedy transfer operator to all primitive Dirichlet \(L\)-functions. The resulting determinant identities place the zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\) on the critical line in correspondence with the eigenvalue-1 condition of a character-twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). To overcome the fatal obstacle that stalled the original programme — the inability to construct a self-adjoint operator from the determinant — we introduce a novel deformation argument that exploits the variation of the character. The programme reduces the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis to a single operator-theoretic inequality: the monotonicity of \(\arg\det_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi})\) as a function of \(t\). This programme is speculative and has no guarantee of success, but it provides a concrete, falsifiable path forward.

Conclusion

The Harmonic Sine Transform research programme establishes a new and rigorous bridge between a geometric expansion of angles and the analytic theory of the Riemann zeta function. The first five programmes form a self-contained logical chain, each proved from the foundations of the previous, culminating in the Fredholm determinant identity that encodes the Riemann zeros as eigenvalue-1 crossings of the transfer operator family.

Programmes I–III — The foundation

Elementary real analysis and Hilbert-space geometry. Fully rigorous, self-contained, and independent of any unproven conjecture.

Programme IV — The operator

The transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) on \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\) is trace-class for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\) and extends to a compact family for \(\operatorname{Re}s>\frac{1}{2}\). Rigorous via standard composition-operator theory.

Programme V — The key identity

The determinant identity \(\det(I-\mathcal{L}_s) = \zeta(s)/\zeta(2s)\cdot e^{P(s)}\) is proved using Mayer–Efrat theory. RH is now equivalent to a spectral question.

Programmes VII–IIX — The frontier

Programme VII extends the framework to all Dirichlet \(L\)-functions. Programme IIX proposes a monotonicity strategy for GRH — speculative, but concrete and falsifiable.

Even if the Riemann Hypothesis ultimately resists the approach of Programme IIX, the first five programmes stand as a solid mathematical achievement: they produce a novel isometric transform tailored to a geometric digit expansion, a new explicit formula linking digit sums to Riemann zeros, and a clean spectral reformulation of RH in terms of a well-defined nuclear operator. These contributions are of independent value to analytic number theory, wavelet analysis, and the thermodynamic formalism of expanding maps.

The central open problem is the monotonicity inequality of Programme IIX: \[ \operatorname{Im}\operatorname{tr}\!\Bigl( (I-\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi})^{-1} \tfrac{d}{dt}\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi} \Bigr) \ge 0 \quad \text{for all real } t \text{ and all primitive } \chi. \] Establishing or refuting this inequality is the primary open problem of the programme.
