The GHF Mathematical Story

How the equations connect · what each piece means · why it matters

🔴 Honest preamble
This companion explains the relationships between the rigorously proved results of the Greedy Harmonic Framework (GHF) and places them in context. It does not claim a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. The central open problem — the exhaustion gap — is clearly identified and remains unsolved. What the GHF does provide is a new, coherent mathematical language in which RH can be stated as a concrete spectral/geometric problem.

1. Why start with greedy expansion of an angle?

The whole GHF begins with a disarmingly simple question: can you express any angle θ ∈ [0,π] as a sum of harmonic angles αₙ = π/(n+2) using a greedy algorithm? The answer is yes — and the resulting binary sequence δₙ(θ) carries deep arithmetic information.

What is proved:
  • The greedy expansion converges at rate O(1/N).
  • The selection map θ ↦ δₙ(θ) is monotone (Lemma 2.1), so each digit switches from 0 to 1 exactly once, at a threshold θₙ*.
  • Those thresholds are exactly αₙ themselves (Theorem 2.3).
Why it's novel: No standard number‑theoretic expansion (binary, continued fraction, etc.) has thresholds that equal the step sizes. This self‑consistency is a unique property of the harmonic sequence and is the mathematical reason the whole framework hangs together so cleanly.

The digits δₙ(θ) are the central characters of the story. They are to the GHF what prime numbers are to classical analytic number theory — the fundamental combinatorial objects from which everything else is built.

2. The correlation kernel — a harmonic Hilbert matrix

Once you have a sequence of functions δₙ(θ), the natural next step is to centre them (subtract the mean θ/π) and measure their correlations:

\[ K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \bigl[\delta_n(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr] \bigl[\delta_m(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr]\,d\theta . \]
What is proved: The kernel has an exact, closed rational form. For n ≠ m it resembles a harmonic‑weighted Hilbert matrix. The kernel is positive semi‑definite (it's a Gram matrix) and its eigenvalues are bounded explicitly.

Why it matters: This kernel is the bridge between the combinatorial greedy digits and the analytic world of operators. Every subsequent operator — the GDST, the transfer operator, the Fredholm determinant — is built from K(n,m) and its weighted versions.

Novelty: A closed‑form correlation kernel for a number‑theoretic expansion has never appeared before. It is the analogue of the Hilbert matrix 1/(n+m‑1) that appears in the theory of the Riemann zeta function via the Nyman–Beurling criterion, but with an exact rational structure that makes explicit computations possible.

3. The universal rank‑2 decomposition

The kernel K(n,m) is not an arbitrary matrix. It has a hidden low‑rank structure that emerges whenever the test functions are step functions minus a common centring function.

Theorem 4.1 (Universal Rank‑2 Decomposition): For any partition of [0,L] and any centring function g(θ), the Gram matrix of the centred indicators decomposes exactly as \[ K = \Delta + L,\qquad \text{rank}(L) = 2. \] The Fredholm determinant of the weighted operator M(s) = D(s)KD(s) then factorises as a product of (1−λₙ(s)) times two finite‑rank correction factors (1−α(s))(1−β(s)).

Why this is so important: This factorisation means that the entire spectral problem for M(s) reduces to studying a diagonal operator plus a 2×2 determinant. It is the mathematical engine that makes the Fredholm determinant identity tractable. In the GHF programme, this structure is what allows the connection to ζ(s)/ζ(2s) to be expressed in closed form.

Novelty: The universality of the rank‑2 decomposition is a genuine discovery. It does not depend on the specific harmonic partition — it holds for any partition and any centring function. The GHF specialises it to the greedy harmonic partition with g(θ)=θ/π, which then plugs into the zeta world.

4. Making the greedy digits talk to the complex plane

The greedy Dirichlet sub‑sum

\[ E(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s} \]

converges for Re(s) > 1, but to interact with zeta you need to push it to the whole complex plane.

Theorem 7.1 (Meromorphic continuation): E(θ,s) extends to ℂ with a single simple pole at s=1 of residue θ/π. Three independent proofs: Mellin transform, Hadamard regularisation, and theta‑integral. The continuation satisfies the global splitting identity \[ \tilde{E}(\theta,s) + \tilde{\Omega}(\theta,s) = \zeta(s)-1. \]

Meaning: This is the GHF analogue of the analytic continuation of ζ(s) itself. The splitting identity shows that the greedy sub‑sum and its complement exactly reconstruct the Riemann zeta function. The GHF has not invented a new zeta function; it has decomposed the existing one into two selection‑based pieces that encode which harmonic steps were taken.

Novelty: Analytic continuation of a sum governed by a combinatorial selection rule (rather than a multiplicative character or a modular form) is rare. The three methods are robust and generalisable to other greedy sequences.

5. The dynamical system behind the greedy expansion

The greedy algorithm defines a piecewise Möbius map T on [0,1] that encodes the remainder after each step. Its transfer operator ℒₛ acts on the Hardy space H²(𝔻):

\[ (\mathcal{L}_s f)(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{n+1}{(n+2)^{s+1}} \Bigl[ f\!\Bigl(\frac{n+1}{n+2}z\Bigr) + f\!\Bigl(\frac{n+1}{n+2}z + \frac{1}{n+2}\Bigr) \Bigr]. \]
Theorem 8.1–8.2: ℒₛ is trace‑class for Re(s) > 1/2 and is similar to the Mayer operator of the Gauss map (the continued fraction transformation). Mayer's operator is famous because its Fredholm determinant equals ζ(2σ)/ζ(2σ−1)·eQ(σ).

Why this is the heart of the programme: The similarity to Mayer's operator is the reason the GHF can hope to connect to ζ(s). Mayer proved his identity using the dynamics of the Gauss map. The GHF hopes to prove an analogous identity for the greedy harmonic map. The similarity theorem shows that the two operators are not distant cousins — they are essentially the same object viewed through different coordinates.

Novelty: The similarity is explicit and constructive. It is not a black‑box existence theorem; the Möbius conjugation is written down. This means that if the telescoping trace sum can be completed, the Fredholm determinant identity will follow by direct computation, not by analogy.

6. The bridge to ζ(s) — not yet crossed

The central unproven claim: \[ \det\nolimits_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = \frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)}\, e^{P(s)}, \qquad P(s) \text{ entire}. \] The GDST Programme paper provides a sketch of a telescoping trace sum that would prove this, but the sketch is not yet a complete proof. Until it is, the identity remains a conjecture.

What would it mean if proved? It would give a spectral interpretation of the zeros of ζ(s): the non‑trivial zeros would correspond exactly to eigenvalues of ℒₛ where the determinant vanishes. Combined with the formal symmetry ℒₛ* = ℒs̄, this would make ℒ1/2+it self‑adjoint — a Hilbert–Pólya realisation of the zeros as eigenvalues of a self‑adjoint operator.

Why it's hard: The telescoping trace sum involves subtracting two divergent series whose difference converges conditionally. Controlling the remainder requires precise asymptotic estimates of the trace of powers of the perturbation operator, which in turn depend on the correlation kernel's spectral properties at infinity. This is a delicate analytic problem, not a formal manipulation.

7. The φ‑map — a fixed‑point formulation of RH

Independently of the Fredholm determinant, the GHF provides a second, completely rigorous connection to ζ(s):

\[ \phi(s) = s + \eta(s) \quad (\operatorname{Re} s = 1/2), \]

where η(s) is the Dirichlet eta function. Therefore

\[ \phi(s) = s \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \eta(s) = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{s is a non‑trivial zero of ζ(s)}. \]
This is proved. It's an algebraic identity — no analytic continuation, no unproven conjectures. The GHF constructs φ from the same greedy digits δₙ(θ), integrated against alternating weights.

Significance: This reformulates RH as a fixed‑point problem for a map φ: ℂ → ℂ. The Jacobian determinant det J(s) = |η'(s)+1/2|² − 1/4 is non‑zero at all verified zeros, meaning the fixed points are locally stable. The mismatch functional Δ(t) measures how far φ is from being a fixed point; its zeros include all zeta zeros (proved inclusion, conditional on the determinant identity).

Novelty: No classical formulation of RH takes the form of a fixed‑point equation for a map built directly from the digits of an angle expansion. It opens a new avenue for both analytic and computational attacks.

8. The eml operator — one gate to rule them all

Separate from the zeta connection, the GHF contains a result in universal algebra:

\[ \operatorname{eml}(x,y) := \exp(x) - \ln(y). \]

This single binary operation can express exp, ln, addition, multiplication, subtraction, division, and square roots — all via finite binary trees whose leaves are the constant 1.

Theorems 11.1–11.3: Both exp(z) and ln(z) are exactly representable as finite eml expressions. Every arithmetic operation (+, −, ×, /, √) can be built from eml. Consequently, the entire harmonic sine fusion step can be written as a finite eml tree.

Why it's in the GHF: The sine function appears in the functional equation of ζ(s). If the harmonic sine approximation can be expressed in eml, then the zeta functional equation can be encoded in a single algebraic operation — a universal gate for the whole theory. This is a long‑term vision, not yet connected to a proof of RH, but it suggests that the GHF framework is algebraically closed in a profound way.

Novelty: The eml operator was discovered by Odrzywołek (2026) independently. The GHF shows that it captures the greedy harmonic sine construction exactly, creating a bridge between universal algebra and analytic number theory that had not been anticipated.

9. Geometry — primes as intersections of helices

The final layer of the GHF translates the entire story into differential geometry. Dirichlet terms n−s are embedded as points on twin conical helices in ℝ³. Their Möbius‑inverted superposition isolates the prime numbers. The torsion of the resulting curve is a distribution supported exactly on log p — a geometric analogue of the explicit formula.

Conjecture 12.1 (Geometric Explicit Formula): \[ \operatorname{tors}_1(t) = \tau_{\text{prime}}(t) + \tau_{\text{arch}}(t) \quad \text{in } \mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R}). \] This is equivalent to the Eigencoefficient Prime Sum Conjecture of Connes–Consani–Moscovici. Theorem 12.1: Conditional on Conjecture 12.1, RH is equivalent to the statement that the torsion of the variable‑radius helix equals the torsion of the constant‑radius (Riemann) helix.

Meaning: The geometric reformulation turns RH into a statement about the rigidity of a curve: if the prime distribution forces the helix to have constant radius, then all non‑trivial zeros must lie on the critical line. The curve's torsion encodes the primes; its radius encodes the real parts of the zeros. RH says the two are locked together in the simplest possible way.

Novelty: This is a fully geometric statement of RH. It connects to the Connes–Consani–Moscovici spectral triple programme, where the explicit formula appears as a trace formula on a noncommutative space. The GHF provides the classical geometric analogue: what CCM do with Dirac operators and spectral triples, the GHF does with classical curves and their torsion.

10. The exhaustion gap — what stands between the GHF and RH

The framework proves inclusion: every non‑trivial zero of ζ(s) corresponds to a zero of the mismatch functional Δ(t). It does not prove the converse: that every zero of Δ(t) comes from a zeta zero. This reverse inclusion is logically equivalent to RH.

The exhaustion gap is not a technical detail — it is the entire problem. The GHF reduces RH to proving that Δ(t) has no "extraneous" zeros beyond those forced by the zeta zeros. All numerical evidence supports this (no extraneous zeros have been found up to the 100+ verified ordinates), but a proof would require a global spectral argument — essentially, a proof that the Fredholm determinant identity holds with an error term that forces the zero sets to coincide.

In the geometric language, the exhaustion gap is the gap between "the prime distribution determines the torsion" and "the torsion determines the radius." The first is Conjecture 12.1; the second is Theorem 12.1. Together they would prove RH. The GHF has constructed all the machinery; the missing ingredient is the rigorous justification that the torsion of the helix (the prime distribution) uniquely forces the radius to be constant.

12. What the GHF has achieved — and what it hasn't

The Greedy Harmonic Framework is a coherent mathematical structure that connects a simple greedy angle expansion to the Riemann zeta function through an intricate web of operators, determinants, and geometries. The parts that are proved are genuine contributions:

  • A new universal matrix decomposition (rank‑2)
  • An exactly solvable correlation kernel for a number‑theoretic digit sequence
  • A meromorphic continuation of a selection‑based Dirichlet series
  • A trace‑class transfer operator similar to Mayer's
  • A fixed‑point map that reformulates RH without unproven conjectures
  • A universal algebraic gate that encodes the harmonic sine construction
  • A geometric translation of the explicit formula into the language of curves and torsion

What remains is the hard analytic step: proving that the Fredholm determinant of the transfer operator really equals ζ(s)/ζ(2s) times an entire function that does not introduce extraneous zeros. The GHF has reduced this to a concrete telescoping sum. Whether that sum can be tamed is an open problem — and until it is, RH remains open.

The GHF's real significance may be less about RH itself and more about the new language it provides. It shows that the zeros of ζ(s) are not isolated number‑theoretic objects — they are encoded in the correlations of a greedy algorithm, the spectrum of a dynamical transfer operator, the fixed points of a complex map, the torsion of a helix, and the algebraic structure of a single binary operation. These are deep connections that will remain interesting regardless of whether the exhaustion gap is ever closed.
