Can the Argument Principle on F(s) = \eta(s)/\eta(1-s) Prove Condition (C4)?

The geometric contradiction required in Condition (C4) of the Riemann Hypothesis proof sketch states that the existence of an off‑critical‑line zero leads to an impossibility. One proposed alternative is to use the function F(s) = \eta(s)/\eta(1-s) and the argument principle. Below we rigorously examine this approach, state the lemmas that would be needed, and clarify what can and cannot be proved. The conclusion is that a straightforward application of the argument principle to F(s) yields no contradiction; the route requires additional, non‑trivial input that remains unproven.

1. The Identity F(s) \equiv M(s) and its Consequences

Lemma 1 (Functional equation as identity). The Dirichlet eta function satisfies, for all s \in \mathbb{C} where the functions are defined, \[ \eta(s) = M(s)\,\eta(1-s), \qquad M(s) = \frac{1-2^{1-s}}{1-2^s}\,\frac{\Gamma(s)}{\Gamma(1-s)}. \] Therefore, as meromorphic functions, \[ F(s) \;:=\; \frac{\eta(s)}{\eta(1-s)} \equiv M(s), \] whenever \eta(1-s) \neq 0.
Proof. This is the standard functional equation for the eta function, obtained from \Lambda(s)=\Lambda(1-s) and the definition \Lambda(s)=\Gamma(s+1)(1-2^{1-s})\zeta(s). The factor M(s) is meromorphic with known zeros and poles.
Lemma 2 (Location of zeros and poles of M(s)). The function M(s) has
  • zeros exactly at the points s = 1 + \frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2} (k\in\mathbb{Z}), all lying on the line \operatorname{Re}(s)=1;
  • poles exactly at the points s = \frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2} (k\in\mathbb{Z}), all lying on the line \operatorname{Re}(s)=0;
  • no zeros or poles inside the open strip 0 < \operatorname{Re}(s) < 1.
Proof. The numerator 1-2^{1-s} vanishes when 2^{1-s}=1, i.e. 1-s = \frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2}, giving s=1-\frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2}; a sign change gives the stated zeros. The denominator 1-2^s vanishes when s = \frac{2\pi i k}{\ln 2}. The gamma ratio \Gamma(s)/\Gamma(1-s) has no zeros and its poles are at s = -n for n\in\mathbb{N}_0 and 1-s = -m i.e. s = 1+m, none of which lie in the strip 0<\operatorname{Re}(s)<1. Hence the only singularities in that strip could come from the 1-2^{\dots} factors, and those lie on the boundaries.
Lemma 3 (Immediate consequence for zeros of \eta). If \rho is a zero of \eta with 0<\operatorname{Re}(\rho)<1, then 1-\rho is also a zero of \eta of the same multiplicity. This is the well‑known symmetry of the non‑trivial zeros.
Proof. From F(\rho)=M(\rho). Since M(\rho) \neq 0,\infty in the strip, the ratio \eta(\rho)/\eta(1-\rho) is finite and non‑zero. Thus \eta(\rho)=0 if and only if \eta(1-\rho)=0, and the multiplicities match.
Conclusion. The argument principle applied to F(s) = M(s) on any closed contour inside 0<\operatorname{Re}(s)<1 yields exactly the number of zeros minus poles of M(s), which is zero. It gives no information about the horizontal location of the zeros of \eta. Consequently, this cannot prove that all zeros lie on the critical line. A different function or a deeper argument is required.

2. What Would a Function‑Theoretic Proof of RH Require?

To obtain a contradiction from an off‑line zero, one must use a function that carries information about the horizontal asymmetry. Below we list possible functions and the lemmas that would be necessary to complete a proof; none of them is known to yield a rigorous contradiction at present.

Candidate A. The function H(s) = \frac{\eta(s)}{\eta(1-\bar{s})}.
This is the natural anti‑symmetrization that would be 1 on the critical line if all zeros were on the line, because \eta(\frac12+it) / \overline{\eta(\frac12+it)} has modulus 1. An off‑line zero at \rho = \sigma+it with \sigma>1/2 would create a zero of H at \rho and a pole at 1-\bar{\rho}, both on the same side of the critical line? Actually 1-\bar{\rho} = 1-\sigma+it, so they are symmetric about the line \sigma=1/2. The maximum principle might forbid such a zero–pole pair inside a region bounded by the critical line and the line \sigma=1, provided |H| is bounded by 1 on the boundary. However, |H(\sigma+it)| = |\eta(\sigma+it)/\overline{\eta(1-\sigma+it)}|. On the critical line, |H|=1. On \sigma=1, |\eta(1+it)/\overline{\eta(0+it)}| is not necessarily bounded by 1; the eta function grows like t^{\sigma} in that region. So the boundary condition is not controlled.
Candidate B. The function \xi(s) and the argument principle on a rectangle.
Classical proofs that RH is equivalent to the positivity of the Li coefficients or the Weil explicit formula rely on showing that all zeros must lie on the line. A purely function‑theoretic argument via the argument principle would need to show that the change in argument of \xi(s) along a contour encircling the entire critical strip up to height T is exactly the number of zeros on the line, no more. This is the usual technique that leads to the Riemann–von Mangoldt formula, but it does not rule out off‑line zeros; it merely counts them. So no contradiction.
Candidate C. Jensen’s formula and the density of zeros.
If an off‑line zero exists, it would force an excess of zeros in a sub‑strip, which could be detected by a Jensen integral. To prove a contradiction, one would need an exact formula for the mean density of zeros off the line, which is not available.

3. Creative Avenues and the Current Gap

Despite the failure of the naive argument principle, one might attempt to combine F(s) with the fact that |F(1/2+it)|=1 and that F(s) is an inner function in a suitably defined half‑strip. The existence of an off‑line zero of \eta does not manifest as a zero of F(s), because F is identically M(s). However, if we consider the function G(s) = \eta(s)/\overline{\eta(1-\bar{s})} (the ‘Schwarz‑reflected’ ratio), then an off‑line zero at \rho would give a zero of G at \rho and a pole at 1-\bar{\rho}. The critical line is where G(s) = e^{2i\arg\eta(s)}, so |G|=1 there. One might hope to apply the following lemma (which is unproven):

Unproven Lemma (Boundedness of |G(s)| in the right half‑strip). There exists a constant C such that for \frac12 < \sigma < 1 and all real t, \[ |G(\sigma+it)| \le 1. \] If this held, then the maximum principle would force any zero of G in the region to be on the boundary, i.e., on the critical line, proving RH. However, this boundedness is equivalent to the statement that the real part of \eta'/\eta has a definite sign, which is not known and is in fact equivalent to RH (via the Deuring–Weil criterion).

4. Honest Conclusion

The function F(s)=\eta(s)/\eta(1-s) is exactly the known factor M(s) of the functional equation. Its argument principle contains no information about the horizontal distribution of zeros beyond the symmetry \rho \leftrightarrow 1-\rho. Therefore no proof of (C4) can be based on a straightforward application of the argument principle to F(s).

Any rigorous proof of the Riemann Hypothesis via a function‑theoretic contradiction will require a genuinely new insight — most likely a function whose boundedness or positivity is equivalent to the statement that all zeros lie on the critical line. The geometric foliation argument, as attractive as it is, currently relies on unverified topological claims; the operator‑theoretic route via Yakaboylu’s intertwiner remains the most concrete, but even there the positivity of W is not yet proven.

The lemmas stated above are absolutely rigorous, but they are not sufficient. A complete proof of (C4) is still an open problem.
