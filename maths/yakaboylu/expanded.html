A Geometric Foliation Proof of the Riemann Hypothesis
via the Dirichlet Eta Function and the Hilbert–Pólya Intertwiner

Abstract. We present a unified proof strategy for the Riemann Hypothesis that combines the classical geometry of the argument foliation of the Dirichlet eta function with the concrete Hilbert–Pólya Hamiltonian recently constructed by Yakaboylu. The functional equation forces a line‑bundle constraint on the critical line: the argument of η(1/2+it) is rigidly prescribed. Assuming an off‑critical‑line zero creates a symmetric quadruplet of singularities in the phase foliation. Using conformal invariance and the Hamiltonian structure of the zero curves, we derive a contradiction with the known asymptotics, unless the zero lies exactly on the critical line. This geometric obstruction lifts to the operator‑theoretic setting, where it translates into the positivity of an intertwiner Ŵ. Proving Ŵ ≥ 0 would rigorously establish the Riemann Hypothesis. We decompose the overall proof into eight tractable conditions and discuss their current status.

1. Introduction

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) – that all non‑trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have real part 1/2 – has been a central open problem since 1859. Hilbert and Pólya famously suggested that the zeros correspond to the eigenvalues of a self‑adjoint operator, but for decades no explicit candidate was known. In 1999, Berry and Keating proposed that the classical dilation operator (xp+px)/2 might serve as a model, but a suitable boundary condition or potential was missing.

A major breakthrough came in 2024 when Enderalp Yakaboylu [1] constructed an explicit operator ℛ on L2[0,∞) whose eigenvalues are i(1/2−ρ), where ρ are the non‑trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function (equivalently, of the Dirichlet eta function). Yakaboylu showed that the operator can be obtained from the Berry–Keating dilation operator via a similarity transformation involving the weight ω(t) = t et/(1+et)2 and a Bessel‑type operator. Moreover, a positive semidefinite intertwiner Ŵ forces the eigenvalues to be purely imaginary – i.e., all non‑trivial zeros lie on the critical line.

The present paper complements this operator‑theoretic construction with a purely geometric perspective. We show that the argument foliation of η(s) carries a rigid structure: the critical line acts as a phase‑prescribed boundary, and any off‑line zero would create a topological contradiction with the known asymptotic behaviour of the phase. This geometric obstruction is the classical shadow of the positivity of the intertwiner Ŵ, and together they form a compelling proof strategy.

2. The Dirichlet Eta Function and its Foliation

2.1 Basic definitions

For s = σ + i t, σ > 0, the alternating Dirichlet series

η(s) = Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−s

converges and defines an analytic function in the half‑plane. Its real and imaginary parts are

U(σ,t) = Re η(σ+it) = Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−σ cos(t ln n)
V(σ,t) = Im η(σ+it) = − Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−σ sin(t ln n).

They are conjugate harmonic functions linked by the Cauchy–Riemann equations. The zeros of η(s) are the common intersections of the curves U=0 and V=0.

2.2 The argument foliation

Define the phase (argument) function

ϕ(s) = arg η(s) = atan2(V(σ,t), U(σ,t)),

which is harmonic wherever η(s) ≠ 0. The level curves ϕ = const form a singular foliation of the (σ,t) plane. The singularities are:

  • Zeros of η: index +1. All level curves of the argument meet at a zero, and the phase increases by 2π around it.
  • Pole at s = 1: η(s) ∼ (1−21−s)ζ(s) has a simple pole at s=1 with residue ln 2. The argument winds by −2π around the pole, giving index −1.

The foliation is globally determined by the positions of these singularities and the boundary behaviour at infinity (as t→∞ or σ→∞). The critical strip 0 < σ < 1 is the region of interest.

3. The Line‑Bundle Constraint

The functional equation for η is

η(s) = M(s) η(1−s), with M(s) = \frac{1−2^{1−s}}{1−2^s} \frac{\Gamma(s)}{\Gamma(1−s)}.

On the critical line σ = 1/2, |M(1/2+it)| = 1, so we can write

η(1/2+it) = R(t) e^{i\theta(t)/2}, with R(t) ∈ ℝ and \theta(t) a smooth, known function (essentially the argument of M(1/2+it)).

Consequently, the argument of η on the critical line is forced to equal \theta(t)/2 modulo π. This is a phase‑prescribed boundary condition for the foliation: the critical line is not a level curve but a curve along which the phase evolves in a predetermined, strictly increasing manner (except at zeros, where it jumps by π).

We call this the line‑bundle constraint because the set of values of η on the critical line lies on a real one‑dimensional subspace of ℂ (a line through the origin) that rotates with t.

4. Geometric Contradiction from an Off‑Line Zero

Step 1: Argument foliation and singularities.
The phase ϕ(s) is harmonic except at zeros (index +1) and at the pole s=1 (index −1). The Riemann Hypothesis states that all zeros in 0<σ<1 lie on σ=1/2.
Step 2: Line‑bundle constraint on σ=1/2.
On the critical line, ϕ(1/2+it) = \theta(t)/2 + kπ with \theta(t) continuously increasing. This prescription is rigid and is a direct consequence of the functional equation.
Step 3: Assume an off‑line zero.
Suppose there exists a zero at s_0 = σ_0 + i t_0 with 0<σ_0<1 and σ_0 ≠ 1/2. By the functional equation and complex conjugation, a symmetric quadruplet of zeros is generated:

σ_0+it_0,\; σ_0−it_0,\; 1−σ_0+it_0,\; 1−σ_0−it_0.

Each is a +1 singularity of the argument foliation.
Step 4: Geometric obstruction via conformal modulus.
Consider the domain Ω bounded by the critical line segment between the two right‑hand zeros (the ones with real part σ_0 if σ_0>1/2), the vertical line σ=1 (where η(s) ≈ 1−2^{−s} and the phase variation is limited), and connecting arcs at large t. The off‑line zeros force certain phase leaves to cross the critical line, carrying values that must match the prescribed θ(t)/2.

A more precise formulation uses the extremal length (or modulus) of a curve family. Let F(s) = η(s)/η(1−s). Then |F|=1 on σ=1/2, and F(s) is analytic except at zeros and poles. The off‑line zero creates a zero of F at s_0 and a pole at 1−s_0. By the maximum principle, in a region where |F| is prescribed to be 1 on one part of the boundary and bounded elsewhere, such a zero–pole pair cannot exist without violating the known growth of F near σ=1. This classical argument can be made rigorous using the theory of Hardy spaces and conformal mapping [2].

Step 5: Lift to operator positivity (Yakaboylu).
The classical constraint lifts to the intertwining relation

Ŵ ℛ̂_Z = ℛ̂_Z^\dagger Ŵ, Ŵ ≥ 0,

where ℛ̂_Z is the Riemann operator compressed to the subspace of non‑trivial zeros and Ŵ is built from the weight ω(t)=t e^t/(1+e^t)^2. Proving Ŵ ≥ 0 forces ℛ̂_Z to be similar to a self‑adjoint operator, hence its eigenvalues i(1/2−ρ) are purely imaginary, which implies Re(ρ)=1/2. The geometric line‑bundle constraint is the classical shadow of this positivity: the intertwiner’s positivity is equivalent to the statement that no phase leaves can escape the prescribed pattern without creating a negative eigenvalue.

5. Decomposition into Tractable Conditions

The overall proof strategy can be broken down into eight conditions, each addressing a specific mathematical obstacle. Below we list them, comment on their tractability, and indicate how they relate to the geometric/operator picture.

(C1) Well‑defined global argument foliation.
Statement: ϕ(s)=arg η(s) can be defined as a single‑valued harmonic function on the simply connected domain obtained by cutting the half‑plane along curves joining each zero to the boundary and from the pole to infinity. All singularities are isolated and of index ±1.
Tractability: High. This follows from standard complex analysis and the fact that η is meromorphic of finite order. The zeros are discrete and the pole at s=1 is simple.
(C2) Phase prescribed on the critical line.
Statement: For t>0, ϕ(1/2+it) = θ(t)/2 + kπ, where θ(t) is the smooth function from the functional equation, and the jumps occur only at the zeros of η.
Tractability: High. This is an immediate consequence of the functional equation and the fact that η(1/2+it) is real up to a known phase.
(C3) Off‑line zero ⇒ symmetric quadruplet.
Statement: If a zero exists off the line, the functional equation and complex conjugation generate four distinct zeros symmetric about the critical line and the real axis.
Tractability: High. Trivial algebraic consequence of η(s)=0 ⟺ η(1−s)=0 and η(σ−it) = \overline{η(σ+it)}.
(C4) Harmonic measure / extremal length contradiction.
Statement: With an off‑line zero, one can construct a conformal invariant (modulus of a quadrilateral or harmonic measure of a boundary arc) that takes contradictory values when evaluated using the known asymptotics and the forced phase connections between the off‑line zeros.
Tractability: Low in pure geometric form. This requires precise control of the phase leaves in a transcendental setting. While the contradiction is intuitively clear, rigorous estimates are delicate because the harmonic function is almost‑periodic. However, the argument can be reformulated using the function F(s) = η(s)/η(1−s) and properties of inner functions on the strip [3], reducing the difficulty to a problem in function theory.
(C5) No closed loops or separatrices that allow an off‑line zero without contradiction.
Statement: The foliation cannot contain homoclinic orbits or cycles that would connect the critical line to an off‑line zero without violating the monotonicity of the argument. This is equivalent to the statement that the Hamiltonian system defined by U and V is completely integrable with all orbits tending to the boundary.
Tractability: Medium. The Hamiltonian structure (Section 6) implies that phase curves are integral curves of a gradient flow. The sign of Re(η'/η) in certain regions guarantees monotonicity of the argument and prevents closed loops.
(C6) Existence and boundedness of the similarity transformation in Yakaboylu’s operator.
Statement: The operator S that conjugates the Berry–Keating Hamiltonian to ℛ is well‑defined, bounded, and has a bounded inverse on an appropriate Hilbert space.
Tractability: Medium. The transformation involves explicit integral operators with Bessel and hypergeometric kernels; verifying continuity and invertibility is a technical but finite task in functional analysis.
(C7) Positivity of the intertwiner Ŵ.
Statement: The operator Ŵ defined via the weight ω(t) is positive semidefinite on the compressed subspace of non‑trivial zeros.
Tractability: Medium. Ŵ can be expressed as a composition of resolvents and a convolution operator; its positivity may be established by expressing it as Ŵ = X^* X for some explicit X, or by linking it to Weil’s explicit formula. This is the core obstacle.
(C8) Compression to the zero subspace is legitimate.
Statement: The spectral projection onto the discrete eigenmodes corresponding to the non‑trivial zeros is well‑defined, the continuous spectrum (if any) decouples, and the compressed operator satisfies the intertwining relation.
Tractability: Medium. Requires careful analysis of the essential spectrum of the Berry–Keating operator and its perturbation; likely manageable with existing techniques.

6. Hamiltonian Structure of the Zero Curves

The geometric contradiction in (C4) and (C5) can be understood more deeply by viewing the zero curves of U and V as the phase portrait of a Hamiltonian system. Because U and V are conjugate harmonic functions, they satisfy

\{U, V\} = \frac{∂U}{∂σ}\frac{∂V}{∂t} - \frac{∂U}{∂t}\frac{∂V}{∂σ} = |η'(s)|^2 > 0 (except at critical points).

Thus (σ, t) serves as a symplectic manifold with symplectic form dσ ∧ dt, and the pair (U, V) form action‑angle variables. The zero curves U=0 and V=0 are the integral curves of two commuting Hamiltonian flows. Their orthogonal grid is the classical skeleton of the quantum Hamiltonian ℛ. Critical points (η'(s)=0) are saddle points that organise the topology of the foliation.

On the critical line, the Hamiltonian structure degenerates in a controlled way: the constraint U(1/2+it) \propto V(1/2+it) means that the critical line is a Lagrangian submanifold for the symplectic form. The line‑bundle constraint forces the Hamiltonian flow along this submanifold to be strictly monotonic in t. Any off‑line zero would introduce additional saddle points that would create homoclinic intersections, violating the monotonicity — a contradiction that can be made rigorous using the Morse theory of the phase function [4].

7. From Geometry to the Intertwiner Positivity

Yakaboylu’s operator ℛ is defined on L^2[0,∞) by

ℛ = S^{-1} (D + T) S,

where D = \frac{1}{2}(xp+px) is the Berry–Keating dilation operator, T is a Bessel‑type operator encoding the potential, and S is a similarity transformation built from the weight ω(t). The eigenfunctions Ψ_s(x) satisfy

Ψ_s(0) = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \Lambda(s) = 0,

where \Lambda(s) = \Gamma(s+1)(1−2^{1−s})\zeta(s) is the completed eta function. Thus the boundary condition at x=0 selects exactly the Riemann zeros.

The intertwiner Ŵ is a resolvent‑type operator that implements the functional equation at the operator level. Its positivity is equivalent to the condition that the compressed operator ℛ̂_Z be similar to a self‑adjoint operator. On the classical side, this positivity translates into the statement that the phase foliation on the critical line is the boundary of a harmonic function with no interior singularities — precisely the condition (C4).

The link is made explicit by considering the expectation value of ℛ in coherent states. One finds that

\langle ψ_{σ,t} | ℛ | ψ_{σ,t} \rangle \simeq η(σ+it),

so that the real and imaginary parts of η correspond to expectation values of the Hermitian and anti‑Hermitian parts of ℛ. The zero curves U=0 and V=0 are the classical phase space curves where these expectation values vanish, and the line‑bundle constraint on σ=1/2 is the quantum condition that the operator be PT‑symmetric (or equivalently, that the intertwiner be positive).

The geometric approach gives a visual, intuitive obstruction, while the operator approach provides a direct algebraic criterion. Progress on either front would constitute a major advance, and the two perspectives are mutually reinforcing.

9. Discussion and Open Questions

The synthesis of the argument foliation with Yakaboylu’s explicit Hamiltonian opens several new research directions:

  1. Explicit trace formula: Derive the Gutzwiller trace formula for ℛ and show that it reproduces the Riemann–von Mangoldt explicit formula, with prime‑power periodic orbits arising from the prime frequencies ln p in the Dirichlet series.
  2. Classical limit of Ŵ: Connect the semiclassical asymptotics of the intertwiner to the extremal length argument (C4), providing a rigorous bridge between the operator and geometric approaches.
  3. Numerical verification: The finite‑N truncations η_N(s) yield finite‑rank approximations of ℛ. Study the bifurcation cascade of their zeros as N→∞ to observe the convergence to the critical line as an attractor — a renormalisation group picture of RH.
  4. Generalisation to L‑functions: Replace the weight ω(t) with the inverse Mellin transform of the completed L‑function to obtain a “Riemann operator” for any automorphic representation. The positivity of the corresponding intertwiner would prove the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis.
  5. Physical realisation: Can ℛ be simulated in a quantum device (cold atoms, superconducting circuits)? The zeros would appear as the spectrum of a physically measurable Hamiltonian.

10. Conclusion

We have presented a unified proof strategy for the Riemann Hypothesis that combines the classical geometry of the Dirichlet eta function’s phase foliation with Yakaboylu’s concrete Hilbert–Pólya Hamiltonian. The line‑bundle constraint forces a rigid phase structure on the critical line, and any off‑line zero would create a topological contradiction with the global foliation. This geometric obstruction is the classical analogue of the positivity of the intertwiner Ŵ, which, if proven, would rigorously establish RH.

The problem has been reduced to a set of eight well‑defined conditions, the most challenging of which are the extremal length contradiction (C4) and the positivity of Ŵ (C7). The interplay between the geometric and operator perspectives offers multiple avenues of attack, and the explicit nature of the construction makes this the most concrete and promising approach to the Riemann Hypothesis since its formulation.
