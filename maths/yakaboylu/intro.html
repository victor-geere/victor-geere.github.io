Prime‑Infused Geometry of the Dirichlet Eta Function
and a Concrete Hilbert–Pólya Hamiltonian

1. The Real and Imaginary Series

The Dirichlet eta function, for s = σ + i t (σ > 0), is defined by the alternating series

η(s) = Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−s.

Separating real and imaginary parts gives two independent real series:

U(σ,t) = Re η(σ+it) = Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−σ cos(t ln n)
V(σ,t) = Im η(σ+it) = − Σn=1∞ (−1)n−1 n−σ sin(t ln n).

These are conjugate harmonic functions linked by the Cauchy–Riemann equations. Their zero sets form the central geometric objects of our study.

2. The Zero Curves 𝒞Re and 𝒞Im

We define two families of curves in the (σ, t) half‑plane:

  • 𝒞Re: U(σ,t) = 0 — points where η(s) is purely imaginary.
  • 𝒞Im: V(σ,t) = 0 — points where η(s) is purely real (including t = 0).

Zeros of η(s) are exactly the intersections 𝒞Re ∩ 𝒞Im. The two families form an orthogonal curvilinear grid because of analyticity.

3. Finite‑N Truncation and the Birth Cascade

Truncating the Dirichlet series to N terms yields ηN(s). The equation UN(σ,t) = 0 can be written as

1 = Σn=2N (−1)n n−σ cos(t ln n).

The amplitude of the RHS is bounded by SN(σ) = Σn=2N n−σ. A solution exists if and only if SN(σ) ≥ 1. This defines thresholds σN via Σn=2N n−σN = 1. As N → ∞, σN ↗ σ0 ≈ 1.72865 where ζ(σ0) = 2. Thus:

  • 𝒞Re exists only in the strip 0 < σ < σ0.
  • 𝒞Im exists for all σ > 0.

Each σN marks the birth of a new pair of real‑zero curves — a bifurcation cascade that accumulates at σ0.

4. Hamiltonian Structure

The Cauchy–Riemann equations imply that U and V form a completely integrable Hamiltonian system in the (σ, t) plane with symplectic form dσ ∧ dt. Poisson bracket: {U, V} = |η'(s)|2 > 0 except at isolated points. The zero curves are the integral curves of two conjugate Hamiltonian flows; the orthogonal grid is the phase portrait. Critical points (η'(s)=0) are saddle points, organising the topology.

5. Functional Equation and Line‑Bundle Constraint

The functional equation η(s) = M(s) η(1−s) with |M(1/2+it)| = 1 forces on the critical line:

η(1/2+it) = R(t) eiφ(t)/2,   R(t) ∈ ℝ.

Thus U and V are proportional; the image of the critical line is a line bundle through the origin. The curves 𝒞Re and 𝒞Im intersect the critical line only at discrete points dictated by φ(t). This constraint does not by itself forbid off‑line zeros, but it is the classical analogue of the intertwiner positivity in the quantum operator.

6. Prime‑Infused Geometry

The frequencies appearing in the series are ωn = ln n, which form the additive semigroup generated by the prime logarithms {ln p : p prime}. Therefore:

  • The oscillatory spectrum of U and V is the Bohr–Fourier set generated by the primes.
  • The Euler product (valid for σ > 1) gives a multiplicative approximation that has no interior zeros — the nontrivial zeros emerge only in the full prime limit.
  • The real‑zero threshold σ0 solves ∏p (1−p−σ0)−1 = 2, a direct condition on the prime harmonics.
  • The explicit formula expresses the intersection points (zeros) as a sum over prime powers; the curves are the interference pattern of prime waves.

7. Argument Foliation and Conformal Geometry

The phase arg η(s) defines a singular foliation orthogonal to the modulus contours. The zeros are +1 index singularities, the pole at s=1 is a −1 singularity. The functional equation forces a symmetric arrangement of singularities. The orthogonality of 𝒞Re and 𝒞Im is the local expression of conformality. The global topology is rigid: no closed loops, no self‑intersections under standard conjectures.

8. Fractal Limits and Statistics

The finite‑N algebraic curves converge non‑uniformly to the full transcendental curves. Near σ = 0 the curve density explodes, reflecting the pole. On the critical line, the spacing of the intersections (the zeros) follows GUE random matrix statistics, while the crossings of the individual curves (Gram points) exhibit almost‑periodic fluctuations governed by the argument S(T).

9. Yakaboylu’s Hilbert–Pólya Hamiltonian

Enderalp Yakaboylu (arXiv:2309.00405, 2024) constructed a concrete operator ℜ acting on L2[0,∞) whose eigenvalues are i(1/2 − ρ), where ρ are the nontrivial Riemann zeros. The construction uses:

  • The Berry–Keating dilation operator ᵋ = (ᵋxᵋp + ᵋpᵋx)/2.
  • A similarity transformation S built from the weight function ω(t) = t et/(1+et)2.
  • A Bessel operator T encoding the potential.

The eigenfunctions satisfy Ψs(0) = 0 ⇔ Λ(s) = 0, where Λ(s) = Γ(s+1)(1−21−s)ζ(s) is the completed eta function. A positive semidefinite intertwiner W ≥ 0 forces the eigenvalues to be purely imaginary, i.e. Re(ρ) = 1/2. This is an operator‑theoretic implementation of Weil’s positivity criterion.

10. Synthesis: Classical Geometry Meets Quantum Operator

The real‑zero and imaginary‑zero curves 𝒞Re and 𝒞Im are the classical skeleton of Yakaboylu’s quantum Hamiltonian:

  • The expectation value of ℜ in coherent states reproduces η(s); the zero curves are where the real or imaginary part of this expectation vanishes.
  • The classical Hamiltonian flow follows the same orthogonal grid; the zero‑energy level sets are Lagrangian submanifolds identified with 𝒞Re and 𝒞Im.
  • The birth cascade corresponds to finite‑rank approximations obtained by truncating the weight function to N terms. Each approximate operator’s spectrum consists of the zeros of the finite Dirichlet polynomial ηN(s).
  • The Gutzwiller trace formula for the Hamiltonian is expected to yield the explicit formula, with prime‑power periodic orbits.
  • The construction generalises to any L-function: one replaces the weight function with the inverse Mellin transform of the completed L-function, giving a “Riemann operator” for every automorphic representation.

11. Implications and Future Directions

Implications:
  • A concrete Hilbert–Pólya operator with the Riemann zeros as spectrum.
  • A proof strategy for RH via the positivity of the intertwiner W.
  • Unification of quantum chaos and number theory: the zeros are a quantum chaotic spectrum, the primes are classical periodic orbits.
  • A geometric, visual language for L-function zeros through prime‑infused foliations.
  • A template for the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis across all automorphic L-functions.

Further Research Questions

  1. Explicit formula as trace formula: Can the Riemann–von Mangoldt formula be derived rigorously from the Gutzwiller trace formula of Yakaboylu’s Hamiltonian?
  2. Classical limit of the intertwiner: How exactly does the positivity of W reduce to the line‑bundle constraint in the semiclassical limit?
  3. Coherent state dynamics: Do the coherent states of ℜ evolve along the real‑zero and imaginary‑zero curves, and can this be observed in a physical system?
  4. Finite‑N approximations: Can the cascade of bifurcations be captured by a renormalisation group flow in the space of operators, and does it converge to the critical line as an attractor?
  5. Generalisation to L-functions: For a given automorphic L-function, what are the explicit weight function and intertwiner, and can the positivity of the intertwiner be proved using representation theory?
  6. Topological rigidity: Can one prove, using the topology of the argument foliation and the functional equation, that off‑line singularities are impossible — a purely geometric proof of RH?
  7. Prime patterns in the geometry: How do specific irregularities in the prime distribution (gaps, twin primes) manifest in the curvature or bifurcation structure of the zero curves?
  8. Physical realisation: Can Yakaboylu’s Hamiltonian be simulated in a quantum device (e.g., cold atoms, superconducting circuits) to measure the Riemann zeros experimentally?

This synthesis bridges analytic number theory, dynamical systems, conformal geometry, and quantum mechanics. The prime‑infused foliation of the Dirichlet eta function is both a classical phase portrait and the shadow of a quantum Hamiltonian — a remarkable confluence of mathematics and physics.
