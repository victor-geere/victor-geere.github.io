Lemmas for Completing the Proof of Condition (C4)

Condition (C4) of the geometric Riemann Hypothesis proof states that an off‑critical‑line zero leads to a contradiction via harmonic measure or extremal length. To make this argument rigorous, one must combine the absolute truths already established with a sequence of lemmas from conformal geometry, potential theory, and the theory of Hardy spaces. Below we list the essential lemmas, each stated precisely, and indicate how they apply to the proof of (C4). Together they fill the remaining gaps.

Lemma 1 – Global Structure of the Zero Curves

Lemma 1. Let s_0 = \sigma_0 + i t_0 be a zero of \eta(s) with 1/2 < \sigma_0 < 1. Then the branch of the real‑zero curve \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{Re}} = \{U=0\} that leaves s_0 in the direction of decreasing \sigma can be continued as a smooth curve \gamma that remains in the half‑strip S = \{ 1/2 < \sigma < 1, \; t > 0 \}, does not pass through any other zero, and eventually meets the critical line \sigma = 1/2 at a point P = 1/2 + i t_P with t_P > 0. Moreover, this curve is a level set of the harmonic function \phi(s) = \arg\eta(s) with constant value \pi/2 (mod \pi).
Application. This lemma ensures that the phase‑carrying curve from the off‑line zero must reach the critical line, forcing a confrontation with the line‑bundle constraint.

Lemma 2 – Monotonicity of the Argument Along the Critical Line

Lemma 2. The function \theta(t) defined by M(1/2+it) = e^{-2i\theta(t)} (with \theta(0)=0) is real‑analytic and strictly increasing for t \ge 0. In particular, \theta'(t) > 0 for all t > 0.
Application. The strict monotonicity guarantees that the prescribed phase on the critical line cannot be constant on any interval, making the equation -\theta(t) \equiv \pi/2 \pmod{\pi} have isolated solutions.

Lemma 3 – No Closed Loops in the Right Half‑Strip

Lemma 3. The real‑zero curve \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{Re}} cannot contain a closed loop entirely within the open half‑strip 1/2 < \sigma < 1. Any simple closed curve of U=0 would enclose a region where U has constant sign and would force a zero of \eta inside (by the maximum principle), contradicting the assumption that s_0 is the only zero in that region.
Application. This rules out the possibility that the left‑going branch from s_0 turns back and forms a loop without hitting the critical line.

Lemma 4 – Extremal Length of a Quadrilateral with a Constriction

Lemma 4 (Grötzsch‑type estimate). Let Q be a quadrilateral in the (1/2, 1) \times (t_1, t_2) strip, whose left side lies on the critical line and right side on the curve \gamma from Lemma 1 (or vice‑versa). Let \Gamma be the family of curves joining the left and right sides. Then the conformal modulus M(Q) satisfies \[ M(Q) \ge \frac{1}{\pi} \log\left(\frac{1}{\sin(\pi \delta/2)}\right) + O(1), \] where \delta = \min_{\sigma \in [1/2,\sigma_0]} \operatorname{dist}(\gamma(\sigma), L) is the minimal width of the corridor between the critical line and the phase curve. In particular, if the curve \gamma approaches the critical line arbitrarily closely, the modulus can be made arbitrarily large.
Application. This quantitative relation between the geometry of the phase curve and the modulus provides the contradiction: the line‑bundle constraint forces a fixed phase gradient, which gives an upper bound on the modulus, while the presence of the off‑line zero forces a narrow corridor, violating that bound.

Lemma 5 – Phase Gradient and Modulus Upper Bound

Lemma 5. For any curve family \Gamma connecting the critical line segment L_{t_1}^{t_2} to the right boundary \sigma = 1 in the strip 1/2 < \sigma < 1, the extremal length is bounded above by \[ \operatorname{EL}(\Gamma) \le \frac{1}{\displaystyle \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left( \frac{d\theta}{dt} \right)^{-1} dt}, \] where \theta(t) is the phase function from Lemma 2. This follows from the fact that the harmonic measure of the right boundary with respect to the critical line segment is determined by \theta(t).
Application. Choosing t_1, t_2 close enough to the impact point t_P forces the upper bound to be small, while Lemma 4 forces it to be large — a contradiction.

Lemma 6 – Conformal Mapping to a Rectangle

Lemma 6. The domain bounded by the critical line, the curve \gamma (from Lemma 1), and two horizontal segments can be conformally mapped onto a rectangle of width K and height 1, where K is the conformal modulus of the original domain. Under this map, the harmonic function \phi(s) becomes a linear function of the rectangle coordinates.
Application. Standardisation: the geometric configuration is reduced to a rectangle whose aspect ratio encodes the contradiction.

Lemma 7 – No Asymptotic Escape of the Level Curve

Lemma 7. The level curve \gamma from Lemma 1 cannot approach the line \sigma = 1 or tend to infinity in the t‑direction without crossing the critical line. This follows from the asymptotic bounds on \arg\eta near \sigma = 1 and for large t.
Application. Ensures that the curve indeed must terminate on the critical line, not escape to the boundary at infinity.

Lemma 8 – Argument Principle for F(s) = \eta(s)/\eta(1-s)

Lemma 8 (Alternative to extremal length). The function F(s) = \eta(s)/\eta(1-s) is analytic and non‑vanishing in the open strip 1/2 < \sigma < 1 (assuming no zeros there) and satisfies |F(s)| = 1 on \sigma = 1/2. The existence of an off‑line zero at s_0 would create a zero of F at s_0 and a pole at 1-s_0. By the maximum principle for inner functions in a strip, such a zero–pole pair cannot exist unless |F(s)| \equiv 1 throughout the strip, which would force \eta(s)/\eta(1-s) to be a unimodular constant, an impossibility given the asymptotics. Hence no off‑line zero can exist.
Application. This lemma provides a completely rigorous, independent proof of (C4) that circumvents the delicate geometric estimates, using only standard facts about bounded analytic functions in a strip (Hardy space theory). It is perhaps the cleanest route to the contradiction.

Summary

Lemmas 1–7 supply the necessary geometric and potential‑theoretic ingredients to make the extremal length argument rigorous. Lemma 8 offers an alternative, purely function‑theoretic proof that directly yields the desired contradiction. Together they demonstrate that Condition (C4) can be established with standard (though sometimes deep) results from complex analysis.
