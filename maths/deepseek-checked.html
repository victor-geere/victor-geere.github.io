PART 1 Greedy Harmonic Decomposition
Definition 2.1 — Harmonic Angles
\[\alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{n+2}, \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \dots\]

The harmonic angles satisfy \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha_n = \infty\) and \(\alpha_n \to 0\). The series of harmonic angles diverges logarithmically: \(\sum_{n=0}^N \alpha_n = \pi (H_{N+2} - 1)\), where \(H_k = \sum_{j=1}^k 1/j\) is the \(k\)-th harmonic number.

Definition 2.2 — Greedy Selection Algorithm

For a target angle \(\theta \in [0, \pi]\), initialize \(\theta_0 = 0\). For \(n = 0, 1, 2, \dots\) define:

\[\delta_n(\theta) = \Theta(\theta - \theta_n - \alpha_n) \in \{0, 1\}, \qquad \theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + \delta_n(\theta) \alpha_n\]

where \(\Theta\) is the Heaviside step function. The algorithm selects \(\alpha_n\) if it does not cause the accumulated angle to exceed \(\theta\).

The sine reconstruction is: \(s_0 = 0, c_0 = 1\), and

\[s_{n+1} = \begin{cases} \sin(\theta_n)\cos(\alpha_n) + \cos(\theta_n)\sin(\alpha_n), & \text{if } \delta_n = 1,\\ s_n, & \text{if } \delta_n = 0, \end{cases}\] \[c_{n+1} = \begin{cases} \cos(\theta_n)\cos(\alpha_n) - \sin(\theta_n)\sin(\alpha_n), & \text{if } \delta_n = 1,\\ c_n, & \text{if } \delta_n = 0. \end{cases}\]
Theorem 2.4 — Harmonic Reconstruction of Sine
\[\sin(\theta) = \lim_{N\to\infty} s_N, \qquad |\sin(\theta) - s_N| \leq \frac{\pi}{N+2}\]

Convergence rate is \(O(1/N)\). The algorithm is "greedy" because at each step it takes the largest available harmonic fraction that does not exceed the remaining angle.

Lemma 3.3 — Selection Density
\[D(N, \theta) := \sum_{n=0}^N \delta_n(\theta) = \frac{\theta}{\pi} \ln(N+2) + O(1)\]
PART 2 Correlation Structure & Gram Matrix
Definition 4.2 — Threshold Angles

By selection monotonicity (Lemma 4.1), there exist unique thresholds \(\theta_n^* \in [0, \pi]\) such that:

\[\delta_n(\theta) = \begin{cases} 0, & \theta < \theta_n^*, \\ 1, & \theta \geq \theta_n^*. \end{cases}\]

The thresholds satisfy the self-consistency equation: \(\theta_n^* = \alpha_n + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \delta_k(\theta_n^*) \alpha_k\).

First few: \(\theta_0^* = \pi/2\), \(\theta_1^* \approx 1.879\), \(\theta_2^* \approx 2.461\).

Definition 4.4 — Correlation Kernel
\[K(n, m) = \int_0^\pi \left[\delta_n(\theta) - \frac{\theta}{\pi}\right] \left[\delta_m(\theta) - \frac{\theta}{\pi}\right] d\theta\]

The centring by \(\theta/\pi\) removes the mean, making the kernel a measure of correlation between selection indicators.

Lemma 4.6 — Diagonal Kernel
\[K(n, n) = \frac{(\theta_n^*)^3 + (\pi - \theta_n^*)^3}{3\pi^2}\]
Off-Diagonal Kernel (claimed in referenced documents)

The documents claim a closed-form expression for off-diagonal entries:

\[K(n, m) = 4p_n p_m - a_n - a_m \quad (n \neq m)\]

where \(p_n = \ell_n/4\) and \(a_n\) are "explicit moments given in ghd-inversion.html". However, the derivation of this formula is not provided in the public documents, and the definitions of \(p_n\), \(a_n\) remain unclear from the available materials.

PART 3 Harmonic Sine Transform (HST)
Haar Basis from Greedy Tree

The greedy harmonic tree generates a collection of cylinder intervals \(I_\varepsilon\) indexed by binary sequences \(\varepsilon = (\varepsilon_0, \dots, \varepsilon_{n-1})\). For each split node \(\varepsilon\), define the Haar function:

\[\psi_\varepsilon(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_n}} \mathbf{1}_{I_{\varepsilon 1}}(\theta) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{\ell(\varepsilon) - \alpha_n}} \mathbf{1}_{I_{\varepsilon 0}}(\theta)\]

Together with the constant function \(\psi_\emptyset(\theta) = 1/\sqrt{\pi}\), the set \(\mathcal{B} = \{\psi_\emptyset\} \cup \{\psi_\varepsilon : \varepsilon \text{ split node}\}\) forms an orthonormal basis of \(L^2[0, \pi]\).

Harmonic Sine Transform

For \(f \in L^2[0, \pi]\), define \(\mathcal{H}f(\psi) = \langle f, \psi \rangle_{L^2}\) for \(\psi \in \mathcal{B}\). The map \(\mathcal{H}: L^2[0, \pi] \to \ell^2(\mathcal{B})\) is an isometric isomorphism.

\[f(\theta) = \sum_{\psi \in \mathcal{B}} \mathcal{H}f(\psi) \psi(\theta)\]
Greedy Harmonic Zeta Function

For fixed \(\theta\), the Dirichlet series weighted by selection indicators:

\[\Phi(\theta, s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}, \qquad \operatorname{Re} s > 1\]

Boundary values: \(\Phi(0, s) = 0\), \(\Phi(\pi, s) = \zeta(s) - 1 - \Omega(\pi, s)\) where \(\Omega(\pi, s) = \sum_{n: \delta_n(\pi) = 0} (n+2)^{-s}\).

Important note: The claim that \(\Phi(\pi, s) = \zeta(s) - 1\) is not exactly true; there are indices for which \(\delta_n(\pi) = 0\) (as noted in Remark 3.3 of the eta paper). The correction term \(\Omega(\pi, s)\) must be accounted for.

PART 4 Transfer Operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\)
Dynamical System

Let \(x = \theta/\pi \in [0, 1]\). Define the remainder \(r_n = x - \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{\delta_k(\theta)}{k+2}\). The scaled remainder \(\tilde{r}_n = (n+2) r_n\) evolves under the map:

\[T(y) = \begin{cases} \frac{n+2}{n+1} y, & y \in \left(0, \frac{1}{n+2}\right], \\[6pt] \frac{n+2}{n+1} \left(y - \frac{1}{n+2}\right), & y \in \left(\frac{1}{n+2}, \frac{1}{n+1}\right]. \end{cases}\]

\(T\) is uniformly expanding and admits a countable Markov partition. It is conjugate to a piece of the Gauss map.

Transfer Operator

For functions on the unit interval, the transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) (acting on a suitable Hardy space \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\)) is:

\[\mathcal{L}_s f(y) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{n+1}{(n+2)^{s+1}} \left[ f\left(\frac{n+1}{n+2} y\right) + f\left(\frac{n+1}{n+2} y + \frac{1}{n+2}\right) \right]\]

For \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1\), \(\mathcal{L}_s\) is trace-class. It admits a meromorphic continuation to \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1/2\).

Resolvent Representation (claimed)
\[\Phi(\theta, s) = x s^{-1} + \langle (I - \mathcal{L}_s)^{-1} \mathcal{L}_s \mathbf{1}_{[1, \infty)} \rangle(y_0)\]

where \(y_0 = 2x\) and \(\mathbf{1}_{[1, \infty)}\) is a test function. This provides the meromorphic continuation of \(\Phi(\theta, s)\).

PART 5 Fredholm Determinant & Zeta Connection
Claim — Fredholm Determinant Identity
\[\Delta(s) := \det\nolimits_{(1)}(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = \frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)} e^{P(s)}\]

where \(P(s)\) is claimed to be an entire function, zero-free for \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1/2\). This is stated to follow from "standard thermodynamic formalism for maps conjugate to the Gauss map" (citing Mayer and Efrat).

Corollary (claimed) — Riemann Zeros as Eigenvalue Parameters
\[\det\nolimits_{(1)}(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = 0 \iff \zeta(s) = 0 \quad \text{(non-trivial zeros)}\]

For every non-trivial zero \(\rho\) of \(\zeta(s)\), there exists \(\psi_\rho \in \mathcal{H}\) such that \(\mathcal{L}_\rho \psi_\rho = \psi_\rho\).

Claim — Symmetry on Critical Line

From the explicit formula (6), one verifies that \(\mathcal{L}_s^* = \mathcal{L}_{\bar{s}}\) with respect to the inner product weighted by the invariant measure. Consequently, for \(s = 1/2 + it\), \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\) is self-adjoint in the weighted inner product, completing the Hilbert–Pólya vision.

⚠️ Critical Gap — Gram Matrix Unitary Equivalence

The Addendum claims that \(\mathcal{H} \circ \mathcal{L}_s \circ \mathcal{H}^{-1} = M(s) = D(s) K D(s)\), where \(D(s) = \operatorname{diag}((n+1/2)^{-s})\) and \(K\) is the Gram matrix. However:

  • The definition of the Gram matrix \(K\) in this context is not consistent with the correlation kernel \(K(n,m)\) defined in the decomposition paper. The dimensions and indexing are unclear.
  • The "direct computation" of matrix elements for \(n=m=1\) and \(n=2, m=1\) is stated without any actual integrals, measures, or weights being specified.
  • The unitary equivalence between a transfer operator on a Hardy space and a Gram matrix on \(\ell^2\) requires explicit identification of the HST basis with the eigenfunctions of \(\mathcal{L}_s\). This identification is not carried out in the available documents.
  • The claim that the Gram matrix has a rank-2 correction structure that leads to the factorisation \((1-\alpha(s))(1-\beta(s))\) is unsubstantiated.
⚠️ Critical Gap — Infinite Product Identification

The Addendum claims:

\[\prod_{n=1}^\infty (1 - \ell_n w_n(s)^2) = \zeta(2s)^{-1} \cdot C + O(s^{-3/2})\]

But:

  • The greedy weights \(w_n(s)\) are never defined in any of the available documents.
  • The lengths \(\ell_n\) are presumably related to the cylinder intervals, but no explicit formula connecting them to the Euler product for \(\zeta(2s)\) is derived.
  • The Euler product \(\zeta(2s)^{-1} = \prod_p (1 - p^{-2s})\) involves primes, while the greedy harmonic decomposition involves the integers \(n+2\). A rigorous connection between these two products is a major unsolved step.
⚠️ Critical Gap — Zero-Freeness Argument

The Addendum argues that \(e^{P(s)}\) is zero-free for \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1/2\) because \(\alpha(s)\) and \(\beta(s)\) are positive for real \(s > 1/2\). This reasoning is mathematically invalid:

  • Positivity on the real line does not imply non-vanishing off the real line. A function can be strictly positive on \(\mathbb{R}_{>1/2}\) yet have complex zeros. Example: \(f(s) = 1 + (s - 2)^2\) is positive on \(\mathbb{R}\) but vanishes at \(s = 2 \pm i\).
  • The argument attempts to use "continuity and compactness on compact subsets" to extend positivity to the complex domain, but this is not how analytic continuation works.
  • To prove zero-freeness, one would need bounds on \(|\alpha(s)|\) and \(|\beta(s)|\) in the half-plane (e.g., using maximum modulus principle or Rouché's theorem), which are not provided.
⚠️ Critical Gap — Meromorphic Continuation

The claim that the Fredholm determinant identity provides a meromorphic continuation of \(\zeta(s)/\zeta(2s) e^{P(s)}\) to \(\mathbb{C}\) is stated without proof. The entire function \(P(s)\) is not explicitly constructed, nor are its growth properties established. Without this, the analytic continuation of the determinant cannot be verified.

PART 6 HST Explicit Formula
Theorem 4.1 — HST Explicit Formula (claimed)

For a test function \(\phi: (0, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}\) with Mellin transform \(\hat{\phi}(s) = \int_0^\infty \phi(u) e^{(s-1)u} du\), and for suitable \(f \in L^2[0, \pi]\):

\[S_f(\phi) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \phi(\log(n+2)) = \bar{f}_1 \hat{\phi}(1) + \sum_{\rho} \hat{\phi}(\rho) \frac{c_f(\rho)}{\zeta'(\rho)} + \text{trivial zero terms}\]

where \(a_n = \int_0^\pi f(\theta) (\delta_n(\theta) - \theta/\pi) d\theta\), \(\bar{f}_1 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^\pi f(\theta) \theta d\theta\), and \(c_f(\rho)\) are coefficients related to the HST of \(f\) at the zero \(\rho\).

This is an explicit formula analogous to the Weil explicit formula, with the zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) appearing as frequencies in the spectral decomposition.

PART 7 Honest Assessment
What Has Been Rigorously Established
  • Greedy Harmonic Decomposition: The algorithm, its convergence rate \(O(1/N)\), and the selection density \(D(N, \theta) = (\theta/\pi) \ln(N+2) + O(1)\) are rigorously proved in the decomposition paper. The monotonicity of \(\delta_n(\theta)\) and existence of threshold angles \(\theta_n^*\) are established.
  • Correlation Kernel: The diagonal kernel formula \(K(n,n) = ((\theta_n^*)^3 + (\pi - \theta_n^*)^3) / (3\pi^2)\) is derived. The off-diagonal expression is claimed but not derived in the available documents.
  • HST Basis: The construction of an orthonormal Haar basis from the greedy tree is described, though completeness is only sketched.
  • Transfer Operator: The definition of \(\mathcal{L}_s\) and its connection to a dynamical system are presented. The operator is explicitly defined.
  • Greedy Dirichlet Sub-sum: The series \(E(\theta, s) = \sum \delta_n(\theta) (n+2)^{-s}\) and its boundary values are studied. The connection to the eta function via signed sub-sums is explored.
What Remains Unproven (Honest Assessment)
  • Unitary equivalence between \(\mathcal{L}_s\) and the Gram matrix \(M(s)\) is not demonstrated. The "direct computation" is not provided in any public document.
  • The Fredholm determinant identity \(\det(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = \zeta(s)/\zeta(2s) e^{P(s)}\) is stated as following from "standard thermodynamic formalism," but no derivation specific to this operator is given.
  • The infinite product identification with the Euler product for \(\zeta(2s)^{-1}\) is a major leap. The greedy weights are not defined, and no connection between the greedy partition and the primes is established.
  • The rank-2 correction structure of the Gram matrix that leads to the factorisation \((1-\alpha(s))(1-\beta(s))\) is not derived.
  • Zero-freeness of \(e^{P(s)}\) is argued using invalid reasoning (real positivity does not imply complex zero-freeness).
  • The entire function \(P(s)\) is never explicitly constructed, nor are its growth properties established.
  • The explicit formula (Theorem 4.1 of the HST paper) is stated without derivation of the coefficients \(c_f(\rho)\).
  • Self-adjointness on the critical line is claimed but the weighted inner product and the invariant measure are not fully specified.
Conclusion

The Greedy Harmonic Framework contains interesting and non-trivial mathematical constructions: a novel greedy algorithm for sine reconstruction, a correlation kernel with explicit diagonal entries, a transfer operator associated with a dynamical system, and an explicit formula that resembles the Weil explicit formula. However, the central claims connecting this framework to the Riemann zeta function—specifically the Fredholm determinant identity, the unitary equivalence, and the zero-freeness argument—remain unproven in the available documentation. The gaps identified are not merely technical details but involve fundamental steps that would need to be filled before the framework could be considered a viable approach to the Riemann Hypothesis.
