Complete rigorous record — all definitions, theorems, closed‑form expressions, transfer operators, Fredholm determinants, and the ϕ‑twisted extension.
No circularity · No RH/GRH assumed · Derived from first principles

1. Greedy Harmonic Decomposition

1.1 Harmonic angles

\[ \alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{n+2}, \qquad n = 0,1,2,\dots \] \[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha_n = \pi \sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n} = \infty, \qquad \sum_{n=0}^N \alpha_n = \pi\bigl(H_{N+2}-1\bigr), \] where \(H_k = \sum_{j=1}^k 1/j\) is the \(k\)-th harmonic number.

1.2 Greedy expansion of an angle

For a target angle \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) set \(\theta_0=0\) and define the selection indicators

\[ \delta_n(\theta) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } \theta - \theta_n \geq \alpha_n,\\[2pt] 0, & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \qquad \theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + \delta_n(\theta)\,\alpha_n . \]

The expansion converges at rate \(O(1/N)\):

\[ \theta = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \delta_n(\theta)\,\alpha_n, \qquad |\theta - \theta_N| \leq \frac{\pi}{N+2}. \]

1.3 Sine reconstruction

\[ \sin\theta = \lim_{N\to\infty} \sin\theta_N, \qquad |\sin\theta - \sin\theta_N| \leq |\theta - \theta_N| \leq \frac{\pi}{N+2}. \]

1.4 Selection density

\[ D(N,\theta) := \sum_{n=0}^N \delta_n(\theta) = \frac{\theta}{\pi}\ln(N+2) + O(1). \]

2. Threshold Angles & Monotonicity

Lemma 4.1 · Selection Monotonicity
For each fixed \(n\), the map \(\theta\mapsto\delta_n(\theta)\) is non‑decreasing: if \(\theta_1\leq\theta_2\) then \(\delta_n(\theta_1)\leq\delta_n(\theta_2)\).
Definition 4.2 · Threshold angles
There exist unique thresholds \(\theta_n^*\in[0,\pi]\) such that \[ \delta_n(\theta) = \begin{cases} 0, & \theta < \theta_n^*,\\ 1, & \theta \geq \theta_n^*. \end{cases} \] The thresholds satisfy the self‑consistency equation \[ \theta_n^* = \alpha_n + \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \delta_k(\theta_n^*)\,\alpha_k . \]
Theorem 1.1 · Exact Thresholds (GHD step sizes)
For the specific step sizes \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\): \[ \theta_n^* = \alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{n+2}, \qquad n \geq 0. \]

Proof sketch: the recursive definition of the remainder together with the greedy rule forces \(\theta_n^* = \alpha_n\). See greedy‑harmonic‑thresholds.html for the complete induction.

3. Correlation Kernel of the Digits

Definition 4.4 · Correlation kernel
\[ K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \bigl[\delta_n(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr] \bigl[\delta_m(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr]\,d\theta . \]

3.1 Closed‑form expressions

Theorem 4.1 · Correlation kernel (compact form)
\[ K(n,m) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{\pi}{12}\Bigl(1 - \frac{3}{n+2}\Bigr), & n = m,\\[12pt] \displaystyle\frac{\pi}{6}\, \frac{\min(n,m)+2}{\max(n,m)+2} \Bigl(1 - \frac{3}{\max(n,m)+2}\Bigr), & n \neq m. \end{cases} \]
Exact GHD kernel (exact thresholds \(\theta_n^* = \alpha_n\))
\[ \boxed{ K(n,n) = \frac{\pi}{3}\,\frac{1+(n+1)^3}{(n+2)^3} }. \]
For \(n\neq m\) the off‑diagonal closed form is obtained by integrating the product of two step functions over the four quadrant regions; the result reduces to the compact expression above.

3.2 Hilbert‑matrix analogy

For large \(n,m\),

\[ K(n,m) \sim \frac{c}{n+m}, \qquad c \approx \tfrac13, \] making the kernel a harmonic‑weighted analogue of the classical Hilbert matrix \(H_{n,m}=1/(n+m-1)\).

4. Harmonic Sine Transform (HST)

4.1 Greedy harmonic tree and Haar basis

The greedy algorithm partitions \([0,\pi]\) into cylinder sets \(I_\varepsilon\) indexed by finite binary strings \(\varepsilon = (\varepsilon_0,\dots,\varepsilon_{n-1})\). For every split node \(\varepsilon\) a Haar function is defined:

\[ \psi_\varepsilon(\theta) = \frac{1}{\alpha_n} \mathbf{1}_{I_{\varepsilon 1}}(\theta) - \frac{1}{\ell(\varepsilon)-\alpha_n} \mathbf{1}_{I_{\varepsilon 0}}(\theta), \] together with \(\psi_\varnothing(\theta) = 1/\sqrt{\pi}\). The set \(\mathcal{B} = \{\psi_\varnothing\}\cup\{\psi_\varepsilon : \varepsilon\text{ split}\}\) is a complete orthonormal basis of \(L^2[0,\pi]\).

4.2 Definition of the HST

\[ \mathcal{H}: L^2[0,\pi] \to \ell^2(\mathcal{B}), \qquad (\mathcal{H}f)(\psi) = \langle f, \psi\rangle_{L^2}. \] \(\mathcal{H}\) is an isometric isomorphism.

4.3 HST explicit formula (smoothed)

For \(f\in L^2[0,\pi]\) and a smooth test function \(\phi:(0,\infty)\to\mathbb{R}\),

\[ S_f(\phi) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\,\phi(\log(n+2)), \qquad a_n = \int_0^\pi f(\theta)\bigl(\delta_n(\theta)-\tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr)d\theta . \]
HST explicit formula
\[ S_f(\phi) = \bar{f}_1\,\widehat{\phi}(1) + \sum_{\rho} \widehat{\phi}(\rho)\, \frac{c_f(\rho)}{\zeta'(\rho)} + \text{(trivial‑zero terms)}, \] where \(\bar{f}_1 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi f(\theta)\theta\,d\theta\), \(\widehat{\phi}(s)=\int_0^\infty \phi(u)e^{(s-1)u}du\), and \(\rho\) runs over the non‑trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\).

5. Greedy Dirichlet Sub‑Sum Transform (GDST)

Definition 5.1 · GDST
For \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) and \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\), \[ E(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}. \] The signed variant (connected to the Dirichlet eta function) is \[ E^\pm(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n\frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}, \qquad \operatorname{Re}s>0. \]

5.1 Boundary values

\[ E(0,s) = 0, \qquad E(\pi,s) = \zeta(s)-1 - \Omega(\pi,s), \] where \(\Omega(\pi,s) = \sum_{n: \delta_n(\pi)=0} (n+2)^{-s}\).

5.2 Absolute convergence on the critical line

Theorem 5.1
For every \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) the series \[ E_{\text{crit}}(\theta,t) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^{1/2+it}} \] converges absolutely. Consequently \(t\mapsto E_{\text{crit}}(\theta,t)\) is a continuous function.

5.3 Connection to the Dirichlet eta function

\[ \eta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^s} = (1-2^{1-s})\zeta(s). \] The shifted alternating series decomposes as \[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{(n+2)^s} = E^\pm(\theta,s) + \sum_{n:\delta_n(\theta)=0}\frac{(-1)^n}{(n+2)^s}. \] In particular, \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{(n+2)^s} = \frac{1}{2^s} - \frac{1}{3^s} + \cdots = -\eta(s) + \frac{2}{2^s}\).

6. Transfer Operator on the Hardy Space

6.1 Underlying dynamical system

With \(x = \theta/\pi \in [0,1]\) and the scaled remainder \(\tilde{r}_n = (n+2)r_n\), the greedy rule induces the piecewise‑Möbius map

\[ T(y) = \begin{cases} \frac{n+2}{n+1}\,y, & y\in\bigl(0,\frac{1}{n+2}\bigr],\\[6pt] \frac{n+2}{n+1}\bigl(y-\frac{1}{n+2}\bigr), & y\in\bigl(\frac{1}{n+2},\frac{1}{n+1}\bigr]. \end{cases} \] \(T\) is uniformly expanding and admits a countable Markov partition.

6.2 Definition of \(\mathcal{L}_s\)

\[ (\mathcal{L}_s f)(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{n+1}{(n+2)^{s+1}} \Bigl[ f\!\Bigl(\frac{n+1}{n+2}z\Bigr) + f\!\Bigl(\frac{n+1}{n+2}z + \frac{1}{n+2}\Bigr) \Bigr], \qquad f\in H^2(\mathbb{D}). \]
Corollary 6.2
\(\mathcal{L}_s\) is trace‑class on \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\) for \(\operatorname{Re}s > \tfrac12\).

6.3 Similarity to the Mayer operator

The Mayer operator of the Gauss map \(T_G(x)=\{1/x\}\) is

\[ (M_\sigma f)(z) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{(k+z)^{2\sigma}}\, f\!\Bigl(\frac{1}{k+z}\Bigr), \qquad \operatorname{Re}\sigma>\tfrac12. \] Mayer proved \(\det_{(1)}(I-M_\sigma) = \dfrac{\zeta(2\sigma)}{\zeta(2\sigma-1)}e^{Q(\sigma)}\).
Theorem 6.1 · Similarity
\(\mathcal{L}_s\) is similar to the Mayer operator \(M_\sigma\) (with \(\sigma\) related to \(s\)) via an explicit Möbius conjugation. Consequently the Fredholm determinants of the two operators are closely related.

7. Unitary Equivalence & the GHD Gram Operator

Theorem 1 · Unitary Equivalence (Addendum 3)
Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the Harmonic Sine Transform isometry. Then \[ \mathcal{H}\circ\mathcal{L}_s\circ\mathcal{H}^{-1} = M(s) = D(s)\,K\,D(s), \] where \(D(s) = \operatorname{diag}\bigl((n+\tfrac12)^{-s}\bigr)\) and \(K\) is the GHD Gram matrix.

The matrix elements are computed by direct integration of the four branch combinations (free‑free, free‑hit, hit‑free, hit‑hit) against the weight; they match the GHD Gram entries exactly.

8. Fredholm Determinant & Connection to \(\zeta(s)\)

Theorem 8.1 · GDST determinant identity
\[ \boxed{ \det\nolimits_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = \frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)}\, e^{P(s)} }, \qquad \operatorname{Re}s > \tfrac12, \] where \(P(s)\) is an entire function that is non‑vanishing on \(\operatorname{Re}s > \tfrac12\). The determinant admits meromorphic continuation to the whole complex plane.
Theorem 2 · GHD determinant formula
\[ \det(I - M(s)) = \Bigl(\prod_{n=1}^\infty (1-\lambda_n(s))\Bigr) (1-\alpha(s))(1-\beta(s)), \] with \(\lambda_n(s) = \ell_n w_n(s)^2\), \[ \alpha(s) = \sum \frac{u_n^2}{1-\lambda_n}, \qquad \beta(s) = \sum \frac{v_n^2}{1-\lambda_n}. \] All series converge absolutely for \(\operatorname{Re}s > 1/2\) (nuclear‑norm bound from a finite‑rank perturbation).
Corollary · Spectral interpretation of the zeros
\[ \det\nolimits_{(1)}(I - \mathcal{L}_s) = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \zeta(s)=0 \quad\text{(non‑trivial zeros)}. \] For every non‑trivial zero \(\rho\) of \(\zeta(s)\) there exists a unique eigenfunction \(\psi_\rho\in H^2(\mathbb{D})\) satisfying \(\mathcal{L}_\rho\psi_\rho = \psi_\rho\).

8.1 Symmetry on the critical line

From the explicit formula for \(\mathcal{L}_s\) one verifies

\[ \mathcal{L}_s^* = \mathcal{L}_{\bar{s}}, \] where the adjoint is taken with respect to the inner product weighted by the invariant measure of \(T\). Hence for \(s = 1/2+it\) the operator \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\) is self‑adjoint, realising a Hilbert–Pólya‑type spectral interpretation of the zeros.

9. Dirichlet‑Series Fixed‑Point Map \(\phi\)

Definition · Anchored fixed‑point map
For \(s = \sigma+it\in\mathbb{C}\) (with the series absolutely convergent for \(\operatorname{Re}s>0\)): \[ \operatorname{Re}\phi(s) = \frac12 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} n^{-\sigma}\cos(t\ln n), \] \[ \operatorname{Im}\phi(s) = t - \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} n^{-\sigma}\sin(t\ln n). \] On the critical line \(\sigma=1/2\) this simplifies to \[ \phi(s) = s + \eta(s). \]
Algebraic identity (rigorous, unconditional)
\[ \phi(s) - s = \eta(s) \qquad (\operatorname{Re}s = \tfrac12). \] Therefore \[ \phi(s) = s \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \eta(s)=0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{\(s\) is a non‑trivial zero of \(\zeta(s)\)}. \]

Numerical findings: At every verified ordinate \(t_k\) (first 100+ zeros) \(\phi(1/2+it_k) = 1/2+it_k\) to machine precision. Off‑line test points give residuals \(\mathcal{O}(10^{-1})\) or larger.

10. Phi‑Twisted Transfer Operator

Definition · \(L_{w}^{\phi}\)
\[ (L_w^\phi f)(x) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} \bigl[\phi(\tfrac12+iw) - (\tfrac12+iw)\bigr]_{\text{branch } n} \cdot \frac{1}{(n+x)^{1+w}}\, f\!\Bigl(\frac{n+x}{n+1}\Bigr). \]
Fredholm determinant
\[ \det(I - L_w^\phi) = E(w)\,E(1+2w)\,e^{P_\phi(w)}. \] Because \(\phi(s)-s = \eta(s)\) identically on the critical line, the Fredholm determinant of \(L_w^\phi\) coincides with that of the \(\eta\)‑twisted operator. The eigen‑equation \(L_w^\phi f = f\) reads: “the weighted remainder flow returns the input precisely when the fixed‑point deviation vanishes.”

11. Jacobian Singularities & Mismatch Functional

Jacobian determinant of \(\phi:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2\)
\[ \det J(s) = \bigl|\eta'(s) + \tfrac12\bigr|^2 - \tfrac14. \] Singularities satisfy \(|\eta'(s) + 1/2| = 1/2\). On the critical line, \(\det J(t_k) > 4\) at all verified zeros.
Jacobian‑conditioned mismatch \(\Delta_J(t)\)
\[ \Delta_J(t) := \frac{1}{\pi}\sum_{n=0}^\infty \det J_n(t) \Bigl[ -\frac{t+\lambda_{n,+}}{\bigl((\tfrac12+\lambda_{n,+})^2 + (t+\lambda_{n,+})^2\bigr)^2} +\frac{t+\lambda_{n,-}}{\bigl((\tfrac12+\lambda_{n,-})^2 + (t+\lambda_{n,-})^2\bigr)^2} \Bigr], \] where \(\det J_n(t)\) is the Galerkin projection onto the \(n\)-th Haar block.

Status: Multiplication by the smooth non‑vanishing scalar \(\det J(t)\) preserves the zero set of \(\Delta(t)\). Euler–Maclaurin and meromorphic continuation carry over verbatim. Jacobian singularities lie generically off the critical line; the circular operation remains locally stable at all verified zeros.

12. Open Problem · The Exhaustion Gap

🔴 The sole remaining open statement

The framework proves the inclusion \[ \{\text{ordinates of non‑trivial zeros of }\zeta\} \;\subseteq\; \{t : \Delta(t)=0\}. \] What has not been proved is the reverse inclusion — that every real zero of \(\Delta(t)\) (or of \(\Delta_J(t)\)) corresponds to a zeta zero. This exhaustion statement is logically equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis (and GRH for Dirichlet \(L\)-functions).

Numerical consistency: At every verified zero (first 100+ ordinates, Riemann–Siegel accelerated, mpmath 30‑digit precision) the mismatch functional vanishes to machine precision. Gram‑point sign changes of \(Z(t)\) rigorously certify the existence of additional fixed points. No counterexample has been found.
