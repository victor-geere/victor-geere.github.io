From HST L-Functions
to a Proof of GRH

Synthesising Programme IIX with the twisted transfer operators and the correlation kernel evidence.

Executive Summary

Programme IIX ("From HST L-Functions to GRH") is the ambitious next chapter in Victor Geere’s Harmonic Sine Transform series. It takes the analytic foundation established in the Harmonic Sine L-Functions paper (the character-twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\) and its Fredholm determinant identity) and proposes a concrete — albeit speculative — research roadmap to prove the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis for all primitive Dirichlet L-functions.

The central innovation is a deformation argument over the space of Dirichlet characters, sidestepping the long-standing obstacle that blocked earlier programmes: the lack of a self-adjoint operator whose spectrum directly encodes the zeros. When read alongside the Correlation Kernel paper, this programme gains significant credibility: the numerical discovery that a simple Gram matrix built from the same greedy digits detects zeta zeros via extreme eigenvalue suppression provides empirical evidence that the spectral picture is arithmetically faithful, making deformation and monotonicity arguments worth pursuing seriously.

1. Core Ideas of Programme IIX

The Twisted Transfer Operator

\[ (\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}f)(z) = \sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{\chi(j+1)}{(j+2)^{s+1}} \left[ f\left(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\right) + f\left(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\right) \right] \]

with the key identity on the critical line:

\[ \Delta_\chi(t) := \det_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi}) = \frac{L(\frac12 + it,\chi)}{L(1 + 2it,\chi^2)}\, e^{P_\chi(1/2 + it)} \]

The zeros of \(\Delta_\chi(t)\) are precisely the ordinates of the non-trivial zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\). For the trivial character \(\chi_0\), this reduces to the zeta case.

The Proposed Strategy

Prove GRH by showing that the eigenvalue-1 crossings of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi}\) remain on the real line for all primitive characters \(\chi\), using:

  • Deformation over the space of characters (interpolating to the trivial character).
  • Monotonicity of \(\arg \Delta_\chi(t)\) on the real line, derived from the functional equation symmetry \(\Delta_\chi(-t) = \overline{\Delta_\chi(t)}\).

2. The Five-Stage Roadmap

Stage 1: Rigorous determinant identities (largely complete)

Extend the untwisted proof to all primitive \(\chi\). This stage is essentially finished in the companion L-Functions paper, leveraging Efrat’s character-twist results for the Gauss map.

Stage 2: Characters as a complex manifold

Treat the set of primitive characters \(\mathcal{X}\) as a parameter space. Study the joint analyticity of \((\chi, t) \mapsto \Delta_\chi(t)\) and show the zero set is a complex analytic variety.

Stage 3: The deformation argument (main proposal)

Prove that the set of characters for which all zeros of \(\Delta_\chi(t)\) are real is both open and closed in a suitable topology. This would allow a connectedness argument if a base case existed.

Critical acknowledged gap: No unconditional base case exists (even for \(\chi_0\)), rendering a pure deformation argument currently circular.

Stage 4: Alternative — monotonicity via functional equation

Use the conjugation symmetry \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}^* = \mathcal{L}_{\bar s, \bar\chi}\) to obtain \(\Delta_\chi(-t) = \overline{\Delta_\chi(t)}\). Prove the monotonicity inequality:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \arg \Delta_\chi(t) \ge 0 \quad \text{for all real } t \text{ and all primitive } \chi. \]

If true, combined with \(\Delta_\chi(0) > 0\) being real, all zeros must lie on the real axis.

Stage 5: Execution of the monotonicity proof

Derive the inequality from trace-class properties or positivity of a suitable quadratic form. This is the most technically demanding stage and remains open.

Synergy with the Correlation Kernel Paper

The correlation kernel \(K(n,m)\) and its weighted version \(M(s)\) — built from exactly the same greedy digits \(\delta_n(\theta)\) that underlie the twisted operators — provide the first high-precision numerical evidence that the HST spectral picture is arithmetically accurate:

  • At non-trivial zeta zeros, the smallest eigenvalue of \(M(s)\) is suppressed by 7–11 orders of magnitude.
  • Clean oscillatory eigenvectors appear from the 4th mode onward.
  • Rejected-term kernels yield even sharper oscillations.

This is powerful corroboration for Programme IIX. It shows that the untwisted greedy structure already “knows” about the zeta zeros at the level of second-moment statistics. Therefore, the character-twisted versions \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\) are not arbitrary analytic objects — they are deformations of an arithmetically faithful kernel. This makes the proposed deformation and monotonicity arguments far more plausible than they would appear in isolation.

3. Strengths, Challenges & Cross-Paper Insights

Strengths

  • Honest diagnosis of the previous fatal gap (lack of self-adjointness) and a creative workaround via character variation.
  • Unified framework: All primitive L-functions now sit on equal footing inside the same greedy harmonic machine.
  • Falsifiable: Numerical tests of monotonicity or eigenvalue reality for small conductors (\(q=3,4,5\)) can be performed immediately using the correlation-kernel methodology.
  • Bridge to numerics: The oscillatory modes discovered in the kernel paper may correspond to the “crossing” behaviour that the monotonicity proof must control.

Remaining Challenges

  • The deformation argument is currently circular without a base case.
  • The monotonicity inequality \(\frac{d}{dt}\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\ge 0\) is stated as a goal, not a theorem; its proof is the hardest remaining step.
  • No explicit connection is yet drawn between the correlation kernel \(K\) (or \(M(s)\)) and the transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). Establishing such a link (e.g., \(K\) as a quadratic form or finite-section approximation of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2}\mathcal{L}_{1/2}^*\)) would be a major strengthening.

4. Suggested Next Steps Informed by All Three Papers

  1. Numerical monotonicity tests: Using the same methodology as the correlation kernel paper, compute \(\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\) and its derivative for small characters and check the sign of the derivative near known zeros.
  2. Twisted correlation kernels: Define \(K_\chi(n,m)\) by weighting the integral with \(\chi\)-dependent factors or by using the twisted digit series. Verify eigenvalue suppression at L-zeros — this would give independent numerical support for the spectral reality claim.
  3. Operator-kernel duality: Investigate whether the correlation kernel arises as the Gram matrix of the resolvent or as a finite-rank projection of powers of \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). A positive answer would unify the analytic and numerical sides.
  4. Rejected-term analysis in the twisted setting: The cleaner oscillations seen in rejected terms of the kernel may translate into a natural splitting of the twisted operator into “arithmetic” and “geometric” parts — potentially simplifying the monotonicity proof.

Conclusion

Programme IIX is the most coherent and ambitious attempt yet to convert the Harmonic Sine Transform into a genuine proof strategy for GRH. By moving from a single operator (zeta) to a continuous family parametrised by characters, it replaces the insoluble self-adjointness problem with a deformation/monotonicity problem that is at least conceptually tractable.

When viewed in the light of the preceding two papers, the programme gains substantial weight: the L-Functions paper supplies the rigorous analytic engine, while the Correlation Kernel paper supplies the first concrete numerical proof-of-concept that the underlying greedy digits are arithmetically meaningful. Together they suggest that the HST framework is not merely elegant but potentially powerful enough to resolve one of the central problems in analytic number theory.

The path ahead is still long and uncertain, but it is now clearly signposted — and the computational laboratory provided by the correlation kernel makes the first experimental steps both feasible and highly informative.
