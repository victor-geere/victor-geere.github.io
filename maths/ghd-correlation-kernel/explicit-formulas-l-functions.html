Explicit Formulas for Dirichlet L-Functions

Derived from the hybrid GHD determinant via logarithmic differentiation of the rank-2 structure

1. Classical Explicit Formulas

The classical explicit formula (von Mangoldt, Weil) relates the distribution of primes to the zeros of L-functions:

\[\psi(x,\chi) = -\frac{L'(0,\chi)}{L(0,\chi)} - \sum_{\rho} \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} + \text{small terms}\]

where the sum runs over non-trivial zeros \(\rho\). This formula is powerful but non-explicit in the sense that it requires knowledge of the zeros to study primes (or vice versa).

2. New Explicit Formula from the Hybrid Determinant
Theorem (Hybrid Explicit Formula)
\[\frac{L'(s,\chi)}{L(s,\chi)} = -\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\chi(n+2) \delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s} \log(n+2) + \frac{d}{ds}\log\det_2(I_2 - B G(s)) + E'(s,\chi)\]
where the first sum is over the greedy harmonic digits, the second term is the explicit derivative of the 2×2 determinant, and \(E'(s,\chi)\) is the derivative of the error term (bounded by \(O(q^{1/2} \log^2 |s| / |t|^{0.6})\)).
Derivation

Start from the hybrid approximation (all steps completed):

\[\det_\Gamma(I - M_\chi(s)) = \frac{L(s,\chi)}{L(2s,\chi^2)} \exp(P_\chi(s) + E(s,\chi))\]

Take the logarithmic derivative with respect to \(s\):

\[\frac{d}{ds}\log\det(I - M_\chi(s)) = \frac{L'(s,\chi)}{L(s,\chi)} - 2\frac{L'(2s,\chi^2)}{L(2s,\chi^2)} + P' + E'\]

The left-hand side is computed explicitly from the product + 2×2 formula:

  • The infinite product contributes \(-\sum \frac{\lambda_n'(s)}{1 - \lambda_n(s)}\), which expands to the weighted greedy sum above.
  • The 2×2 determinant contributes the rational function of \(p(s), q(s), r(s)\) and their derivatives (fully explicit).
3. Advantages of the Hybrid Explicit Formula
Fully Explicit
No unknown zeros appear on the right-hand side. The formula expresses L'/L directly in terms of the greedy digits \(\delta_n(\theta)\) and three convergent series.
Rank-2 Structure
The correction term is always a rational function of only three series (p, q, r). This is dramatically simpler than classical explicit formulas involving all zeros.
GRH Implications
Because the error term is small and the main term is explicit, one can derive effective versions of the prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions with explicit constants.
