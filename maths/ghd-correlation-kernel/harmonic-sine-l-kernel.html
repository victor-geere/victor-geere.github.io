Harmonic Sine L-Functions Kernel

Review: Harmonic Sine L-Functions in Light of the Correlation Kernel.

Executive Summary

The paper "Harmonic Sine L-Functions: Generalising the Greedy Harmonic Zeta Function to Dirichlet L-Functions" elegantly extends the Harmonic Sine Transform (HST) framework to all primitive Dirichlet L-functions by character-twisting the same underlying greedy harmonic digits. When read alongside "A Correlation Kernel from Greedy Harmonic Decomposition and its Connection to Riemann Zeta Zeros", a powerful synergy emerges: the numerical spectral evidence from the correlation kernel provides compelling computational validation for the analytic claims of the L-function generalization, while the shared combinatorial foundation (the untwisted greedy digits \(\delta_n(\theta)\)) suggests natural pathways for twisted kernels that could numerically probe the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis (GRH).

Both papers rely on the identical greedy harmonic expansion of \(\theta \in [0,\pi]\) using steps \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\), with digit functions \(\delta_n(\theta) \in \{0,1\}\) determined by the threshold rule. This common substrate is the key bridge.

1. Core Contribution of the L-Functions Paper

The central achievement is a character-twisted generalization that preserves the combinatorial structure of the original greedy harmonic zeta function while incorporating arithmetic information via Dirichlet characters.

Key Definitions

\[ L_\chi(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\chi(n+2)\,\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}, \qquad \operatorname{Re}s > 1 \] Harmonic sine L-function (twisted greedy Dirichlet series)

The twisted transfer operator on the Hardy space \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\) is:

\[ (\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi} f)(z) = \sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{\chi(j+1)}{(j+2)^{s+1}} \left[ f\left(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\right) + f\left(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\right) \right] \]

Main Theorem (Fredholm Determinant Identity)

\[ \det_{(1)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}) = \frac{L(s,\chi)}{L(2s,\chi^2)}\, e^{P_\chi(s)} \] where \(P_\chi(s)\) is entire and non-vanishing in the critical strip. Extends meromorphically via the regularized determinant \(\det_{(2)}\).

On the critical line \(s = \frac12 + it\):

\[ \Delta_\chi(t) := \det_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{1/2 + it,\chi}) = \frac{L(\frac12 + it,\chi)}{L(1 + 2it,\chi^2)}\, e^{P_\chi(1/2 + it)} \]

Spectral interpretation: The non-trivial zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\) on the critical line are precisely the values of \(t\) at which 1 is an eigenvalue of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi}\). Thus GRH for \(L(s,\chi)\) is equivalent to the reality of the spectrum of the twisted operator — exactly parallel to the untwisted zeta case.

2. Core Contribution of the Correlation Kernel Paper

This paper constructs a positive semi-definite correlation kernel directly from the same greedy digit functions, without any character twisting:

\[ \phi_n(\theta) = \delta_n(\theta) - \frac{\theta}{\pi}, \qquad K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \phi_n(\theta)\,\phi_m(\theta)\,d\theta \]

Key numerical observations for the \(N \times N\) matrix \(K_N\):

  • Leading eigenvalue scales as \(\lambda_1(N) \approx 1.028\, N\)
  • From the 4th eigenvector onward: a clear hierarchy of oscillatory modes with increasing frequency
  • The kernel built from rejected terms yields cleaner oscillations

The weighted operator (Mellin-type weighting) is:

\[ M_{n,m}(s) = K(n,m) \cdot (n+2)^{-(s + 1/2)}(m+2)^{-(s + 1/2)} \]

Striking numerical result: On the critical line \(\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2\), the smallest eigenvalue of \(M(s)\) is strongly suppressed (order \(10^{-7}\) to \(10^{-11}\)) precisely at the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\), while at generic points it hovers around \(-0.06\). The double Mellin transform \(D(s)\) shows analogous suppression.

The Unifying Insight

The correlation kernel paper supplies the missing numerical "ground truth" for the untwisted case that makes the analytic generalization in the L-functions paper far more credible.

Both papers rest on the identical set of indicator functions \(\{\delta_n(\theta)\}_{n=0}^\infty\). The L-paper analytically twists their Dirichlet series and the associated transfer operator. The kernel paper studies the unweighted Gram matrix of the centered digits \(\phi_n(\theta)\) and discovers that its spectrum (after natural Mellin weighting) "knows" about the zeta zeros through eigenvalue suppression. This is powerful evidence that the greedy harmonic digits genuinely encode deep arithmetic information.

3. Synergies and Deeper Connections

Shared Combinatorial Skeleton

The fact that the L-function paper deliberately keeps the digits \(\delta_n(\theta)\) untwisted (only the weights are multiplied by \(\chi(n+2)\)) is crucial. It means the correlation kernel \(K(n,m)\) is the natural "inner-product matrix" of the very same features that, when twisted, generate \(L(s,\chi)\). One can therefore immediately define a family of twisted correlation kernels:

\[ K_\chi(n,m) := \int_0^\pi \phi_n(\theta)\,\phi_m(\theta) \cdot w_\chi(\theta)\,d\theta \] (or more naturally, weight the series defining \(K\) itself by characters). Numerical checks for small conductors (\(q=3,4,5\)) would likely reveal analogous eigenvalue suppression at the zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\).

Operator vs. Kernel Duality

The transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\) is a weighted sum over the two branches of the greedy map. The correlation kernel is the Gram matrix of the digit indicator functions in \(L^2([0,\pi])\). These are dual pictures of the same underlying dynamics:

  • The resolvent of \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\) encodes the orbit sums that define \(L_\chi(\theta,s)\)
  • The kernel \(K\) (and its weighted version \(M(s)\)) captures the second-moment / covariance structure of those same orbits

The observed eigenvalue suppression in \(M(s)\) at zeta zeros is therefore consistent with (and provides independent numerical support for) the claim that eigenvalue 1 crossings of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\) occur exactly at those zeros.

Implications for GRH

The L-paper reduces GRH to "reality of the spectrum" of a family of operators indexed by characters. The correlation kernel paper shows that, already in the untwisted case, the spectrum of a concrete finite-dimensional matrix approximation (after weighting) is exquisitely sensitive to the zeta zeros. This suggests that the "reality obstacle" may be attackable via monotonicity or variational principles on these kernels — precisely the direction hinted at in Programme IIX of the HST series.

4. Strengths, Limitations & Open Questions

Aspect L-Functions Paper Correlation Kernel Paper Synergy
Analytic Power Excellent (Fredholm det identity + meromorphic continuation) Limited (purely numerical) Kernel supplies numerical validation of the analytic claims
Computational Evidence Only small-q sketches proposed Strong (eigenvalue suppression at \(10^{-11}\)) Kernel evidence retroactively strengthens L-paper
Path to Proof Reality of spectrum (same as zeta case) Observational only Twisted kernels + oscillatory modes may yield new inequalities

Open Directions Suggested by the Pair

  1. Twisted kernels for small characters: Compute \(K_\chi\) and \(M_\chi(s)\) for \(\chi\) mod 3,4,5 and verify suppression at L-zeros.
  2. Relation between \(K\) and \(\mathcal{L}\): Is the correlation kernel the quadratic form associated with \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2}\mathcal{L}_{1/2}^*\) or a power of the resolvent? A precise operator-theoretic link would be valuable.
  3. Galois action on twisted operators: The family \(\{\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\}\) indexed by characters may admit additional symmetries (as hinted in the discussion section) that the kernel picture could illuminate numerically.
  4. Rejected vs. accepted terms: The cleaner oscillations in the rejected-term kernel suggest a natural decomposition that might separate "arithmetic" from "geometric" contributions — worth exploring in the twisted setting.

Conclusion

Reading the Harmonic Sine L-Functions paper through the lens of the Correlation Kernel paper transforms what might have appeared as a formal generalization into a deeply coherent research programme. The numerical discovery that a simple Gram matrix built from the greedy digits "detects" zeta zeros via eigenvalue suppression provides independent, high-precision evidence that the combinatorial skeleton of the HST is arithmetically rich. The character-twisting construction then lifts this richness uniformly to all primitive Dirichlet L-functions.

Together, these two works demonstrate that the greedy harmonic decomposition is not merely a curiosity but a genuine bridge between analysis (transfer operators, Fredholm determinants) and arithmetic (zeta/L-zeros, characters). The path to GRH/GRH via "reality of the spectrum" now has both a clean analytic formulation and a concrete numerical laboratory in which to test ideas.

This pairing exemplifies the strength of the HST programme: rigorous analytic identities paired with high-resolution numerical phenomenology, all built on the same elegant combinatorial foundation.
