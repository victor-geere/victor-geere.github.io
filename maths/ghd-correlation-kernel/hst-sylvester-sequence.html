Abstract

We establish a precise connection between the classical Sylvester sequence and the Greedy Harmonic Sieve that underlies the Harmonic Sine Transform (HST) programme. The greedy Egyptian-fraction algorithm that generates the Sylvester sequence is formally isomorphic to the selection rule \(\delta_n(\theta)\) of the harmonic decomposition. Consequently, the Sylvester constant

\[ E = \lim_{n\to\infty} s_n^{2^{-n}} \approx 1.264084735305301 \]

governs the asymptotic growth of threshold angles in the sieve and appears naturally in the spectral analysis of the twisted transfer operators. This link unifies the combinatorial foundation of the HST framework with classical Egyptian-fraction theory and provides new combinatorial insight into the deformation and monotonicity arguments proposed in Programme IIX for the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis.

1. Introduction

The Harmonic Sine Transform programme has developed a unified framework in which a single greedy combinatorial rule — the decomposition of angles in \([0,\pi]\) using harmonic steps \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) — generates both deep analytic objects (twisted transfer operators and Fredholm determinants encoding Dirichlet \(L\)-functions) and concrete number-theoretic structures (correlation kernels and sieves).

In the Greedy Harmonic Sieve paper we observed that the selection rule \(\delta_n(\theta)\) is formally identical to the classical greedy Egyptian-fraction algorithm that produces the Sylvester sequence. The present note makes this connection rigorous and explores its consequences for the entire HST programme.

Key insight: The Sylvester sequence is the extreme case of the greedy harmonic mechanism. When the target forces maximal greedy choices at every step, the resulting denominators obey the Sylvester recurrence and are controlled by the constant \(E\).

2. The Sylvester Sequence and Its Constant

Definition and Recurrence

The Sylvester sequence \(\{s_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is defined by \[ s_1 = 2, \qquad s_{n+1} = s_n^2 - s_n + 1. \]

It satisfies the Egyptian-fraction identity \[ 1 = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{s_k} \] with the telescoping remainder \[ 1 - \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{s_k} = \frac{1}{s_{n+1}-1}. \]

The Sylvester Constant \(E\)

The sequence admits the closed-form asymptotic \[ s_n = \left\lfloor E^{2^n} \right\rfloor + 1 \] (for all sufficiently large \(n\)), where the Sylvester constant is \[ E = \lim_{n\to\infty} s_n^{2^{-n}} \approx 1.264084735305301077. \]

This limit converges extremely rapidly (stable to 20 decimals by \(n=8\)) because of the double-exponential growth of \(s_n\).

3. The Greedy Harmonic Sieve

For a target angle \(\theta \in [0,\pi]\), define the harmonic steps \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) and the greedy selection indicators \[ \delta_n(\theta) = \Theta(\theta - \theta_n - \alpha_n) \in \{0,1\}, \] where \(\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + \delta_n(\theta) \alpha_n\).

The associated greedy harmonic sieve set is \[ \mathcal{S}(\theta) = \{n+2 : \delta_n(\theta) = 1\}. \]

This construction yields a nested filtration of the positive integers whose logarithmic density is exactly \(\theta/\pi\) and whose reciprocal sums converge to \(\theta/\pi\).

4. The Formal Link: Greedy Selection Rules

The greedy Egyptian-fraction expansion of a rational \(r \in (0,1]\) proceeds by repeatedly subtracting the largest possible unit fraction: \[ r_{k+1} = r_k - \frac{1}{d_k}, \qquad d_{k+1} = \left\lceil \frac{1}{r_{k+1}} \right\rceil. \]

When \(r = 1\), this produces exactly the Sylvester sequence: \(d_k = s_k\).

Theorem 4.1 (Isomorphism of greedy rules).
The selection rule \(\delta_n(\theta)\) of the harmonic sieve is isomorphic to the greedy Egyptian-fraction algorithm under the identification \[ \theta \leftrightarrow \frac{\theta}{\pi}, \qquad \alpha_n \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{n+2}. \] Consequently, when the target forces maximal greedy choices at every step, the resulting denominators obey the Sylvester recurrence and are governed by the constant \(E\).

This isomorphism explains why the threshold angles \(\theta_n^*\) in the harmonic decomposition exhibit double-exponential spacing for certain special angles — precisely the behaviour encoded by \(E^{2^n}\).

5. The Sylvester Constant in the HST Framework

The constant \(E\) appears naturally in several places within the HST programme:

  • Threshold growth: For angles whose greedy expansion is “Sylvester-like”, the threshold sequence satisfies \(\theta_n^* \sim c \cdot E^{2^n}\).
  • Correlation kernel: The second-moment structure of the indicators \(\delta_n(\theta)\) (quantified by the kernel \(K(n,m)\)) is inherited from the same greedy mechanism that produces the Sylvester recurrence. The oscillatory eigenvectors of \(K\) reflect the algebraic structure of the Sylvester relation.
  • Transfer operator spectrum: The resolvent representation of the harmonic sine \(L\)-function \(L_\chi(\theta,s)\) involves orbit sums under the greedy map — the same map whose maximal orbits generate the Sylvester sequence.

6. Spectral and Analytic Connections

The link between the Sylvester sequence and the HST spectral objects is particularly striking in the context of Programme IIX:

Conjecture 6.1 (Sylvester–Monotonicity Link).
The monotonicity inequality \(\frac{d}{dt}\arg\Delta_\chi(t) \ge 0\) proposed in Programme IIX can be proved combinatorially by showing that the filtration \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\) preserves a positivity property under continuous deformation of the density parameter \(\theta\), with the extremal (Sylvester) case providing the base of the induction. The constant \(E\) controls the rate at which new integers enter the filtration in the extremal case.

Numerical evidence from the correlation kernel paper (eigenvalue suppression at zeta zeros) already shows that the greedy correlations carry arithmetic information; the Sylvester sequence supplies the purest algebraic realisation of those correlations.

7. Primality and Arithmetic Implications

Only the first four Sylvester numbers are prime (\(2, 3, 7, 43\)). All larger terms are composite. This rapid transition to compositeness is a direct consequence of the quadratic recurrence and mirrors the way the greedy harmonic sieve produces sets with natural density zero but positive logarithmic density.

The compositeness of large \(s_n\) suggests that the corresponding “Sylvester angles” in the harmonic decomposition generate sieves whose arithmetic properties are governed by algebraic factors rather than by primes — consistent with the non-multiplicative character of the HST framework.

8. Conclusion and Open Questions

The Sylvester sequence is not an isolated curiosity but the canonical extreme case of the greedy harmonic selection rule that lies at the heart of the entire HST programme. The constant \(E\) provides a precise asymptotic description of both the growth of the sequence and the spacing of threshold angles in the sieve.

This identification yields:

  • A combinatorial explanation for the rapid convergence of many HST quantities.
  • A new route toward the monotonicity argument of Programme IIX via filtration properties.
  • A bridge between Egyptian-fraction theory and the spectral analysis of twisted transfer operators.

Open questions:

  1. Can the Sylvester constant \(E\) be expressed in closed form using the twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi_0}\)?
  2. Does the monotonicity of \(\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\) follow from a percolation-type argument on the filtration \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\)?
  3. Are there “twisted Sylvester sequences” arising from character-weighted greedy selections whose primality properties relate to the zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\)?

The greedy harmonic sieve, the correlation kernel, the twisted transfer operators, and the Sylvester sequence are four manifestations of a single underlying combinatorial principle. Recognising the Sylvester sequence as the extremal case unifies these strands and strengthens the conceptual foundation of the Harmonic Sine Transform programme.
