Abstract

The greedy harmonic decomposition of an angle \(\theta \in [0,\pi]\) produces binary selection indicators \(\delta_n(\theta) \in \{0,1\}\) that define a deterministic subset \(\mathcal{S}(\theta) \subset \{2,3,4,\ldots\}\) of the natural numbers. This paper investigates the sieve-theoretic properties of these sets. We show that \(\mathcal{S}(\theta)\) has natural density zero for all \(\theta < \pi\) but possesses a well-defined logarithmic density equal to \(\theta/\pi\), making it a natural example of a set whose logarithmic and natural densities disagree.

We compare the greedy harmonic sieve with classical arithmetic sieves (Eratosthenes, Selberg, Brun), identifying both structural analogies and fundamental differences. We establish a precise probabilistic model via independent Bernoulli trials with success probability \(x/(n+2)\), show that the greedy sieve matches this model in its first moment but differs in its correlation structure (as quantified in the companion Correlation Kernel paper), and connect the construction to the classical theory of Egyptian fractions.

Expanded perspective (this edition): By cross-referencing the Harmonic Sine L-Functions paper and Programme IIX, we show how the sieve sets \(\mathcal{S}(\theta)\) serve as the combinatorial foundation for the twisted transfer operators whose Fredholm determinants encode Dirichlet L-functions, and how the filtration property of \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\) offers a potential combinatorial route toward the monotonicity argument proposed for proving the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis.

1. Setup and Recap

We recall the essential definitions from the companion paper On the Correlation Structure of a Greedy Harmonic Decomposition of the Sine Function [Geere, 2026a].

Definition 1.1 (Greedy harmonic decomposition)

\[ \delta_n(\theta) = \Theta\bigl(\theta - \theta_n - \alpha_n\bigr) \in \{0,1\}, \] where \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\) and \(\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + \delta_n \alpha_n\).

Definition 1.2 & 1.3

Selection is monotone in \(\theta\), with well-defined threshold angles \(\theta_n^*\). The selection count satisfies \(D(N,\theta) = x \ln(N+2) + O(1)\) for \(x = \theta/\pi\).

Cross-reference to Correlation Kernel paper: The functions \(\phi_n(\theta) = \delta_n(\theta) - \theta/\pi\) are precisely the centered indicators whose Gram matrix \(K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \phi_n(\theta)\phi_m(\theta)\,d\theta\) reveals the correlation structure that distinguishes the greedy sieve from independent models (see Section 4 below).

2. The Greedy Harmonic Sieve

Definition 2.1 (Sieve set)

\[ \mathcal{S}(\theta) = \{n+2 : n \geq 0,\; \delta_n(\theta) = 1\} \]

Proposition 2.2 (Basic properties)

  1. Boundary cases: \(\mathcal{S}(0) = \emptyset\), \(\mathcal{S}(\pi) = \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2}\).
  2. Monotone nesting: \(\theta_1 \leq \theta_2 \implies \mathcal{S}(\theta_1) \subseteq \mathcal{S}(\theta_2)\).
  3. The family \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\) forms a continuous filtration of the integers.
  4. Counting function: \(|\mathcal{S}(\theta) \cap [2,N]| = (\theta/\pi)\ln N + O(1)\).
Link to Programme IIX: The filtration property of \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\) is the combinatorial analogue of the continuous deformation over characters proposed in Stage 3 of Programme IIX. Just as \(\theta\) continuously “fills” the integers, the character parameter continuously deforms the twisted operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). This suggests that monotonicity properties of the sieve filtration may translate into the desired monotonicity of \(\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\).

Definition 2.4 & Lemma 2.5 (Reciprocal sum)

\[ \Sigma(\theta, N) = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}(\theta),\, k\leq N} \frac{1}{k} = \frac{\theta}{\pi} + O\left(\frac{1}{N}\right) \]

3. Density Properties

Theorem 3.1 (Natural vs Logarithmic Density)

For every fixed \(\theta \in [0,\pi)\), the set \(\mathcal{S}(\theta)\) has natural density zero:

\[ \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{|\mathcal{S}(\theta) \cap [1,N]|}{N} = 0. \]

However, it possesses a well-defined logarithmic density equal to \(\theta/\pi\):

\[ \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{\ln N} \sum_{k=1}^N \frac{1_{\mathcal{S}(\theta)}(k)}{k} = \frac{\theta}{\pi}. \]

Proof sketch (original + expanded): The natural-density claim follows directly from the counting function \(D(N,\theta) = O(\ln N)\). The logarithmic-density result is immediate from Lemma 2.5, since the reciprocal sum \(\Sigma(\theta,N)\) converges to \(\theta/\pi\).

Expanded insight from L-Functions paper: When the sieve is twisted by a primitive character \(\chi\) (as in the definition of the harmonic sine L-function \(L_\chi(\theta,s)\)), the resulting weighted reciprocal sums become the partial sums of a Dirichlet series whose meromorphic continuation is governed by the twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). The logarithmic density \(\theta/\pi\) then appears as the residue at the pole \(s=1\) of the twisted series, consistent with the determinant identity \(\det(I-\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}) = L(s,\chi)/L(2s,\chi^2) \cdot e^{P_\chi(s)}\).

4. A Probabilistic Comparison (Expanded with Correlation Kernel)

Definition 4.1 (Independent Bernoulli model)

Consider independent random variables \(X_n \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p_n)\) with \(p_n = x/(n+2)\), \(x = \theta/\pi\). Let \(\mathcal{S}^{\text{rand}}(x)\) be the random set where \(n+2 \in \mathcal{S}^{\text{rand}}\) iff \(X_n = 1\).

Proposition 4.2 (First-moment agreement)

\(\mathbb{E}[|\mathcal{S}^{\text{rand}}(x) \cap [2,N]|] = x\ln N + O(1)\), matching the deterministic count \(D(N,\theta)\).

Theorem 4.3 (Correlation discrepancy — key gap filled)

While first moments agree, the second-moment structure diverges. The covariance structure of the greedy indicators is given exactly by the correlation kernel of the companion paper:

\[ \text{Cov}(\delta_n(\theta),\delta_m(\theta)) = K(n,m) + O\left(\frac{1}{n+m}\right), \] where \[ K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \left(\delta_n(\theta)-\frac{\theta}{\pi}\right)\left(\delta_m(\theta)-\frac{\theta}{\pi}\right)\,d\theta. \]

The kernel \(K\) is positive semi-definite. Its leading eigenvalue scales as \(\lambda_1(N) \approx 1.028 N\), and from the fourth eigenvector onward one observes a clear hierarchy of oscillatory modes. These oscillations explain why the greedy sieve deviates from the independent model in higher moments and why its eigenvalue spectrum (when weighted by \((n+2)^{-(s+1/2)}\)) exhibits dramatic suppression precisely at the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

Expanded numerical evidence (from Correlation Kernel paper): When the weighted operator \(M_{n,m}(s) = K(n,m) \cdot (n+2)^{-(s+1/2)}(m+2)^{-(s+1/2)}\) is evaluated on the critical line, its smallest eigenvalue drops by 7–11 orders of magnitude exactly at the ordinates of the non-trivial zeta zeros. This provides strong numerical confirmation that the deterministic correlations encoded in the greedy sieve are arithmetically significant — a fact that underpins both the L-function determinant identities and the proposed GRH strategy in Programme IIX.

5. Comparison with Arithmetic Sieves

Feature Greedy Harmonic Sieve Classical Arithmetic Sieves
Driving mechanism Geometric (harmonic angles) Arithmetic (primes / factorisation)
Multiplicative structure Absent Present (Euler products)
Counting function \(\sim c \ln N\) \(\sim N/\log N\) or smaller
Density type Logarithmic (natural = 0) Usually natural density
Correlations Strong, deterministic, oscillatory (see K(n,m)) Multiplicative, often weakly dependent

The greedy harmonic sieve is therefore a non-arithmetic, geometrically motivated sieve whose properties derive from the divergence of the harmonic series rather than from the distribution of primes.

6. Connection to Egyptian Fraction Algorithms

The greedy choice of the largest possible harmonic step \(\alpha_n\) at each stage is formally identical to the classical greedy Egyptian fraction algorithm (Sylvester–Fibonacci). Every angle \(\theta\) admits a representation

\[ \frac{\theta}{\pi} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{n+2} \]

which is the greedy Egyptian fraction expansion of the rational \(\theta/\pi\) in the harmonic basis.

Expanded remark: This Egyptian-fraction representation is the starting point for the resolvent identity used in the proof of the Fredholm determinant formula in the L-Functions paper. The orbit of the point \(y_0 = 2\theta/\pi\) under the greedy map generates the partial sums that appear in the expression for \(L_\chi(\theta,s)\).

7. The Generating Dirichlet Series (Expanded)

The natural generating function attached to the sieve is the Dirichlet series

\[ \Phi(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}, \qquad \operatorname{Re}s > 1. \]

Expanded connection to L-Functions paper: When twisted by a primitive Dirichlet character \(\chi\), one obtains the harmonic sine L-function

\[ L_\chi(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\chi(n+2)\,\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}. \]

whose meromorphic continuation and functional equation are controlled by the twisted transfer operator \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\). The Fredholm determinant identity

\[ \det_{(2)}(I - \mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}) = \frac{L(s,\chi)}{L(2s,\chi^2)}\, e^{P_\chi(s)} \]

shows that the zeros of \(L(s,\chi)\) on the critical line are in one-to-one correspondence with the eigenvalue-1 crossings of \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it,\chi}\).

Thus the sieve sets \(\mathcal{S}(\theta)\) are the common combinatorial substrate from which both the classical sieve properties and the deep arithmetic information encoded in Dirichlet L-functions emerge.

8. Discussion (Expanded with Programme IIX)

The greedy harmonic sieve occupies a distinctive position in sieve theory: it is deterministic, geometrically constructed, and free of multiplicative structure, yet its logarithmic density and reciprocal sums are governed by the same harmonic divergence that underlies the Riemann zeta function.

Implications for the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis

The correlation kernel paper demonstrates that the second-moment structure of the greedy digits already “detects” the non-trivial zeta zeros through extreme eigenvalue suppression. Programme IIX proposes to prove GRH by deforming the twisted transfer operators over the space of characters and establishing monotonicity of \(\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\).

Synthesis (new in this expanded edition): The filtration property of \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\) and the Egyptian-fraction representation provide a combinatorial model for the continuous deformation over characters. If one can prove that the reality of eigenvalue crossings is preserved under continuous variation of the density parameter \(\theta\) (or of the character), this would supply the missing base case or monotonicity inequality needed to complete the Programme IIX strategy. The oscillatory eigenvectors of the correlation kernel may correspond to the “crossing modes” whose reality must be controlled.

In short, the greedy harmonic sieve is not merely an interesting combinatorial object — it is the foundational layer on which the entire analytic and spectral machinery of the Harmonic Sine Transform programme is built.

Conclusion & Future Directions

The expanded analysis presented here demonstrates that the greedy harmonic sieve is the unifying combinatorial core of the HST research programme. Its density properties, correlation structure (quantified by the kernel paper), and Egyptian-fraction representation directly underpin the twisted transfer operators and Fredholm determinant identities of the L-Functions paper, while its filtration property offers a concrete combinatorial pathway toward the deformation and monotonicity arguments of Programme IIX.

Recommended next steps:

  1. Define twisted greedy sieves \(\mathcal{S}_\chi(\theta)\) and study their densities in relation to \(L(s,\chi)\).
  2. Use the correlation kernel \(K(n,m)\) to derive explicit bounds on the discrepancy between the greedy sieve and the independent Bernoulli model.
  3. Investigate whether the monotonicity of \(\arg\Delta_\chi(t)\) can be proved using percolation or filtration arguments on \(\{\mathcal{S}(\theta)\}\).

This expanded edition was prepared to strengthen the internal coherence of the HST programme by explicitly linking its combinatorial, analytic, and spectral components.
