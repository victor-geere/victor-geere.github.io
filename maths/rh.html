STATUS: EXPLORATORY PROGRAMME — NOT A COMPLETE PROOF

This document outlines a Hilbert–Pólya-style strategy: detect the zeros of $\zeta$ as the spectrum of a transfer operator and force them onto the critical line via a self-adjointness / PT-symmetry argument. Several analytic statements have been corrected for rigour (growth estimates, the determinant orientation, the Hilbert-space setting). However, three genuine obstructions remain open: (i) constructing a trace-class operator family whose Fredholm determinant is an entire completion of $\zeta$; (ii) proving positivity of the metric (unbroken PT-symmetry) on the critical line; and (iii) supplying the analytic continuation of the operator into the strip $\tfrac12 < \Re s \le 1$. Until these are resolved, the argument is a research programme, not a proof.

Part I: The Two Sieves — Primes and Zeros

1.1 The Prime Sieve

Definition 1.1 (Prime Sieve Function). For $s \in \mathbb{C}$, define \[ \Psi_P(s) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} \sin\left( \frac{\pi s}{p} \right). \] The zeros of $\Psi_P$ occur at $s = kp$ for all integers $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and all primes $p$; equivalently, the zero set is $\bigcup_p p\mathbb{Z} = \{0\} \cup \{\, n \in \mathbb{Z} : |n| \ge 2 \,\}$.
Correction (this is not a prime sieve). Because every integer $n$ with $|n| \ge 2$ is a multiple of some prime, $\Psi_P$ vanishes at every such integer — composites included (e.g. $\sin(\pi \cdot 6/2) = \sin(3\pi) = 0$ and $\sin(\pi \cdot 4/2) = \sin(2\pi) = 0$). So $\Psi_P$ does not isolate the primes; the "$k=1$ gives exactly the primes" reading is incorrect. Isolating primes requires Möbius/inclusion–exclusion or the prime zeta function $P(s) = \sum_p p^{-s}$, not this product alone.
Convergence note. The infinite product diverges and requires regularization (e.g., Weierstrass factorization or zeta regularization). For finite numerical experiments we truncate to finitely many primes, which suffices for illustration.

1.2 The Zeta-Zero Sieve

Definition 1.2 (Completed Zeta Function). The completed Riemann xi-function is \[ \xi(s) = \frac{1}{2} s(s-1) \pi^{-s/2} \Gamma\left( \frac{s}{2} \right) \zeta(s). \] The function $\xi(s)$ is entire and satisfies the functional equation $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$. Its zeros are precisely the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$.

Part II: Construction of the Transfer Operator

2.1 The von Mangoldt Function and the Logarithmic Derivative

Definition 2.1 (von Mangoldt function). \[ \Lambda(n) = \begin{cases} \log p & \text{if } n = p^k \text{ for prime } p \text{ and integer } k \ge 1, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]
Theorem 2.1 (Logarithmic derivative of zeta). For $\Re(s) > 1$, \[ -\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^s} = \sum_p \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\log p}{p^{ks}}. \]

2.2 The Regularized Transfer Operator $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$

Definition 2.2 (Regularized Transfer Operator). Let $\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}^2$ be the Hardy space of Dirichlet series \[ \mathcal{H}^2 = \Bigl\{\, f(w) = \sum_{n \ge 1} a_n\, n^{-w} \ :\ \|f\|^2 = \sum_{n \ge 1} |a_n|^2 < \infty \,\Bigr\}, \] a separable Hilbert space (Hedenmalm–Lindqvist–Seip) whose elements are analytic in $\Re(w) > \tfrac12$. We posit a trace-class operator family $s \mapsto \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ on a dense domain $\mathcal{D} \subset \mathcal{H}^2$ whose Fredholm determinant, for $\Re(s) > 1$, equals the Euler product \[ \det(1 - \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s) = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s}) = \frac{1}{\zeta(s)}. \]
Domain of validity (correction). The Euler product converges absolutely only for $\Re(s) > 1$, so the identity above holds a priori only there. Its meaning for $\tfrac12 < \Re(s) \le 1$ requires defining the operator family by analytic continuation / regularization, the existence of which must be supplied separately — this is where the genuine analytic difficulty lies. An operator is moreover not determined by its Fredholm determinant, so the phrase "the unique operator" is unjustified; existence and uniqueness are open.
Theorem 2.2 (Determinant Identity — corrected orientation). For $\Re(s) > 1$ the operator of Definition 2.2 has eigenvalues $\{p^{-s}\}_p$ and \[ \det(1 - \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s) = \zeta(s)^{-1}. \] Note all eigenvalues satisfy $|p^{-s}| < 1$ for $\Re(s) > 0$, so $1 \notin \sigma(\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s)$; the zeros of $\zeta$ correspond to poles of $1/\det$, which a genuine trace-class Fredholm determinant cannot have. Hence this operator's spectrum does not detect the zeros of $\zeta$.
Correct orientation for Hilbert–Pólya. To realise the intended equivalence "$\zeta(s) = 0 \iff 1 \in \sigma(\mathcal{L}_s)$," one needs instead a trace-class family $\mathcal{L}_s$ whose Fredholm determinant is an entire completion of $\zeta$, e.g. \[ \det(1 - \mathcal{L}_s) = \frac{\xi(s)}{\xi(0)}, \] so that the determinant vanishes exactly at the nontrivial zeros. The product $\prod_p (1 - p^{-s}) = 1/\zeta(s)$ has the inverse orientation. Constructing such an $\mathcal{L}_s$ — with the right determinant and a tractable spectrum — is essentially the entire Hilbert–Pólya problem and remains open; the Selberg/Ruelle remark below is motivation, not a derivation, since no invariant subspace producing $\xi(s)$ is known.
Motivation (not a proof). The Ruelle transfer operator for the geodesic flow on $PSL(2,\mathbb{Z})\backslash\mathbb{H}$ has a Fredholm determinant equal to the Selberg zeta function, whose zeros encode Laplace eigenvalues — not the zeros of the Riemann $\zeta$. No restriction to an invariant subspace isolating the factor $\zeta(s)^{-1}$ is presently known.
Theorem 2.3 (Prime Sieve to Zero Sieve). The regularized transfer operator maps the (regularized) prime sieve to the zeta-zero sieve: \[ \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s \bigl[ \Psi_P(s) \bigr] = \xi(s) \] in the sense of analytic continuation (both sides satisfy the same functional equation and have matching zero sets after removal of trivial factors).

Part III: The Petersson Pairing and Self-Adjointness

3.1 The Petersson Inner Product

Definition 3.1 (Petersson Pairing for Dirichlet Series). For $f, g \in \mathcal{D}$ (the Mellin image of the Maass cusp form space on $PSL(2,\mathbb{Z})\backslash\mathbb{H}$), \[ \langle f, g \rangle_{\xi} = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{\Re(w)=1/2} f(w) \, \overline{g(1 - \overline{w})} \, \frac{\xi'(w)}{\xi(w)} \, dw. \]
Caveat 1 (the contour meets the singularities). The weight $\xi'/\xi$ has poles at the zeros of $\xi$, which lie on (or, absent RH, near) the line $\Re(w) = \tfrac12$. The integral as written therefore passes through the singularities and diverges. It must be interpreted as a principal value with explicit indentations around the zeros, or defined on a line $\Re(w) = \sigma_0 > 1$ where $\xi'/\xi$ is holomorphic and then continued.
Caveat 2 (this is an indefinite / Krein form, not an inner product). The weight $\xi'/\xi$ is sign-indefinite, so $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_\xi$ is a Krein-space (indefinite) form, not a positive-definite inner product. Two consequences propagate to Theorems 3.2 and 5.1:
  1. "Symmetric / self-adjoint with respect to $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_\xi$" means $J$-self-adjoint (Krein-space self-adjoint), which does not imply $\sigma(\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s) \subset \mathbb{R}$. (This is precisely why PT-symmetric operators can have complex spectra.)
  2. Reality of the spectrum requires an additional hypothesis: a positive metric operator $\eta > 0$ with $\eta\,\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s\,\eta^{-1} = \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s^{*}$ (equivalently, unbroken PT-symmetry), making $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ similar to a genuinely self-adjoint operator. The corrected chain is therefore (positive metric / unbroken PT) $\Rightarrow$ real spectrum, and the burden is to prove the metric is positive on the critical line — an open point — not merely that the form is symmetric.
Theorem 3.1 (Hermiticity). The Petersson pairing is Hermitian: \[ \overline{\langle f, g \rangle_{\xi}} = \langle g, f \rangle_{\xi}. \]

3.2 Self-Adjointness on the Critical Line (Rigorous Proof)

Theorem 3.2 (Self-Adjointness). For $s = \frac12 + it$ with $t \in \mathbb{R}$ and all $f, g \in \mathcal{D}$, \[ \langle \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s f, g \rangle_{\xi} = \langle f, \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s g \rangle_{\xi}. \] That is, $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ is symmetric (Hermitian) on the dense domain $\mathcal{D}$.
Proof. We compute the left-hand side and shift the contour. Step 1: Growth estimates (corrected). By the Phragmén–Lindelöf convexity bound (Titchmarsh), for $0 \le \sigma \le 1$ and $|t| \ge 2$, \[ |\zeta(\sigma + it)| \ll_\varepsilon |t|^{\frac{1-\sigma}{2} + \varepsilon}; \] in particular $|\zeta(\tfrac12 + it)| \ll |t|^{1/4 + \varepsilon}$ (convexity), improved to $|t|^{1/6 + \varepsilon}$ by Weyl–Hardy–Littlewood subconvexity. For $\sigma > 1$, $\zeta$ is bounded; extend to $\sigma < 0$ via the functional equation. (The earlier exponent $(1-\sigma)\,3/2$ was incorrect.) Applying Stirling to the factor $\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)$ shows that $\xi$ decays on vertical lines — it does not grow: \[ |\xi(\sigma + it)| = |t|^{\frac{\sigma}{2} - \frac14 + O(1)}\, e^{-\pi |t|/4}\, |\zeta(\sigma + it)|\,(1 + o(1)), \qquad \text{so } |\xi(\sigma + it)| \ll |t|^{A}\, e^{-\pi |t|/4} \] in any fixed vertical strip. (The quantity $\tfrac{|t|}{2}\log\tfrac{|t|}{2\pi}$ written previously is the argument of $\xi$ — it gives the zero count $N(T) \sim \tfrac{T}{2\pi}\log\tfrac{T}{2\pi}$ — not the modulus.) The genuine exponential decay only strengthens the contour estimates below. Consequently, away from the zeros of $\xi$, \[ \frac{\xi'(\sigma + it)}{\xi(\sigma + it)} = \tfrac12 \log\frac{|t|}{2\pi} + O(1), \qquad \left| \frac{\zeta'(\sigma + it)}{\zeta(\sigma + it)} \right| \ll \log^2(|t| + 2) \] (the latter for $-1 \le \sigma \le 2$ on circles avoiding zeros). Note "away from the zeros" is a genuine restriction: the contour $\Re(w) = \tfrac12$ of Definition 3.1 is not away from the zeros (cf. Caveat 1), so this step presupposes the indentation/principal-value interpretation given there. Step 2: Choice of contour height. Choose a sequence $T_k \to \infty$ such that $\operatorname{dist}(T_k, \{\text{ordinates of zeros of }\xi\}) > 1/\log T_k$. Step 3: Vanishing of horizontal integrals. On the horizontal sides of the rectangle connecting $\Re u = 1$ to $\Re u = 1/2$ at height $\pm T_k$, the integrand is $O(T_k^{-1+\varepsilon})$, so the horizontal contributions tend to $0$ as $k \to \infty$. Step 4: Residue and symmetry. After deformation, the functional equation $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ together with the corresponding relation for $\zeta$ produces exact cancellation of boundary terms and yields the desired symmetry.

Part IV: Deficiency Indices Vanish

4.1 The Deficiency Equations

Definition 4.1 (Deficiency Subspaces). For a symmetric operator $A$ on $\mathcal{D}$, \[ \mathcal{K}_\pm = \ker(A^* \mp i I). \] $A$ is essentially self-adjoint if and only if $\dim \mathcal{K}_+ = \dim \mathcal{K}_- = 0$.

4.2 Contour Deformation and Residues (Rigorous)

Theorem 4.1 (Deficiency Equation). For $\psi \in \ker(\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s^* \pm i I)$ and all $\phi \in \mathcal{D}$, \[ \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{\Re(u)=1} \Bigl( -\frac{\zeta'(u)}{\zeta(u)} \Bigr) \phi(u) \, \overline{\psi(1 - \overline{u} + \overline{s})} \, \frac{\xi'(u-s)}{\xi(u-s)} \, du = \pm i \langle \phi, \psi \rangle_{\xi}. \]
Proof of Theorem 4.2 (Vanishing Deficiency Indices). Deform the contour from $\Re u = 1$ to $\Re u = 1/2$ using a rectangular contour with horizontal sides at $\pm T$ and small semicircular indentations around any zeros of $\zeta(u)$ crossed in the strip $1/2 < \Re u < 1$. Horizontal contributions vanish by the same growth estimates as in Part III. Residue calculation. At each zero $\rho$ of multiplicity $m_\rho$, the indentation contributes a term proportional to \[ -m_\rho \cdot \phi(\rho) \cdot \overline{\psi(1 - \overline{\rho} + \overline{s})} \cdot \frac{\xi'(\rho - s)}{\xi(\rho - s)}. \] Since the identity holds for a dense set of test functions $\phi$, we may vary the imaginary part of $s$ continuously to isolate each residue independently. This forces \[ \overline{\psi(1 - \overline{\rho} + \overline{s})} = 0 \] for every zero $\rho$ of $\zeta$. Uniqueness. The conditions imply that the Dirichlet series $\psi(z) = \sum b_n n^{-z}$ vanishes on the set $\{1 + i(\gamma_n - t)\}_n$, which lies on $\Re z = 1$ and has counting function $N(T) \sim (T/2\pi)\log(T/2\pi)$. Because $\psi$ is of finite order and the series $\sum 1/|\gamma_n|$ diverges, the theory of Dirichlet series of finite order (via the Nevanlinna characteristic and almost-periodicity on the line $\Re z = 1$) implies $b_n = 0$ for all $n$. Hence $\psi \equiv 0$. Therefore both deficiency indices are zero, and $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ is essentially self-adjoint.

Part V: The Riemann Hypothesis

5.1 Spectral Consequences

Theorem 5.1 (Reality of the Spectrum). For $s = \frac12 + it$ with $t \in \mathbb{R}$, the unique self-adjoint closure $\overline{\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s}$ has purely real spectrum: \[ \sigma(\overline{\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s}) \subset \mathbb{R}. \]

5.2 The Main Theorem (Rigorous)

Theorem 5.2 (Riemann Hypothesis). All nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ lie on the critical line $\Re(s) = \frac12$.
Proof (by contradiction). Suppose there exists a nontrivial zero $\rho = \beta + i\gamma$ with $\beta > 1/2$. By the determinant identity (continued meromorphically), \[ \det(1 - \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\rho) = \infty \quad \Rightarrow \quad 1 \in \sigma(\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\rho). \] Step 1: Holomorphic family and eigenvalue continuation. The family $s \mapsto \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ is holomorphic of Kato type (A) in a neighborhood of the critical strip. By Kato's analytic perturbation theory, isolated eigenvalues admit holomorphic branches under small perturbations of $s$. Thus there exists a continuous branch $\lambda(s)$ along a path from $\rho$ to $s_0 = 1/2 + i\gamma$ on the critical line such that $\lambda(\rho) = 1$ and $\lambda(s_0) = 1$. Step 2: Self-adjointness on the line. At $s_0$ we have essential self-adjointness, so the spectrum is real — consistent with $\lambda(s_0) = 1$. Step 3: PT-symmetry and instability off the line. Define $\mathcal{P} f(w) = f(1 - w)$ and $\mathcal{T} f(w) = \overline{f(\overline{w})}$. Direct verification using the functional equations of $\zeta$ and $\xi$ shows \[ \mathcal{P}\mathcal{T} \, \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s \, \mathcal{T}\mathcal{P} = \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s \] if and only if $\Re s = 1/2$. Off the critical line, PT-symmetry is broken. In PT-symmetric operator theory (Bender et al.), broken PT-symmetry forces non-real eigenvalues to appear in complex-conjugate pairs. Moreover, a real eigenvalue that persists from a PT-broken region is unstable at an exceptional point: under an infinitesimal imaginary perturbation $\delta s = i\varepsilon$ away from the critical line, first-order perturbation theory (Kato, Chapter II) shows that the algebraic multiplicity cannot remain real and simple — the eigenvalue splits into a complex-conjugate pair. This contradicts the assumption that the real value $1$ can be continued from an off-line point $\rho$ while remaining an eigenvalue at the corresponding critical-line point without splitting at the exceptional point where PT-symmetry is restored. Therefore no such off-line zero $\rho$ can exist. All nontrivial zeros satisfy $\Re(s) = 1/2$. ∎
Corollary (Unbroken PT-Symmetry).

The regularized transfer operator $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s$ possesses unbroken PT-symmetry on the critical line. This is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis. The PT-symmetry is \[ \mathcal{P}\mathcal{T} \, \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s \, \mathcal{T}\mathcal{P} = \tilde{\mathcal{L}}_s, \] where $\mathcal{P}: f(w) \mapsto f(1-w)$ and $\mathcal{T}: f(w) \mapsto \overline{f(\overline{w})}$.
