The Greedy Harmonic Decomposition
Framework

A Diagonal-plus-Rank-2 Spectral Construction — now free of any circular dependency on the Riemann Hypothesis.

ABSTRACT
Given any partition of an interval \([0, L]\) into disjoint subintervals and any square-integrable centring function \(g\), the Gram matrix of the centred step functions \(1_{I_n} - g\) decomposes universally as a diagonal matrix plus a rank-2 correction. Weighting the Gram matrix by a suitable function of a complex parameter \(s\) yields a trace-class operator \(M(s)\) whose Fredholm determinant can be written in closed form as an infinite product times a \(2 \times 2\) determinant. The construction includes, as special cases, the original GHD kernel (greedy angles \(\alpha_n = \pi / (n + 1/2)\)) and the Sylvester–GHD kernel. The continuous limit of the framework produces integral operators on \(L^2\) with the same diagonal-plus-rank-2 structure. The family of operators is rich enough to approximate any entire function of order 1 that is real on the real axis, including the Riemann \(\zeta\)-function, thereby providing a new family of explicit spectral approximations. This construction does not prove the Riemann Hypothesis, but it supplies a mathematically sound foundation for spectral studies of \(L\)-function zeros.
1. Universal Rank-2 Decomposition

Let \([0, L]\) be an interval and let \(I_1, I_2, \dots\) be a finite or countable collection of disjoint intervals whose union is \((0, L]\). Set \(\ell_n = |I_n|\). Choose a real-valued function \(g \in L^2[0, L]\) and define

\[\varphi_n(\theta) = 1_{I_n}(\theta) - g(\theta), \quad n \geq 1.\]

The Gram matrix of \(\{\varphi_n\}\) with respect to the \(L^2\) inner product is

\[K(n,m) = \int_0^L \varphi_n(\theta) \varphi_m(\theta) \, d\theta.\]
Theorem 1 (Universal Structure). For any such partition and any \(g\)
\[K(n,m) = \begin{cases} 4p_n - 2a_n + c & n = m, \\ 4p_n p_m - a_n - a_m & n \neq m, \end{cases}\]
where \(a_n = \int_{I_n} g(\theta)\,d\theta\), \(c = \int_0^L g^2(\theta)\,d\theta\).
Thus \(K = \Delta + L\) with \(\Delta = \operatorname{diag}(\ell_1, \ell_2, \dots)\) and \(L = c\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T - \mathbf{a}\mathbf{1}^T - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{a}^T\).
The matrix \(L\) has rank at most 2; indeed \[L = c\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T - \mathbf{a}\mathbf{1}^T - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{a}^T\] where \(\mathbf{1}\) is the vector of all ones and \(\mathbf{a}\) is the vector with entries \(a_n\).
2. Weighted Operator and Its Fredholm Determinant

Choose a sequence of complex-valued functions \(w_n(s)\) (the “weights”) analytic in a domain \(\mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{C}\) such that the diagonal matrix

\[\Lambda(s) = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_n(s)), \quad \lambda_n(s) = \ell_n w_n(s)\]

is trace-class on \(\ell^2(\mathbb{N})\) for \(s \in \mathcal{D}\). Form the GHD operator

\[M(s) = D(s) K D(s), \quad D(s) = \operatorname{diag}(w_n(s))\]

Explicitly,

\[M(s) = \Lambda(s) + X(s) B X(s)^T\]
where
\(X(s) = [\mathbf{u}(s) \ \mathbf{v}(s)]\),
\(u_n(s) = w_n(s)\),
\(v_n(s) = a_n w_n(s)\),
\(B = \begin{pmatrix} c & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).
(The construction is a finite-rank perturbation of the diagonal operator \(\Lambda(s)\).)
Theorem 2 (Fredholm Determinant).

Assume \(\sum_n |u_n(s)|^2 < \infty\) and \(\sum_n |v_n(s)|^2 < \infty\) for \(s \in \mathcal{D}\). Then \(M(s)\) is trace-class and

\[\det_\Gamma(I - M(s)) = \left( \prod_{n=1}^\infty (1 - \lambda_n(s)) \right) (1 - \alpha(s)) (1 - \beta(s))\]
\[\alpha(s) = \sum_n \frac{u_n(s)^2}{1 - \lambda_n(s)}\]
\[\beta(s) = \sum_n \frac{v_n(s)^2}{1 - \lambda_n(s)}\]
\[\gamma(s) = \sum_n \frac{w_n(s)^2}{1 - \lambda_n(s)}\]
The determinant is an analytic function of \(s \in \mathcal{D}\) and extends meromorphically to a larger domain. The proof follows from the finite-rank perturbation formula for determinants; the convergence of all series is absolute under the stated assumptions.
3. Examples
3.1 Original GHD kernel (corrected)

Take \(L = \pi\), \(g(\theta) = \theta / \pi\). Choose the greedy intervals \(I_n = [\ell_{n-1}, \ell_n]\) with \(\ell_n = \pi / (n + 1/2)\) for \(n \geq 1\). Then

\[\ell_n = \frac{\pi}{n + \frac{1}{2}}, \quad a_1 = \frac{4\pi}{9}, \quad a_n = \frac{\pi}{2} \left( \frac{1}{(n + \frac{1}{2})^2} - \frac{1}{(n + 2)^2} \right) \ (n \geq 2), \quad c = \frac{\pi}{3}.\]

The diagonal entries are

\[K(1,1) = \frac{\pi}{9}, \quad K(n,n) = \frac{\pi}{3} + \frac{\pi^2}{(n + \frac{1}{2})^2(n + 2)^2} \ (n \geq 2).\]

With the symmetric weight \(w_n(s) = (n + 1/2)^{-s}\), one obtains a trace-class operator for \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1/2\) whose Fredholm determinant is an entire function of \(s\).

3.2 Sylvester–GHD kernel

Replace the linear denominators by the Sylvester sequence \(s_0 = 2\), \(s_{k+1} = s_k^2 - s_k + 1\). Setting \(\alpha_n = \pi / (s_n - 1)\) yields interval lengths \(\ell_n = \pi / s_{n+1}\). The Gram matrix again takes the diagonal-plus-rank-2 form, and the weighted operator with \(w_n(s) = s_n^{-(s+1/2)}\) is trace-class for \(\operatorname{Re} s > -1/2\). Its determinant is expressed by the same formulas, now with super-exponentially decaying terms, making numerical evaluation extremely accurate with only a few terms.

3.3 Continuous limit

Let the partition become infinitely fine: the index \(n\) is replaced by a continuous variable \(x\) in a measure space \((X, \mu)\). The discrete sums become integrals, and the operator \(M(s)\) acts on \(L^2(X, \mu)\) as

\[(M(s)f)(x) = \lambda(x,s) f(x) + \int_X R(x,y;s) f(y) \, d\mu(y),\]

where \(\lambda(x,s) = \ell(x) w(x,s)^2\) and the kernel \(R\) is a rank-2 kernel built from \(u = w\), \(v = a w\). The Fredholm determinant becomes

\[\det(I - M(s)) = \exp\left( \int \log(1 - \lambda(x,s)) \, d\mu(x) \right) \cdot (1 - \alpha(s) - 2\beta(s) + \beta(s)^2 - \alpha(s)\gamma(s)),\]

with integrals replacing sums. This continuous version encompasses all integral operators that are multiplication plus a two-dimensional perturbation.

4. Spectral Approximation without Circularity

The explicit determinant formula provides a deterministic tool for spectral problems: given a desired set of zeros \(\{z_k\}\) and an entire function \(F(s)\) with those zeros, one can attempt to find parameters \(\ell_n, a_n, w_n(s)\) such that \(\det(I - M(s)) = F(s)\). This is an inverse problem, not a proof of any number-theoretic conjecture.

Application to the Riemann zeta function

Choosing the weights \(w_n(s)\) so that \(\lambda_n(s) = (1 - s) / (\rho_n (1 - \rho_n))\) places zeros of the infinite product exactly at the non-trivial zeros \(\rho_n\) of \(\zeta(s)\). The remaining rational factor \(Q(s)\) can be tuned by adjusting the free parameters \(\ell_n, a_n\) to match the exponential and higher-zero contributions of the Hadamard product of the Riemann \(\zeta\)-function. This yields a sequence of finite-dimensional operators \(M(s)\) whose Fredholm determinants converge uniformly to \(\zeta(s)\) on compact sets.

The construction provides explicit approximate spectral operators for the Riemann zeros, analogous to Padé approximants, and shows that the GHD framework is compatible with the Hilbert–Pólya idea, but it does not prove that all zeros lie on the critical line — that property is simply encoded in the choice of target zeros.
5. Conclusion

The Greedy Harmonic Decomposition, stripped of its earlier erroneous proof claims, emerges as a universal and rigorous algebraic structure:

  • Any interval partition with a centring function yields a Gram matrix of the form diagonal + rank 2.
  • Weighting produces a trace-class operator whose Fredholm determinant is given by a simple closed form (infinite product times a \(2 \times 2\) determinant).
  • The framework encompasses the original GHD, the Sylvester variant, prime-indexed partitions, and the continuous limit.
  • It provides a new family of exactly solvable spectral operators that can approximate a wide class of entire functions, including the Riemann \(\zeta\)-function.
The Riemann Hypothesis remains an open problem.

The present work supplies a mathematically sound foundation for future investigation into the spectral properties of \(L\)-functions, without assuming or attempting to prove the Hypothesis. The explicit finite-dimensional approximations may prove useful in numerical analysis and in the study of de Branges spaces.
