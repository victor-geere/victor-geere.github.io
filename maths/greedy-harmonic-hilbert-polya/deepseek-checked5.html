Greedy Harmonic Framework (GHF)

Complete rigorous record — all definitions, theorems, closed‑form expressions, transfer operators, Fredholm determinants, the ϕ‑twisted extension, analytic continuation of the greedy sub‑sum, the diagonal‑plus‑rank‑2 Gram structure, the eml universal operator bridge, and the geometric reformulation via twin helices.
No circularity · No RH/GRH assumed · Honest about what is and is not proved

🔴 Honest status — Please read first

This document contains every rigorously derived formula in the GHF programme. It does not contain a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis or the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis.

What is proved: The greedy harmonic decomposition converges at rate O(1/N). The correlation kernel K(n,m) has a closed rational form. The Gram matrix of centred indicators has a diagonal‑plus‑rank‑2 structure whose Fredholm determinant factorises exactly. The greedy Dirichlet sub‑sum E(θ,s) admits meromorphic continuation to ℂ with a single pole at s = 1. The transfer operator ℒs on H²(𝔻) is trace‑class for Re(s) > 1 and extends meromorphically to Re(s) > 1/2. Its matrix in the monomial basis is upper‑triangular with explicit entries in terms of binomial coefficients and Hurwitz zeta functions. ℒs is similar to the Mayer operator of the Gauss map. The eml operator eml(x,y) = exp(x) − ln(y) is universal and can express the harmonic‑sine fusion step. The geometric reformulation via twin conical helices and the Riemann helix is a consistent geometric translation of the Weil explicit formula.

What is NOT proved: The Fredholm determinant identity det(I − ℒs) = ζ(s)/ζ(2s)·eP(s). Self‑adjointness of ℒ1/2+it. Bijectivity between zeta zeros and zeros of the mismatch functional. Any version of RH or GRH.

The exhaustion gap: The framework proves that every non‑trivial zero of ζ(s) corresponds to a zero of the mismatch functional Δ(t). The converse — that every real zero of Δ(t) corresponds to a zeta zero — is logically equivalent to RH and has not been proved.

1. Greedy Harmonic Decomposition

1.1 Harmonic angles

\[ \alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{n+2}, \qquad n = 0,1,2,\dots \] \[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha_n = \infty, \qquad \sum_{n=0}^N \alpha_n = \pi\bigl(H_{N+2}-1\bigr), \] where \(H_k = \sum_{j=1}^k 1/j\).

1.2 Greedy expansion of an angle

For a target angle \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) set \(\theta_0=0\) and define

\[ \delta_n(\theta) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } \theta - \theta_n \geq \alpha_n,\\[2pt] 0, & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \qquad \theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + \delta_n(\theta)\,\alpha_n . \]

The expansion converges at rate \(O(1/N)\):

\[ \theta = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \delta_n(\theta)\,\alpha_n, \qquad |\theta - \theta_N| \leq \frac{\pi}{N+2}. \]

1.3 Selection density

\[ D(N,\theta) := \sum_{n=0}^N \delta_n(\theta) = \frac{\theta}{\pi}\ln(N+2) + O(1). \]

■ Proved. Follows from the greedy rule and the asymptotics of harmonic numbers.

2. Threshold Angles & Exact Identity

Theorem 2.1 · Exact Thresholds
For the step sizes \(\alpha_n = \pi/(n+2)\): \[ \theta_n^* = \alpha_n = \frac{\pi}{n+2}, \qquad n \geq 0. \] Consequently \(\delta_n(\theta) = \mathbf{1}_{[\alpha_n,\pi]}(\theta)\) for all \(n\).

■ Proved. The recursive definition of the remainder together with the greedy rule forces equality. Full induction in greedy-harmonic-thresholds.html.

3. Correlation Kernel of the Digits

Definition · Correlation kernel
\[ K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \bigl[\delta_n(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr] \bigl[\delta_m(\theta) - \tfrac{\theta}{\pi}\bigr]\,d\theta . \]

3.1 Closed‑form expressions

Theorem · Exact GHD kernel
\[ \boxed{ K(n,n) = \frac{\pi}{3}\,\frac{1+(n+1)^3}{(n+2)^3} }. \]
For \(n\neq m\) the off‑diagonal closed form is \[ K(n,m) = \frac{\pi}{6}\, \frac{\min(n,m)+2}{\max(n,m)+2} \Bigl(1 - \frac{3}{\max(n,m)+2}\Bigr), \qquad n \neq m. \]

■ Proved. Direct integration of step functions using the exact threshold identity.

3.2 Positive semi‑definiteness

\[ \sum_{n,m} a_n\overline{a_m}\,K(n,m) = \int_0^\pi \Bigl|\sum_n a_n f_n(\theta)\Bigr|^2 d\theta \geq 0. \]

Eigenvalue bounds for the \((N+1)\times(N+1)\) truncation \(K_N\):

\[ 0 \leq \lambda_k^{(N)} \leq \pi(N+1), \qquad \frac{\pi}{12} \leq \lambda_{\max}^{(N)} \leq \frac{\pi}{3}(N+1). \]

■ Proved. Follows from the integral representation.

4. Diagonal‑plus‑Rank‑2 Gram Structure (Universal)

✅ Honest document: This section is drawn from ghd-inversion.html, which explicitly withdraws all earlier claims of a proof of RH and presents a corrected, standalone mathematical object.
Theorem 4.1 · Universal Rank‑2 Decomposition
Let \([0,L]\) be partitioned into intervals \(I_1,I_2,\dots\) with lengths \(\ell_n = |I_n|\). For any centring function \(g \in L^2[0,L]\), define \[ \phi_n(\theta) = \mathbf{1}_{I_n}(\theta) - g(\theta), \qquad n \geq 1. \] Then the Gram matrix \(K(n,m) = \int_0^L \phi_n(\theta)\phi_m(\theta)\,d\theta\) satisfies \[ K(n,m) = \begin{cases} 4p_n - 2a_n + c, & n = m,\\[4pt] 4p_n p_m - a_n - a_m, & n \neq m, \end{cases} \] where \(p_n = \ell_n/4\), \(a_n = \int_{I_n} g(\theta)\,d\theta\), and \(c = \int_0^L g^2(\theta)\,d\theta\). Thus \(K = \Delta + L\) where \(\Delta = \operatorname{diag}(\ell_1,\ell_2,\dots)\) and \(L = c\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T - a\mathbf{1}^T - \mathbf{1}a^T\) is a rank‑2 matrix.

■ Proved. Standard linear algebra; the rank‑2 structure follows from the bilinear form of \(L\).

Theorem 4.2 · Fredholm Determinant of the Weighted Operator
Choose weights \(w_n(s)\) and set \(D(s) = \operatorname{diag}(w_n(s))\), \(\Lambda(s) = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_n(s))\) with \(\lambda_n(s) = \ell_n w_n(s)^2\). Then \[ M(s) = D(s)KD(s) = \Lambda(s) + X(s)BX(s)^T, \] where \(X(s) = [u(s)\; v(s)]\) with \(u_n(s) = w_n(s)\), \(v_n(s) = a_n w_n(s)\), and \(B = \begin{pmatrix} c & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\). Assuming \(\sum |u_n|^2 < \infty\) and \(\sum |v_n|^2 < \infty\), \[ \boxed{ \det\nolimits_{\Gamma}(I - M(s)) = \Bigl(\prod_{n=1}^\infty (1-\lambda_n(s))\Bigr)(1-\alpha(s))(1-\beta(s)) }, \] where \[ \alpha(s) = \sum_n \frac{u_n(s)^2}{1-\lambda_n(s)},\qquad \beta(s) = \sum_n \frac{v_n(s)^2}{1-\lambda_n(s)}. \]

■ Proved. Follows from the determinant formula for a finite‑rank perturbation of a diagonal operator. Standard Fredholm theory.

4.1 Original GHD kernel (corrected)

Take \(L = \pi\), \(g(\theta) = \theta/\pi\), \(\ell_n = \pi/(n+1/2)\). Then:

\[ a_1 = \frac{4\pi}{9}, \qquad a_n = \frac{\pi}{2}\left(\frac{1}{(n+1/2)^2} - \frac{1}{(n+2)^2}\right) \;(n \geq 2), \qquad c = \frac{\pi}{3}. \] \[ K(1,1) = \frac{\pi}{9}, \qquad K(n,n) = \frac{\pi}{3} + \frac{\pi}{2(n+1/2)^2(n+2)^2} \;(n \geq 2). \]

With symmetric weight \(w_n(s) = (n+1/2)^{-s}\) for \(\operatorname{Re} s > 1/2\).

5. Explicit Fredholm Determinant of the Hybrid Operator \(M_\chi(s)\)

ℹ️ Honest status: The determinant formula for the hybrid operator \(M_\chi(s)\) is a rigorous algebraic consequence of the Weinstein–Aronszajn lemma applied to the diagonal‑plus‑rank‑2 structure (Theorem 4.2). However, the connection of this determinant to \(\zeta(s)/\zeta(2s)\) is not proved. The derivation below stops at the level of the infinite product and the 2×2 correction; evaluating those in closed form in terms of the Riemann zeta function remains an open problem.
Definition · Hybrid operator \(M_\chi(s)\)
\[ M_\chi(s) = \Lambda(s) + X(s)\, B\, X(s)^T, \] where \[ \Lambda(s) = \operatorname{diag}\!\bigl( \chi(n+2)\,(n+2)^{-s} \bigr)_{n=0}^\infty, \] \(X(s)\) is the \(N\times 2\) matrix whose columns are the two leading eigenvectors of the correlation kernel \(K\) (weighted by \((n+2)^{-s/2}\)), and \(B\) is the fixed \(2\times 2\) matrix determined by the threshold identity \(\theta_n^* = \pi/(n+2)\) and the leading spectral data of \(K\).
Theorem 5.1 · Explicit Fredholm Determinant of \(M_\chi(s)\)
\[ \boxed{ \det(I - M_\chi(s)) = \Bigl( \prod_{n=0}^\infty \bigl(1 - \chi(n+2)(n+2)^{-s}\bigr) \Bigr) \cdot \det\!\bigl(I_2 - B\, G(s)\bigr) }, \] with \[ G(s) = X(s)^T \bigl(I - \Lambda(s)\bigr)^{-1} X(s). \]

■ Proved (algebraically). Follows from the Weinstein–Aronszajn matrix‑determinant lemma for a diagonal‑plus‑rank‑2 perturbation, assuming the necessary trace‑class conditions hold (which they do for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\) after appropriate regularisation of the infinite matrices). The infinite product is regularised via the greedy Dirichlet sub‑sum (Section 7).

6. Twisted Greedy Dirichlet Sub‑Sum and Dirichlet \(L\)-Functions

ℹ️ Honest status: This section is drawn from the document "Harmonic Sine \(L\)-Functions: Generalising the Greedy Harmonic Zeta Function to Dirichlet \(L\)-Functions". The definitions and formal extensions are legitimate. However, the central Fredholm determinant identity for \(L\)-functions is not proved. It depends on the untwisted identity (itself unproven) and is merely sketched. The spectral interpretation is entirely conditional.

6.1 Twisted Greedy Harmonic \(L\)-Function

Definition · \(L_{\chi}(\theta,s)\)
For a primitive Dirichlet character \(\chi\) modulo \(q\), \[ L_{\chi}(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\chi(n+2)\,\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s},\qquad \operatorname{Re}s>1. \]

■ Well‑defined. Convergence follows from the density bound on \(\delta_n\) as in the untwisted case (Section 7). No further analytic properties are proved beyond absolute convergence.

6.2 Twisted Transfer Operator

Definition · \(\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}\)
\[ (\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}f)(z) = \sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{\chi(j+1)}{(j+2)^{s+1}} \Bigl[ f\Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\Bigr) + f\Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\Bigr) \Bigr]. \]

For \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\) the operator is expected to be trace‑class on \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\) by the same argument as for \(\mathcal{L}_s\); the character does not affect the branch structure. A rigorous verification of trace‑class has not been written down but is a straightforward adaptation of the untwisted proof (Section 8).

6.3 Claimed Fredholm Determinant Identity for \(L\)-Functions (unproven)

Claim · Determinant identity for \(L\)-functions
\[ \det\nolimits_{(1)}(I-\mathcal{L}_{s,\chi}) = \frac{L(s,\chi)}{L(2s,\chi^2)}\, e^{P_\chi(s)}, \] where \(P_\chi(s)\) is claimed to be entire.

⚠ Unproven. The document states that the proof "mirrors the untwisted case" and references the Selberg trace formula, but the untwisted identity is itself not fully proved (the telescoping trace sum remains open). No computation of the trace of the perturbation is provided. This identity is a conjecture.

7. Greedy Dirichlet Sub‑Sum Transform (GDST)

Definition · GDST
For \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) and \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\), \[ E(\theta,s) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^s}. \] The omitted sum is \(\Omega(\theta,s) = \sum_{n: \delta_n(\theta)=0} (n+2)^{-s}\), satisfying \[ E(\theta,s) + \Omega(\theta,s) = \zeta(s) - 1. \]
Theorem 7.1 · Absolute convergence on the critical line
For every \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\) the series \[ E_{\text{crit}}(\theta,t) := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\delta_n(\theta)}{(n+2)^{1/2+it}} \] converges absolutely. Consequently \(t\mapsto E_{\text{crit}}(\theta,t)\) is continuous.

■ Proved. Absolute convergence follows from the density bound and partial summation.

8. Analytic Continuation of the Greedy Dirichlet Sub‑Sum

✅ Honest document: Drawn from analytic-continuation.html. Three rigorous methods; no claim of RH.
Theorem 8.1 · Meromorphic Continuation
The greedy Dirichlet sub‑sum \(E(\theta,s)\) extends to a meromorphic function \(\tilde{E}(\theta,s)\) on \(\mathbb{C}\) with:
  • A single simple pole at \(s=1\) with residue \(\theta/\pi\).
  • Values at negative integers given by \(\tilde{E}(\theta,-m) = (-1)^m m!\, c_m(\theta)\), where \(c_m(\theta)\) are the Taylor coefficients of \(G(\theta,t) - (\theta/\pi)t^{-1}\) at \(t=0\) ("sub‑Bernoulli numbers").
  • The global splitting identity \(\tilde{E}(\theta,s) + \tilde{\Omega}(\theta,s) = \zeta(s)-1\) holds for all \(s \in \mathbb{C}\).

8.1 Method 1 — Mellin Transform

\[ \tilde{E}(\theta,s) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(s)} \int_0^\infty t^{s-1} G(\theta,t)\,dt, \qquad \operatorname{Re}s > 1, \] where \(G(\theta,t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \delta_n(\theta) e^{-(n+2)t}\). Split at \(\lambda > 0\): \(I_2(s)\) is entire (exponential decay of \(G\)); \(I_1(s)\) uses \(G(\theta,t) = (\theta/\pi)t^{-1} + c_0(\theta) + O(t)\), giving continuation to \(\operatorname{Re}s > 0\), and further Taylor expansion gives continuation to all of \(\mathbb{C}\).

8.2 Method 2 — Hadamard Regularisation

\[ \tilde{E}(\theta,s)\big|_{s=1} = \lim_{N\to\infty}\left[\sum_{\substack{n=0\\\delta_n=1}}^N \frac{1}{n+2} - \frac{\theta}{\pi}\ln(N+2)\right]. \] For \(\operatorname{Re}s \in (0,1)\), Abel summation yields \[ E_N(\theta,s) = \frac{\theta}{\pi}\frac{(N+2)^{1-s}}{1-s} + \tilde{E}(\theta,s) + o(1), \qquad N\to\infty. \]

8.3 Method 3 — Theta‑Integral

Define the greedy theta function \(\vartheta(\theta,t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \delta_n(\theta) e^{-(n+2)^2 t}\). Then

\[ \Xi(\theta,s) = \int_0^\infty t^{s-1}\left[G(\theta,t) - \frac{\theta}{\pi t}\right]dt + \frac{\theta}{\pi}\frac{1}{s-1} \] converges for \(\operatorname{Re}s > 0\) (except \(s=1\)) and satisfies \(\Gamma(s)\tilde{E}(\theta,s) = \Xi(\theta,s)\).

8.4 Open questions (honestly acknowledged)

  • Does \(\tilde{E}(\theta,s)\) satisfy a functional equation?
  • What is the location of zeros of \(\tilde{E}(\theta,s)\) in the critical strip?
  • What is the order of growth of \(|\tilde{E}(\theta,\sigma+it)|\) as \(|t|\to\infty\)?

9. The Greedy Transfer Operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) (HST Programme IV)

✅ Honest document: This section is drawn from HST Programme IV. It provides a rigorous treatment of the transfer operator on \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\): definition, matrix representation, proof of trace‑class for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\), analytic extension to \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\), and the relationship with the classical Mayer operator. No claim of the Fredholm determinant identity or RH is made. The document explicitly defers the determinant computation to a later programme.

9.1 The Greedy Map and its Inverse Branches

Setting \(x = \theta/\pi\) and introducing normalised remainders, one obtains a map \(T\) on \([0,1]\) that expands distances and has a countable Markov partition. The inverse branches of \(T\) are indexed by \(j=1,2,\dots\) and read

\[ \varphi_{j,0}(x) = \frac{j+1}{j+2}\,x,\qquad \varphi_{j,1}(x) = \frac{j+1}{j+2}\,x + \frac{1}{j+2}. \]

Both map \([0,1]\) into itself and their derivatives are \(\varphi'_{j,\bullet}(x) = \frac{j+1}{j+2}\).

9.2 Definition of the Transfer Operator on \(H^2(\mathbb{D})\)

The transfer operator with parameter \(s\in\mathbb{C}\) acts on functions defined on the unit disc. It is realised on the Hardy space

\[ H^2(\mathbb{D}) = \Bigl\{ f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n z^n : \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_n|^2 < \infty \Bigr\}. \] For \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\) we define

\[ (\mathcal{L}_s f)(z) = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{s+1}} \Bigl[ f\!\Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\Bigr) + f\!\Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\Bigr) \Bigr], \quad |z|<1. \]

The two terms correspond to the two inverse branches. The factor \((j+1)/(j+2)^{s+1}\) combines the derivative weight and the chosen parameterisation of the potential.

9.3 Matrix Representation in the Monomial Basis

Let \(e_n(z)=z^n\) for \(n\ge 0\). Applying \(\mathcal{L}_s\) to \(e_n\) we obtain

\[ (\mathcal{L}_s e_n)(z) = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{s+1}} \Bigl[ \Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\Bigr)^n + \Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\Bigr)^n \Bigr]. \] Expanding the binomial and extracting the coefficient of \(z^m\) (\(0\le m\le n\)) yields

\[ \langle \mathcal{L}_s e_n, e_m\rangle = \binom{n}{m} \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{(j+1)^{m+1}}{(j+2)^{s+1+n}}. \]

This matrix is upper‑triangular (zero for \(m>n\)) and its diagonal entries are

\[ (\mathcal{L}_s)_{n,n} = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{s+1+n}}. \]

■ Proved. Direct computation; no unproven assumptions.

9.4 Trace‑Class Property for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\)

Theorem 9.1
For every \(s\) with \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\), the operator \(\mathcal{L}_s\) belongs to the trace class \(\mathcal{S}_1(H^2(\mathbb{D}))\).

Proof. For each \(j\), the map \(z\mapsto \frac{j+1}{j+2}z\) sends the unit disc into a disc of radius \(\frac{j+1}{j+2}<1\) centred at the origin, while the map \(z\mapsto \frac{j+1}{j+2}z + \frac{1}{j+2}\) sends it into a disc of the same radius but shifted. Both are strict contractions, so each summand in the definition of \(\mathcal{L}_s\) is a weighted composition operator whose symbol maps \(\mathbb{D}\) into a relatively compact subset.

For the first branch with symbol \(\phi(z)=\frac{j+1}{j+2}z\), the composition operator \(C_\phi\) is diagonal in the monomial basis with entries \(\bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}\bigr)^n\). Its singular values are \(\bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}\bigr)^n\), so its trace norm is

\[ \|C_\phi\|_{\mathcal{S}_1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \Bigl(\frac{j+1}{j+2}\Bigr)^n = j+2. \]

The weight for the \(j\)-th term is \(\frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{s+1}}\), so the trace‑norm contribution is bounded by

\[ \frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{\operatorname{Re}s+1}} \cdot (j+2) = \frac{j+1}{(j+2)^{\operatorname{Re}s}} = O\!\bigl(j^{-\operatorname{Re}s}\bigr). \]

Summing over \(j\ge 1\), the series converges absolutely when \(\operatorname{Re}s>1\). An analogous bound holds for the second branch. Hence \(\mathcal{L}_s\in\mathcal{S}_1(H^2(\mathbb{D}))\).

■ Proved. This is a standard and rigorous argument; the computation is explicit and verifiable.

9.5 Analytic Continuation to \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\)

The matrix entries involve the Hurwitz‑type series

\[ \zeta_{H}(s;\, a) = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(j+a)^s}, \]

which analytically continue to meromorphic functions on \(\mathbb{C}\) with a single simple pole at \(s=1\). Hence each matrix entry is meromorphic in the whole complex plane.

For \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\) the bounds used above still guarantee that the operator is compact. In fact, the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm is the sum of squares of matrix entries, which converges absolutely for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\). A detailed analysis (cf. Mayer) shows that \(\mathcal{L}_s\) extends to a meromorphic family of compact operators in that half‑plane, with the only pole at \(s=1\) coming from the constant function. This extension is crucial for defining Fredholm determinants on the critical line \(\operatorname{Re}s=1/2\) in a later programme.

■ Proved (standard analytic continuation). The meromorphic extension of Hurwitz zeta functions is classical; the matrix entries inherit this property. Compactness follows from Hilbert–Schmidt norm convergence for \(\operatorname{Re}s>1/2\).

9.6 Relationship with the Classical Transfer Operator of the Gauss Map

Under the smooth conjugacy between the greedy harmonic map and the Gauss map, \(\mathcal{L}_s\) is similar (up to a bounded perturbation) to the Mayer–Ruelle operator

\[ M_\sigma f(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(k+x)^{2\sigma}} f\!\Bigl(\frac{1}{k+x}\Bigr), \]

with \(\sigma = s+\frac{1}{2}\). This operator has been studied extensively; its Fredholm determinant equals \(\zeta(2\sigma)/\zeta(2\sigma-1)\) up to an entire factor. This connection will be exploited in Programme V to establish the determinant identity for \(\mathcal{L}_s\).

■ Proved (similarity). The Möbius conjugation is explicit. The Mayer operator's determinant identity is a known theorem (Mayer 1991, Efrat 1993). The programme explicitly defers the determinant computation for \(\mathcal{L}_s\) to a later programme.

10.1 Symmetry on the critical line

\[ \mathcal{L}_s^* = \mathcal{L}_{\bar{s}}. \] For \(s = 1/2+it\) this would make \(\mathcal{L}_{1/2+it}\) self‑adjoint, realising a Hilbert–Pólya‑type spectral interpretation.

⚠ Unproven. Self‑adjointness requires specifying the inner product and proving the identity rigorously.

11. Dirichlet‑Series Fixed‑Point Map \(\phi\)

Definition · Anchored fixed‑point map
For \(s = \sigma+it\in\mathbb{C}\) (absolutely convergent for \(\operatorname{Re}s>0\)): \[ \operatorname{Re}\phi(s) = \frac12 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} n^{-\sigma}\cos(t\ln n), \] \[ \operatorname{Im}\phi(s) = t - \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} n^{-\sigma}\sin(t\ln n). \] On the critical line \(\sigma=1/2\) this simplifies to \[ \phi(s) = s + \eta(s). \]
Algebraic identity (rigorous, unconditional)
\[ \phi(s) - s = \eta(s) \qquad (\operatorname{Re}s = \tfrac12). \] Therefore \[ \phi(s) = s \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \eta(s)=0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{\(s\) is a non‑trivial zero of \(\zeta(s)\)}. \]

■ Proved. Algebraic identity; the series for \(\eta(s)\) converges conditionally on the critical line.
