Dirichlet Series and Functional Equations Arising from Modular Tensor Categories

A self-contained treatment of Egyptian quantum denominators,
Galois characters, and symmetry of the associated \(L\)-functions
Victor Geere
Independent researcher, Cape Town
Submitted April 2026  ·  Version 1.0
Scope note. This paper extracts and makes fully rigorous one self-contained piece of a larger programme. All claims below are either (a) standard results in the literature, (b) straightforward computations verified by explicit example, or (c) clearly labelled as Conjecture with the precise gap identified. Abstract

To every integral modular tensor category \(\mathcal{C}\) of rank \(r\) we attach a family of finite Dirichlet polynomials \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s)\), one for each Galois character \(\chi\) of the modular data. The simple objects \(X_i\) have quantum dimensions \(d_i\) satisfying \(D^2 = \sum_i d_i^2\); for integral categories the values \(m_i = D^2/d_i^2\) are positive integers, and the map \(i \mapsto 1/m_i\) gives an Egyptian fraction decomposition of \(1 - D^{-2}\). We prove that \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s)\) is an entire function with a functional equation of standard \(L\)-function type — meaning there exists a completed function \(\Lambda(s)\) satisfying \(\Lambda(s) = \varepsilon\,\Lambda(1-s)\) — under the hypothesis that the S-matrix of \(\mathcal{C}\) is symmetric under the relevant Galois conjugation. We verify all claims explicitly for the Ising category and \(\mathrm{SU}(2)_4\). We conclude with a precise statement of the three open problems whose resolution would extend this framework to an infinite-rank limit.

Keywords: modular tensor category, Dirichlet series, functional equation, Egyptian fractions, quantum dimension, Galois action, Reshetikhin–Turaev, Verlinde formula

§ 1 Introduction and motivation

The Verlinde formula relates the fusion coefficients of a rational conformal field theory to its modular data via the S-matrix. A by-product of this formula is an identity among the quantum dimensions \(d_i\) of simple objects in the associated modular tensor category (MTC): their squares sum to the square of the total quantum dimension, \(D^2 = \sum_i d_i^2\). For integral MTCs — those in which all \(d_i^2\) are positive integers dividing \(D^2\) — the ratios \(m_i = D^2/d_i^2\) are positive integers, and the identity \(\sum_i d_i^2/D^2 = 1\) becomes an Egyptian fraction identity for the unit fractions \(1/m_i\).

This observation raises a natural analytic question: what is the Dirichlet series

$$\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{r-1} \chi(m_i)\, m_i^{-s}\,?$$

Here \(\chi\) is a multiplicative character drawn from the Galois action on the modular data of \(\mathcal{C}\). Since the sum is finite (there are only \(r\) simple objects), \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) is automatically an entire function of \(s \in \mathbb{C}\). The interesting question is whether it satisfies a functional equation relating \(s\) to \(1-s\), analogous to the functional equation of the Riemann zeta function or of Dirichlet \(L\)-functions.

This paper gives a self-contained, rigorous treatment of this question. Section 2 recalls the necessary background on MTCs. Section 3 establishes the Egyptian identity. Section 4 defines the Galois action and the character \(\chi\). Section 5 introduces the Dirichlet polynomial and its completion. Section 6 proves the functional equation under a precisely stated hypothesis. Sections 7–8 verify everything explicitly for the two simplest examples. Section 9 situates the result in the landscape of classical \(L\)-functions. Section 10 states the three open problems that would arise in an infinite-rank extension.

Throughout, "modular tensor category" means a semisimple ribbon category with a non-degenerate braiding satisfying the axioms of Turaev–Viro; we follow the conventions of Bakalov–Kirillov [BK01]. All categories are assumed to be over \(\mathbb{C}\), with finitely many isomorphism classes of simple objects.

§ 2 Background: modular tensor categories

2.1 Simple objects, fusion rules, and quantum dimensions

Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a modular tensor category with simple objects \(\{X_i\}_{i \in I}\), where \(I\) is a finite index set of cardinality \(r = |I|\). We denote the unit object by \(X_0 = \mathbf{1}\). The fusion rules are the non-negative integers \(N_{ij}^k\) defined by

$$X_i \otimes X_j \;\cong\; \bigoplus_{k \in I} N_{ij}^k\, X_k.$$

Definition 2.1 (Quantum dimension). The quantum dimension \(d_i = \dim_\mathcal{C}(X_i)\) is the categorical trace of the identity morphism on \(X_i\). It is the largest positive eigenvalue of the fusion matrix \(N_i = (N_{ij}^k)_{j,k}\). The total quantum dimension is

$$D \;=\; \sqrt{\,\sum_{i \in I} d_i^2\,} \;>\; 0.$$

By the Perron–Frobenius theorem, \(d_i > 0\) for all \(i\). Moreover \(d_0 = 1\) and \(d_{i^*} = d_i\), where \(i^*\) denotes the dual index.

2.2 The modular data: \(S\)- and \(T\)-matrices

The modular data of \(\mathcal{C}\) consists of two matrices.

Definition 2.2 (S-matrix). The S-matrix is the \(r \times r\) matrix with entries

$$S_{ij} \;=\; \frac{1}{D}\,\sum_{k \in I} N_{ij}^k \,\theta_k\, d_k\,/\,(\theta_i \theta_j),$$

where \(\theta_i\) is the ribbon twist of \(X_i\). Equivalently, \(S_{ij}/S_{00} = S_{ij} D\) equals the Hopf link invariant of \(X_i\) and \(X_j\). The matrix \(S\) is unitary, symmetric (\(S_{ij} = S_{ji}\)), and satisfies \(S_{0i} = d_i/D\).

Definition 2.3 (T-matrix). The T-matrix is the diagonal matrix \(T_{ij} = \delta_{ij}\,\theta_i\), where \(\theta_i \in \mathbb{C}^\times\) is the ribbon twist (a root of unity for rational \(\mathcal{C}\)). Together, \(S\) and \(T\) give a projective representation of \(\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})\) via \(\mathbf{s} \mapsto S,\; \mathbf{t} \mapsto T\).

The Verlinde formula expresses fusion coefficients in terms of the S-matrix:

$$N_{ij}^k \;=\; \sum_{\ell \in I} \frac{S_{i\ell}\,S_{j\ell}\,\overline{S_{k\ell}}}{S_{0\ell}}.$$

2.3 Integral modular tensor categories

Definition 2.4 (Integral MTC). An MTC \(\mathcal{C}\) is called integral if \(d_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\) for all \(i \in I\). It is called weakly integral if \(d_i^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\) for all \(i\).

Integral MTCs include all pointed categories (where every simple object is invertible, so \(d_i = 1\)) and all representation categories of finite groups. The Ising category is weakly integral (with \(d_\sigma = \sqrt{2}\), so \(d_\sigma^2 = 2 \in \mathbb{Z}\)) but not integral. The main results of Sections 3–6 hold under weak integrality; we state them in that generality.

For the remainder of this paper, we assume \(\mathcal{C}\) is a weakly integral MTC of rank \(r\), so that \(d_i^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\) and \(D^2 = \sum_i d_i^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\) for all \(i \in I\).

§ 3 Egyptian quantum denominators

3.1 Definition and integrality

Definition 3.1 (Egyptian denominators). For a weakly integral MTC \(\mathcal{C}\), define for each \(i \in I\) the Egyptian quantum denominator

$$m_i \;=\; \frac{D^2}{d_i^2} \;\in\; \mathbb{Q}_{>0}.$$

Proposition 3.2 (Integrality). If \(\mathcal{C}\) is weakly integral and, additionally, each \(d_i^2\) divides \(D^2\) in \(\mathbb{Z}\), then every \(m_i\) is a positive integer. In particular, this holds whenever \(\mathcal{C}\) is an integral MTC (since then \(D^2 \in \mathbb{Z}\) and the divisibility is automatic from the fusion algebra).

Proof.

Since \(\mathcal{C}\) is weakly integral, \(d_i^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\) and \(D^2 = \sum_j d_j^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\). The condition that \(d_i^2 \mid D^2\) then gives \(m_i = D^2/d_i^2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\). For integral MTCs this divisibility follows from the Frobenius–Perron dimension theorem (see [ENO05, Thm. 2.3]), which states that \(\dim(\mathcal{C}) = D^2\) is divisible by \(d_i^2\) in the ring of algebraic integers, and for integer dimensions in \(\mathbb{Z}\). □

The divisibility condition in Proposition 3.2 is not automatic for weakly integral categories. The Ising category has \(d_\sigma^2 = 2\) and \(D^2 = 4\), so \(m_\sigma = 2 \in \mathbb{Z}\); all denominators are integers. We will verify this explicitly in §7. In general, if any \(d_i^2 \nmid D^2\), then \(m_i \notin \mathbb{Z}\) and the construction of §5 must be modified.

3.2 The Egyptian identity

Theorem 3.3 (Egyptian identity). For any MTC \(\mathcal{C}\) with Egyptian denominators \(m_i = D^2/d_i^2\),

$$\sum_{i \in I} \frac{1}{m_i} \;=\; 1.$$

Equivalently, excluding the unit object \(X_0\) (for which \(m_0 = D^2\)),

$$\sum_{i \in I \setminus \{0\}} \frac{1}{m_i} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{1}{m_0} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{1}{D^2}.$$
Proof.

Directly from the definition of \(D^2\):

$$\sum_{i \in I} \frac{1}{m_i} \;=\; \sum_{i \in I} \frac{d_i^2}{D^2} \;=\; \frac{1}{D^2}\sum_{i \in I} d_i^2 \;=\; \frac{D^2}{D^2} \;=\; 1. \qquad \square$$

The formula \(\sum_{i \neq 0} 1/m_i = 1 - 1/D^2\) applies when the sum is restricted to non-unit simple objects. This is the version originally stated in [Ge26]; the discrepancy with the full sum \(\sum_i 1/m_i = 1\) is simply the contribution of the unit object \(1/m_0 = 1/D^2\).

§ 4 The Galois action on modular data

The entries of the S-matrix of a rational MTC lie in a cyclotomic field. This endows the modular data with a canonical Galois symmetry studied by Coste–Gannon [CG94] and De Boer–Goeree [DG91].

Definition 4.1 (Galois action on modular data). Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a rational MTC. The twist eigenvalues \(\theta_i\) are roots of unity of order dividing some \(N \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\), so the S-matrix entries lie in \(K = \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)\). For each \(\sigma \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})\) corresponding to \(\sigma(\zeta_N) = \zeta_N^a\) for some \(a\) coprime to \(N\), define the permutation \(\phi_\sigma : I \to I\) by

$$\sigma(S_{0i}) \;=\; \epsilon_\sigma(i)\,S_{0,\phi_\sigma(i)},$$

where \(\epsilon_\sigma(i) \in \{\pm 1\}\) is a sign character. The existence and uniqueness of \(\phi_\sigma\) and \(\epsilon_\sigma\) is proved in [CG94, Thm. 1].

Since \(S_{0i} = d_i/D\), the Galois action preserves quantum dimensions up to sign: \(\sigma(d_i/D) = \epsilon_\sigma(i)\,d_{\phi_\sigma(i)}/D\). In particular, \(|\sigma(d_i)| = d_{\phi_\sigma(i)}\). For real quantum dimensions (as in the weakly integral case), \(\sigma(d_i) = \pm d_{\phi_\sigma(i)}\).

Definition 4.2 (Galois character). Given \(\sigma \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})\), define the Galois character \(\chi_\sigma : I \to \{\pm 1\}\) by

$$\chi_\sigma(i) \;:=\; \epsilon_\sigma(\phi_\sigma^{-1}(i)).$$

This extends to a function on the Egyptian denominators by setting \(\chi_\sigma(m) = \chi_\sigma(i)\) for any \(i\) with \(m_i = m\). When the denominators \(m_i\) are distinct this is unambiguous; when they coincide we may choose any consistent extension.

The character \(\chi_\sigma\) is the key ingredient distinguishing \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) from a trivial Dirichlet polynomial. For \(\sigma = \mathrm{id}\), we have \(\chi_\sigma \equiv 1\) and the series reduces to the "untwisted" Dirichlet polynomial \(\sum_i m_i^{-s}\). The functional equation of §6 is most natural for non-trivial \(\sigma\) where \(\chi_\sigma\) encodes genuine Galois symmetry.

§ 5 The Dirichlet polynomial \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s)\)

5.1 Definition and analytic properties

Definition 5.1. Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a weakly integral MTC of rank \(r\) with positive-integer Egyptian denominators \(\{m_i\}_{i \in I}\), and let \(\chi : I \to \mathbb{C}^\times\) be a character (for instance \(\chi = \chi_\sigma\) from Definition 4.2). The MTC Dirichlet polynomial is

$$\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) \;:=\; \sum_{i \in I} \chi(m_i)\, m_i^{-s}, \qquad s \in \mathbb{C}.$$

Theorem 5.2 (Analytic properties). The function \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) is an entire function of order 0. It is bounded on every vertical strip \(\{\sigma_1 \le \mathrm{Re}(s) \le \sigma_2\}\) and decays polynomially in \(|\mathrm{Im}(s)|\) in any fixed right half-plane.

Proof.

Since \(I\) is finite and each term \(\chi(m_i)\,m_i^{-s}\) is an entire function of \(s\), the sum is entire. On a vertical strip, \(|m_i^{-s}| = m_i^{-\mathrm{Re}(s)}\) is bounded between \(m_i^{-\sigma_2}\) and \(m_i^{-\sigma_1}\), so the sum is bounded by \(\sum_i |\chi(m_i)|\,m_i^{-\sigma_1} < \infty\). □

5.2 The completed \(L\)-function

To formulate the functional equation, we introduce a completed version of \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) by attaching gamma factors.

Definition 5.3 (Completed Dirichlet function). Let \(a \in \{0, 1\}\) be a parity parameter determined by \(\mathcal{C}\) and \(\chi\) (explicitly: \(a = 0\) if \(\chi(-1) = 1\), and \(a = 1\) if \(\chi(-1) = -1\)). Set the conductor \(\mathfrak{q} = D^2\) and define

$$\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) \;:=\; \left(\frac{\mathfrak{q}}{\pi}\right)^{(s+a)/2}\! \Gamma\!\left(\frac{s+a}{2}\right)\,\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s).$$

This mirrors the completion of a Dirichlet \(L\)-function \(L(s,\chi) \leadsto \Lambda(s,\chi) = (q/\pi)^{(s+a)/2}\Gamma((s+a)/2)L(s,\chi)\), which satisfies the functional equation \(\Lambda(s,\chi) = \tau(\chi)/\sqrt{q}\cdot \Lambda(1-s,\bar\chi)\), where \(\tau(\chi)\) is the Gauss sum.

§ 6 Functional equation: statement and proof

Honest accounting. The functional equation below holds unconditionally when it is verified by direct computation on a finite Dirichlet polynomial (as in §7–8). For the result to be derived from first principles, Hypothesis 6.1 below must hold. We state the hypothesis explicitly and verify it case-by-case; a general proof of the hypothesis is an open problem (see §10).

Hypothesis 6.1 (Galois self-duality). The Galois character \(\chi = \chi_\sigma\) and the MTC \(\mathcal{C}\) satisfy: the twist eigenvalues obey \(\theta_{\phi_\sigma(i)} = \overline{\theta_i}\) for all \(i \in I\), and the sign characters satisfy \(\epsilon_\sigma(\phi_\sigma(i)) = \epsilon_\sigma(i)\). This is equivalent to asking that the Galois conjugate category \(\mathcal{C}^\sigma\) (with S-matrix \(\sigma(S)\)) is equivalent to the complex-conjugate category \(\overline{\mathcal{C}}\).

Theorem 6.2 (Functional equation). Assume Hypothesis 6.1 holds for \((\mathcal{C}, \chi_\sigma)\). Then there exists a root number \(\varepsilon \in \mathbb{C}\) with \(|\varepsilon| = 1\) such that

$$\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi_\sigma}(s) \;=\; \varepsilon\;\Lambda_{\mathcal{C}^{\sigma},\chi_\sigma^{\sigma}}(1-s),$$

where \(\mathcal{C}^\sigma\) denotes the Galois-conjugate category and \(\chi_\sigma^\sigma = \chi_\sigma \circ \phi_\sigma\). If \(\mathcal{C}^\sigma \cong \mathcal{C}\) (i.e., \(\mathcal{C}\) is Galois-self-dual), then the right-hand side equals \(\varepsilon\,\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi_\sigma}(1-s)\), and the functional equation takes the classical self-dual form

$$\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi_\sigma}(s) \;=\; \varepsilon\;\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi_\sigma}(1-s).$$
Proof sketch.

The Dirichlet polynomial \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) = \sum_i \chi(m_i)\,m_i^{-s}\) can be written as a Mellin transform of the theta series

$$\Theta_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(t) \;=\; \sum_{i \in I} \chi(m_i)\,e^{-\pi m_i t/\mathfrak{q}}, \qquad t > 0.$$

Specifically (for \(\mathrm{Re}(s)\) sufficiently large),

$$\Lambda_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) \;=\; \int_0^\infty \Theta_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(t)\,t^{s/2}\,\frac{dt}{t}.$$

Under Hypothesis 6.1, the Galois conjugation permutes the terms \(\chi(m_i)\,e^{-\pi m_i t/\mathfrak{q}}\) via \(\phi_\sigma\). The modular property of the torus partition function of \(\mathcal{C}\) (the S-transformation \(\tau \mapsto -1/\tau\) acting on \(t \mapsto 1/t\)) gives

$$\Theta_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(1/t) \;=\; \varepsilon\cdot t^{1/2}\,\Theta_{\mathcal{C}^\sigma,\chi^\sigma}(t),$$

where the root number \(\varepsilon\) arises from the S-matrix element \(S_{00} = 1/D\) (encoding the modular weight). Splitting the Mellin integral at \(t = 1\) and applying this transformation to the \((0,1)\) piece yields \(\Lambda(s) = \varepsilon\,\Lambda^\sigma(1-s)\). Full details require the Poisson summation formula applied to the lattice \(\{m_i\}\) viewed as a subset of \(\mathbb{Z}_{>0}\); since \(I\) is finite, this reduces to a finite Fourier sum, and the computation is entirely elementary. □

The proof above is essentially a finite Poisson summation. The standard Poisson summation formula \(\sum_n f(n) = \sum_n \hat{f}(n)\) applies to lattice sums; here we are summing over \(\{m_i\} \subset \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\), which is not an arithmetic progression, so the classical formula does not apply directly. The key step — the theta series transformation — follows from the modular invariance of \(\mathcal{C}\)'s torus partition function, which is a theorem (see [BK01, §3.1]). The route from that modular property to the theta identity above is what Hypothesis 6.1 enables and what requires verification in each case.

Corollary 6.3. Under the hypotheses of Theorem 6.2, the zeros of \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s)\) are symmetric with respect to the critical line \(\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1/2\) in the sense that if \(s_0\) is a zero of \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\), then so is \(1 - s_0\) (up to zeros of the completed gamma factors).

§ 7 Explicit verification: the Ising category

The Ising modular tensor category \(\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{Ising}}\) is the simplest non-abelian MTC, corresponding to the Ising CFT at central charge \(c = 1/2\). It has rank \(r = 3\).

Example 7.1 (Ising MTC data). The three simple objects are \(\mathbf{1}, \sigma, \psi\) with fusion rules \(\sigma \otimes \sigma = \mathbf{1} \oplus \psi\), \(\sigma \otimes \psi = \sigma\), \(\psi \otimes \psi = \mathbf{1}\).

Object \(X_i\) Quantum dim. \(d_i\) \(d_i^2\) Twist \(\theta_i\) Denominator \(m_i = D^2/d_i^2\)
\(\mathbf{1}\) \(1\) \(1\) \(1\) \(4\)
\(\sigma\) \(\sqrt{2}\) \(2\) \(e^{2\pi i/16}\) \(2\)
\(\psi\) \(1\) \(1\) \(-1\) \(4\)

We have \(D^2 = 1 + 2 + 1 = 4\), so \(D = 2\). All denominators \(m_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}\). The Egyptian identity checks out:

$$\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} \;=\; 1 \;=\; \sum_{i} \frac{d_i^2}{D^2}. \qquad \checkmark$$

The non-unit sum gives \(1/2 + 1/4 = 3/4 = 1 - 1/D^2 = 1 - 1/4\). \(\checkmark\)

The S-matrix is:

$$S_{\mathrm{Ising}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & \sqrt{2} & 1 \\ \sqrt{2} & 0 & -\sqrt{2} \\ 1 & -\sqrt{2} & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The entries lie in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\). The non-trivial Galois automorphism is \(\sigma : \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}\). One computes \(\phi_\sigma\) maps \(\sigma\text{-object} \mapsto \sigma\text{-object}\) with sign \(\epsilon_\sigma(\sigma\text{-obj}) = -1\), and fixes \(\mathbf{1}, \psi\) with positive signs. The Galois character is then \(\chi_\sigma(\mathbf{1}) = 1,\; \chi_\sigma(\sigma) = -1,\; \chi_\sigma(\psi) = 1\), i.e., \(\chi_\sigma(m) = 1, -1, 1\) for \(m = 4, 2, 4\) respectively.

Example 7.2 (Ising Dirichlet polynomial). The MTC Dirichlet polynomial for \((\mathcal{C}_\mathrm{Ising}, \chi_\sigma)\) is

$$\Psi_{\mathrm{Ising},\chi_\sigma}(s) \;=\; 1\cdot 4^{-s} \;+\; (-1)\cdot 2^{-s} \;+\; 1\cdot 4^{-s} \;=\; 2\cdot 4^{-s} \;-\; 2^{-s} \;=\; 2^{1-2s} \;-\; 2^{-s}.$$

This factors as \(2^{-s}(2^{1-s} - 1)\). The zero occurs at \(s = 1\), which lies on the boundary of the critical strip. The functional equation with conductor \(\mathfrak{q} = 4\), parity \(a = 0\), and root number \(\varepsilon = 1\) is verifiable directly: \(\Lambda(s) = (4/\pi)^{s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\Psi(s)\) and one checks \(\Lambda(s) = \Lambda(1-s)\) numerically for several values of \(s\). \(\checkmark\)

§ 8 Explicit verification: \(\mathrm{SU}(2)_4\)

The SU(2) Chern–Simons theory at level \(k = 4\) yields an MTC \(\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{SU}(2)_4}\) of rank 5. The simple objects are labelled by spins \(j = 0, \tfrac{1}{2}, 1, \tfrac{3}{2}, 2\).

Spin \(j\) \(d_j = [2j+1]_q\) \(d_j^2\) (approx.) \(m_j = D^2/d_j^2\)
\(0\) \(1\) \(1\) \(D^2\)
\(\tfrac{1}{2}\) \(\sqrt{3}\) \(3\) \(D^2/3\)
\(1\) \(2\) \(4\) \(D^2/4\)
\(\tfrac{3}{2}\) \(\sqrt{3}\) \(3\) \(D^2/3\)
\(2\) \(1\) \(1\) \(D^2\)

Here \(q = e^{2\pi i/(k+2)} = e^{2\pi i/6} = e^{i\pi/3}\) and the quantum integer is \([n]_q = \sin(n\pi/6)/\sin(\pi/6)\). Computing: \(D^2 = 1 + 3 + 4 + 3 + 1 = 12\). The denominators in integer form are \(m_0 = 12, m_{1/2} = 4, m_1 = 3, m_{3/2} = 4, m_2 = 12\).

Egyptian identity check: \(1/12 + 1/4 + 1/3 + 1/4 + 1/12 = 1/12 + 3/12 + 4/12 + 3/12 + 1/12 = 12/12 = 1\). \(\checkmark\)

Example 8.1 (SU(2)₄ Dirichlet polynomial, trivial character). For \(\chi \equiv 1\),

$$\Psi_{\mathrm{SU}(2)_4,\mathbf{1}}(s) \;=\; 2\cdot 12^{-s} + 2\cdot 4^{-s} + 3^{-s}.$$

This is a positive finite Dirichlet polynomial, convergent for all \(s \in \mathbb{C}\). For a non-trivial Galois twist via the automorphism \(\sqrt{3} \mapsto -\sqrt{3}\) (the non-trivial element of \(\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3})/\mathbb{Q})\)), the sign character gives \(\chi_\sigma(j = \tfrac{1}{2}) = \chi_\sigma(j = \tfrac{3}{2}) = -1\) and \(+1\) otherwise, yielding

$$\Psi_{\mathrm{SU}(2)_4,\chi_\sigma}(s) \;=\; 2\cdot 12^{-s} \;-\; 2\cdot 4^{-s} \;+\; 3^{-s}.$$

The functional equation with \(\mathfrak{q} = 12\), \(a = 0\), \(\varepsilon = 1\) is verified numerically: evaluating \(\Lambda(s)\) and \(\Lambda(1-s)\) at \(s = 0.3 + 5i\) gives agreement to 10 significant figures. \(\checkmark\)

Verified. The Egyptian identity, integrality of denominators, and the functional equation \(\Lambda(s) = \varepsilon\Lambda(1-s)\) are all verified by direct computation for both the Ising category and SU(2)₄. These are rigorous results, not conjectures.

§ 9 Comparison with classical \(L\)-functions

The following table situates \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) within the broader landscape of automorphic \(L\)-functions.

The absence of an Euler product is a significant departure from classical \(L\)-functions. Finite Dirichlet polynomials satisfying functional equations arise naturally in the theory of self-dual codes and in the study of extremal lattices; see Knopp–Mason [KM03] for the analogous theory in that context. Whether the MTC Dirichlet polynomials here can be incorporated into the Selberg class (which requires an Euler product axiom) is an interesting question that we leave open.

§ 10 Open problems and the infinite-rank limit

The results in this paper are confined to finite-rank MTCs, where all proofs are explicit. The following open problems would need to be resolved to extend the framework to an infinite-rank limit relevant to the Riemann zeta function.

Open Problem 10.1 (General proof of Hypothesis 6.1). Prove, for an arbitrary weakly integral MTC \(\mathcal{C}\) and any non-trivial \(\sigma \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})\), that Hypothesis 6.1 (Galois self-duality) holds. The Coste–Gannon theorem guarantees the existence of \(\phi_\sigma\) and \(\epsilon_\sigma\), but does not impose the compatibility condition \(\epsilon_\sigma(\phi_\sigma(i)) = \epsilon_\sigma(i)\) in general. Counterexamples at rank \(r \ge 5\) may exist.

Open Problem 10.2 (Infinite-rank limit: \(\mathrm{SU}(2)_k\) as \(k \to \infty\)). The MTC \(\mathrm{SU}(2)_k\) has rank \(k+1\) and Egyptian denominators \(m_j^{(k)} = D_k^2/d_j^{(k)2}\) for \(j = 0, \tfrac{1}{2},\ldots,\tfrac{k}{2}\). As \(k \to \infty\), the quantum dimensions \(d_j^{(k)} = [2j+1]_{q_k}\) for \(q_k = e^{i\pi/(k+2)}\) converge to classical dimensions \(2j+1\). The Dirichlet polynomial \(\Psi_{\mathrm{SU}(2)_k,\chi_k}(s)\) becomes a partial sum of an infinite Dirichlet series. Determine: (a) whether this limit series converges to a recognisable \(L\)-function; (b) whether the functional equations for finite \(k\) converge to a functional equation of the limit; and (c) what happens to the root number \(\varepsilon_k\) in the limit.

Open Problem 10.3 (Non-vanishing off the critical line). For fixed finite-rank \(\mathcal{C}\), Corollary 6.3 gives symmetry of zeros about \(\mathrm{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}\). But does \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}(s) \ne 0\) for \(\mathrm{Re}(s) \ne \tfrac{1}{2}\)? For the Ising polynomial \(2^{1-2s} - 2^{-s}\), the only zero is at \(s = 1\), which lies on the real axis, not off the critical line. Determine, for general finite-rank \(\mathcal{C}\) and non-trivial \(\chi\), whether all zeros of \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) lie on the critical line. This is an analogue of the Riemann Hypothesis for MTC Dirichlet polynomials.

Open Problem 10.3 is, in principle, decidable for any fixed \(\mathcal{C}\): since \(\Psi_{\mathcal{C},\chi}\) is an explicit polynomial in \(e^{-s\log m_i}\), its zeros can be located numerically to arbitrary precision. An analytic proof for all \(\mathcal{C}\) simultaneously is a harder question and may connect to the spectral theory of \(\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})\)-representations.
