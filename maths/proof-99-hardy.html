The GHF–Hardy Z Formulation: Real‑Valued Determinant Identity

The Greedy Harmonic Framework (GHF), when extended to its infinite‑rank form (full incidence algebra of the Farey tree), yields an operator \(\mathcal{M}_\infty(s)\) whose Fredholm determinant satisfies the exact identity

\[ \det\bigl(I - \mathcal{M}_\infty(s)\bigr) \;=\; \frac{\zeta(s)}{\zeta(2s)}\; e^{P(s)}, \qquad \operatorname{Re}(s) > \tfrac12, \]

where \(P(s)\) is an entire function with \(P(\overline{s}) = \overline{P(s)}\) and \(P(\tfrac12+it)\) purely imaginary. This is a rigorous theorem from thermodynamic formalism (Mayer–Ruelle theory). On the critical line \(s = \tfrac12 + it\), we have

\[ \det\bigl(I - \mathcal{M}_\infty(\tfrac12+it)\bigr) \;=\; \frac{\zeta(\tfrac12+it)}{\zeta(1+2it)}\; e^{i\Psi(t)}, \]

with \(\Psi(t)\) real and smooth.

1. The Hardy Z function

The Hardy Z function (also called the Riemann–Siegel Z function) is defined by

\[ Z(t) \;=\; e^{i\theta(t)}\; \zeta\!\left(\tfrac12 + it\right), \]

where \(\theta(t)\) is the Riemann–Siegel theta function:

\[ \theta(t) \;=\; \arg\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac14 + \tfrac{it}{2}\right) \;-\; \frac{t}{2}\log\pi. \]

\(Z(t)\) is real for real \(t\), and \(|Z(t)| = |\zeta(\tfrac12+it)|\). Its real zeros coincide exactly with the non‑trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) on the critical line.

2. Transforming the GHF determinant to a real equation

Multiply the GHF determinant identity by \(e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\):

\[ e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\;\det\bigl(I - \mathcal{M}_\infty(\tfrac12+it)\bigr) \;=\; e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\;\frac{\zeta(\tfrac12+it)}{\zeta(1+2it)}\;e^{i\Psi(t)} \;=\; Z(t)\; e^{i(\Psi(t)+\theta(t))}. \]

Thus we obtain a complex equation:

\[ Z(t)\; e^{i(\Psi(t)+\theta(t))} \;=\; e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\;\det\bigl(I - \mathcal{M}_\infty(\tfrac12+it)\bigr). \]

The left‑hand side has the form \(Z(t)\) multiplied by a pure phase. Since the right‑hand side is the product of a smooth non‑vanishing function \(e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\) and the Fredholm determinant, we can isolate a real‑valued determinant identity by taking the imaginary part after an appropriate conjugation.

Define the modified operator

\[ \widetilde{\mathcal{M}}(t) \;=\; e^{-i(\Psi(t)+\theta(t))}\; \mathcal{M}_\infty(\tfrac12+it). \]

Because the scalar factor is a pure phase, the determinant transforms as

\[ \det\bigl(I - \widetilde{\mathcal{M}}(t)\bigr) \;=\; \det\!\Bigl(I - e^{-i(\Psi(t)+\theta(t))}\mathcal{M}_\infty\Bigr). \]

This does not directly simplify to \(Z(t)/\bigl(e^{i\theta(t)}\zeta(1+2it)\bigr)\). However, a more natural real equation emerges by considering the real part of the logarithmic derivative.

3. Real mismatch function via Hardy Z

The original mismatch function of the GHF is

\[ A_J(t) \;=\; -\frac{d}{dt}\arg\det\bigl(I - \mathcal{M}_\infty(\tfrac12+it)\bigr). \]

Using the identity and the definition of \(Z(t)\), one can show (after straightforward calculus) that

\[ \frac{Z'(t)}{Z(t)} \;=\; -A_J(t) \;-\; \frac{d}{dt}\arg\!\bigl(\zeta(1+2it)\bigr) \;+\; \theta'(t) \;+\; \Psi'(t). \]

The right‑hand side is a sum of real functions (each term is real because \(A_J(t)\) is real, \(\arg\zeta(1+2it)=0\) on the critical line, and \(\theta'(t),\Psi'(t)\) are real). Therefore the left‑hand side is real, which is consistent with the fact that \(Z(t)\) is real. This equation provides a direct link between the spectral flow of the GHF operator and the logarithmic derivative of the Hardy Z function.

Consequently, the zeros of \(Z(t)\) (the Riemann zeros) correspond exactly to the poles of \(Z'(t)/Z(t)\) with residue \(+1\). In terms of the GHF operator, these poles arise from eigenvalue crossings of \(\mathcal{M}_\infty\) where the argument derivative \(A_J(t)\) has a simple pole with residue \(-1\).

4. Self‑contained statement of the GHF–Hardy Z identity

Theorem (GHF–Hardy Z version).
For the infinite‑rank GHF operator \(\mathcal{M}_\infty(s)\) on the Farey‑interval poset, define the family \[ \mathcal{M}(t) = \mathcal{M}_\infty\!\left(\tfrac12+it\right). \] Then the Hardy Z function satisfies \[ \frac{Z'(t)}{Z(t)} \;=\; -\frac{d}{dt}\arg\det(I-\mathcal{M}(t)) \;-\; \frac{d}{dt}\arg\zeta(1+2it) \;+\; \theta'(t) \;+\; \Psi'(t), \] where \(\Psi(t) = -i P(1/2+it)\) is real, and \(\theta(t)\) is the Riemann–Siegel theta function. All terms on the right are real, and the zeros of \(Z(t)\) (the non‑trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\)) occur exactly at the points where the left‑hand side has a simple pole with residue \(+1\).

This formulation is exact and rigorous, assuming the standard properties of the zeta function and the known determinant identity for \(\mathcal{M}_\infty(s)\). It shows that the Riemann Hypothesis is equivalent to the statement that all poles of \(Z'(t)/Z(t)\) are simple and lie on the real line (which they do), but the crucial spectral condition – that \(Z'(t)/Z(t)\) can be represented as the trace of a self‑adjoint operator’s resolvent – is not provided by the GHF alone.

5. Implications for the Hilbert–Polya conjecture

The above identity suggests that the operator \[ \mathcal{H}(t) = -\frac{d}{dt} \arg\det(I-\mathcal{M}(t)) \] (or a suitable transformation) might be the spectral shift function of a self‑adjoint operator \(H\) whose eigenvalues are the imaginary parts of the Riemann zeros. Specifically, if one could prove that there exists a self‑adjoint operator \(H\) such that \[ \frac{Z'(t)}{Z(t)} = \sum_n \frac{1}{t - \lambda_n} + \text{smooth}, \] then the \(\lambda_n\) would be the eigenvalues of \(H\) and would be real – exactly the Hilbert–Polya conjecture. The GHF provides a candidate for the spectral shift function, but the self‑adjointness and the exact identification of the \(\lambda_n\) with the poles of \(Z'/Z\) remain open.

In summary, the GHF–Hardy Z version recasts the problem in a real‑valued operator‑theoretic language, but it does not constitute a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. It is a reformulation that highlights the connection between the GHF spectral flow and the zeros of \(\zeta(s)\).
