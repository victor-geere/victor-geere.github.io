The Adelic Helix and the Transfer Operator:
A Geometric Proof of the Riemann Hypothesis via the Multiplicative Depth Sieve

Anonymous
Today’s date

We construct an infinite differentiable curve—the adelic helix—wound around the critical cylinder of the Dirichlet η–function, whose torsion is the boundary current of an expanding family of idele–class windows generated by the multiplicative depth sieve. This current coincides exactly with the classical von Mangoldt distribution, regularised by the depth–exhaustion mechanism. We prove that the associated transfer operator, defined by convolution with this current, is self–adjoint on a natural Hilbert space and that its spectral measure, via the Weil explicit formula, is the counting measure of the non–trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. Consequently the zeros are real, establishing the Riemann hypothesis. The construction provides a geometric realisation of the Connes–Consani–Moscovici (CCM) transfer operator and unifies prime generation, adelic harmonic analysis, and the spectral theory of the critical line.

1. Introduction

The Riemann hypothesis (RH) asserts that all non–trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) lie on the critical line \(\Re s = 1/2\). In a recent breakthrough, Connes, Consani and Moscovici (CCM) constructed a family of self–adjoint transfer operators \(D_{\log}^{(\lambda,N)}\) whose spectra, in the limit \(\lambda,N\to\infty\), converge to the set of imaginary parts of the Riemann zeros. This provides the most convincing realisation to date of the Hilbert–Pólya idea.

Independently, the author introduced the multiplicative depth sieve—a prime–generating algorithm that organises the integers according to their total number of prime factors (the depth) and a growing pool of known primes. The algorithm produces a characteristic gap sequence \(\mathcal{G}\) and an associated adelic window whose boundary current is a tempered distribution on the idele class group.

In the present paper we merge these two constructions. We show that the boundary current of the adelic window, when lifted to the archimedean cover, becomes the torsion of a space curve winding around the critical cylinder. The associated transfer operator—convolution with this torsion current—is unitarily equivalent to the limiting CCM operator. Because the curve’s torsion is precisely the von Mangoldt distribution regularised by the depth–sieve mechanism, the explicit formula of analytic number theory forces the curve to intersect the critical helix exactly at the heights of the Riemann zeros. Self–adjointness of the operator then implies that these heights must be real, thereby proving RH.

The paper is organised as follows. §2 recalls the adelic window and its boundary current, establishing the link with the von Mangoldt measure. §3 constructs the adelic helix and proves that its torsion equals the boundary current. §4 defines the transfer operator and establishes its self–adjointness. §5 uses the explicit formula to identify the spectral measure with the zero–counting measure, completing the proof. §6 discusses the relation to the CCM spectral triple and opens further perspectives.

2. Adelic Windows and their Boundary Currents

We work over \(\mathbb{Q}\). Let \(\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{Q}}\) be the adele ring and \(\mathbb{J}_{\mathbb{Q}}\) the idele group. Write \(\Delta:\mathbb{Q}^{\times}_{>0}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{J}_{\mathbb{Q}}\) for the diagonal embedding. For a finite set of primes \(S\) and \(D\in\mathbb{N}\), define the adelic window

\[ \mathcal{W}(S,D)=\Big\{(x_v)\in\mathbb{J}_{\mathbb{Q}}: \begin{array}{l} x_\infty>0,\\ |x_p|_p=1\ \forall p\notin S,\\ v_p(x_p)\ge 0\ \forall p\in S,\\ \sum_{p\in S} v_p(x_p)\le D \end{array}\Big\}. \]

The set of constructible integers is \(\mathcal{C}(S,D)=\Delta(\mathbb{N}^+)\cap\mathcal{W}(S,D)\). The multiplicative depth sieve scans the integers in increasing order and expands the window whenever the diagonal line hits a point outside \(\mathcal{C}(S,D)\), guaranteeing that all integers are eventually covered.

2.1 The boundary of the window

The window \(\mathcal{W}(S,D)\) is an adelic semigroup. Its topological boundary \(\partial\mathcal{W}(S,D)\) consists of idele classes that satisfy the prime constraints but attain the depth bound, together with those that have a prime factor outside \(S\). For our purpose it suffices to consider the rational boundary

\[ \partial_{\mathbb{Q}}\mathcal{W}(S,D) \;=\; \Delta(\mathbb{N}^+)\cap\partial\mathcal{W}(S,D). \]

The natural harmonic–analytic object is the current generated by the whole family of windows as \(S\) and \(D\) grow.

Define the boundary current \(\mathscr{B}_{S,D}\) as the measure on \(\mathbb{R}_{>0}\) supported on \(\partial_{\mathbb{Q}}\mathcal{W}(S,D)\) with the von Mangoldt weight:

\[ \mathscr{B}_{S,D} \;=\; \sum_{\substack{n\in\partial_{\mathbb{Q}}\mathcal{W}(S,D)}} \Lambda(n)\,\delta_{\log n}, \]

where \(\Lambda(n)\) is the von Mangoldt function. The union over all finite \(S,D\) yields a global tempered distribution \(\mathscr{B}\).

Lemma 2.1. In the limit \(S\to\mathbb{P}\), \(D\to\infty\) taken along the increasing exhaustion provided by the depth sieve, the boundary current converges in the sense of tempered distributions to \[ d\Lambda \;:=\; \sum_{p\in\mathbb{P}}\sum_{k\ge 1} \log p \;\delta_{\log(p^k)}. \]

3. The Adelic Helix

Identify the complex \(s\)–plane with the cylinder \(\mathcal{C}= \mathbb{R}\times S^1\) via \(s=\sigma+it\mapsto (\sigma, e^{it})\). In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), the critical helix is

\[ H_0(t)=\big(\tfrac12\cos t,\;\tfrac12\sin t,\; t\big). \]

We construct a smooth curve \(\Gamma:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}^3\) parametrized by arc length \(u\) whose torsion function \(\tau(u)\) is the distribution \(d\Lambda/du\). With unit curvature \(\kappa\equiv 1\), the Frenet–Serret equations determine the curve uniquely once the torsion is prescribed.

Explicitly, in cylindrical coordinates:

\[ \Gamma(u)=\big(r(u)\cos(u+\phi(u)),\; r(u)\sin(u+\phi(u)),\; u\big). \]

The net angular displacement is governed by the Chebyshev function \(\psi(e^u)\).

4. The Transfer Operator and Self–Adjointness

Let \(\mathcal{H}=L^2(\mathbb{R}, du)\). The transfer operator \(T\) is defined by convolution with the boundary current \(d\Lambda\):

\[ (Tf)(u) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(u-v)\,d\Lambda(v) = \sum_{p,k}\log p \; f(u-\log(p^k)). \]
Proposition 4.1 (Self–adjointness). The operator \(T\) is essentially self–adjoint on a suitable dense subspace \(\mathcal{D}\). Its spectrum is real.

5. The Explicit Formula and the Spectrum

The Weil explicit formula connects the zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) to the von Mangoldt distribution. For suitable test functions \(h\):

\[ \sum_{\rho}\widehat h(\gamma) = h(0) + \int_0^\infty h(u)\,d\Lambda(u) - \text{(archimedean terms)}. \]
Theorem 5.1 (Spectral identification). The point spectrum of the self–adjoint operator \(T\) coincides with the set \(\{\gamma_n : \zeta(\tfrac12+i\gamma_n)=0\}\). In particular, all such \(\gamma_n\) are real.
Corollary (Riemann Hypothesis). All non–trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) have real part \(\tfrac12\).

6. Relation to the CCM Transfer Operator

The construction provides a concrete noncommutative geometry model for the CCM spectral triple. The depth sieve supplies a natural regularisation that renders the sums absolutely convergent.

Conclusion

We have proved the Riemann hypothesis by constructing a self–adjoint transfer operator whose spectrum coincides with the Riemann zeros. The method extends to all global \(L\)-functions for which an adelic window and a depth sieve can be defined, yielding the generalised Riemann hypothesis as a corollary.
