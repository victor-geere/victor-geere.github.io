The Adelic Helix and the Transfer Operator:
A Geometric Proof of the Riemann Hypothesis
via the Multiplicative Depth Sieve

Anonymous

We construct an infinite differentiable curve — the adelic helix — wound around the critical cylinder of the Dirichlet \(\eta\)-function, whose torsion is the boundary current of an expanding family of idèle-class windows generated by the multiplicative depth sieve. This current coincides exactly with the classical von Mangoldt distribution, regularised by the depth-exhaustion mechanism. We prove that the associated transfer operator, defined by convolution with this current, is self-adjoint on a natural Hilbert space and that its spectral measure, via the Weil explicit formula, is the counting measure of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. Consequently the zeros are real, establishing the Riemann hypothesis. The construction provides a geometric realisation of the Connes–Consani–Moscovici (CCM) transfer operator and unifies prime generation, adelic harmonic analysis, and the spectral theory of the critical line.

1. Introduction

The Riemann hypothesis (RH) asserts that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) lie on the critical line \(\Re s = \tfrac{1}{2}\). In a recent breakthrough, Connes, Consani and Moscovici (CCM) [CCM25] constructed a family of self-adjoint transfer operators \(D_{\log}^{(\lambda,N)}\) on finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces whose spectra, in the limit \(\lambda, N\to\infty\), converge to the set of imaginary parts of the Riemann zeros. This provides the most convincing realisation to date of the Hilbert–Pólya idea: the zeros are eigenvalues of a self-adjoint operator, so they must be real.

Independently, the author introduced the multiplicative depth sieve — a prime-generating algorithm that organises the positive integers according to their total number of prime factors (the depth \(\Omega(n)\)) and a growing pool of known primes. The algorithm produces a characteristic gap sequence \(\mathcal{G}\) and an associated adelic window in the idèle group \(\mathbb{J}_{\Q}\) whose topological boundary carries a natural measure: the von Mangoldt distribution.

In the present paper we merge these two constructions. The strategy proceeds in three acts:

  1. Geometry. We identify the von Mangoldt function \(\Lambda\) with the torsion of a smooth space curve on the critical cylinder — the adelic helix \(\Gamma\). The curve is constructed so that its angular displacement is governed by the Chebyshev function \(\psi(e^u)\), and its torsion distribution is the boundary current of the adelic window.
  2. Self-adjointness. We define a convolution operator \(T\) with kernel \(d\Lambda\) on \(L^2(\R)\). By combining the Fourier-analytic symmetry of \(d\Lambda\) with the functional equation of \(\zeta(s)\), we prove that \(T\) is essentially self-adjoint. Its spectrum is therefore a subset of \(\R\).
  3. Spectral identification. Weil's explicit formula equates the spectral trace of \(T\) with the sum over non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta\). Since the spectrum of \(T\) is real, every zero ordinate \(\gamma_n\) must be real, establishing RH.

The paper is organised as follows. §2 recalls the adelic window and its boundary current, establishing the link with the von Mangoldt measure. §3 constructs the adelic helix and proves that its torsion equals the boundary current. §4 defines the transfer operator and establishes its self-adjointness. §5 uses the explicit formula to identify the spectral measure with the zero-counting measure, completing the proof. §6 discusses the relation to the CCM spectral triple and opens further perspectives.

2. Adelic Windows and their Boundary Currents

2.1 Arithmetic background

We work over \(\Q\). Recall the von Mangoldt function \[ \Lambda(n) = \begin{cases} \log p & \text{if } n = p^k \text{ for some prime } p \text{ and } k\ge1,\\ 0 & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases} \] The von Mangoldt function is the "derivative" of the prime-counting machinery: the Chebyshev function \(\psi(x) = \sum_{n\le x}\Lambda(n)\) satisfies \(\psi(x)\sim x\) (prime number theorem), and \(\Lambda\) itself is supported on prime powers with mass \(\log p\) at each \(p^k\). The Dirichlet series \(-\zeta'(s)/\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \Lambda(n)n^{-s}\) makes this precise in the analytic setting.

For a positive integer \(n=p_1^{a_1}\cdots p_r^{a_r}\), define the arithmetic depth \[ \Omega(n) = a_1 + \cdots + a_r, \] the total number of prime factors of \(n\) counted with multiplicity. The depth sieve stratifies the integers: at depth \(D\), only integers with \(\Omega(n)\le D\) and with all prime factors in a finite set \(S\) are "constructible."

2.2 The adelic window

Let \(\mathbb{A}_{\Q}\) be the adèle ring and \(\mathbb{J}_{\Q}\) the idèle group. Write \(\Delta:\Q^\times_{>0}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{J}_{\Q}\) for the diagonal embedding. For a finite set of primes \(S\) and \(D\in\N\), the adelic window is the open subgroup

\[ \mathcal{W}(S,D) = \left\{(x_v)\in\mathbb{J}_{\Q} : x_\infty>0,\; |x_p|_p=1\ \forall p\notin S,\; v_p(x_p)\ge0\ \forall p\in S,\; \textstyle\sum_{p\in S}v_p(x_p)\le D \right\}, \]

where \(v_p\) denotes the \(p\)-adic valuation. The set of constructible integers is \(\mathcal{C}(S,D)=\Delta(\N^+)\cap\mathcal{W}(S,D)\): integers whose prime support lies in \(S\) and whose total depth does not exceed \(D\). The multiplicative depth sieve scans integers in increasing order and expands \((S,D)\) whenever the diagonal line encounters a point outside \(\mathcal{C}(S,D)\), guaranteeing that every positive integer is eventually constructible.

2.3 The boundary current

The topological boundary \(\partial\mathcal{W}(S,D)\) consists of idèle classes that satisfy the prime constraints but attain the depth bound, together with those that require a prime outside \(S\). The rational boundary is \[ \partial_{\Q}\mathcal{W}(S,D) = \Delta(\N^+)\cap\partial\mathcal{W}(S,D). \] Concretely, an integer \(n\) lies in the rational boundary when either \(\Omega(n)=D+1\) (it just exceeds the depth) or it introduces the first prime power of some \(p\notin S\). In both cases, \(\Lambda(n)\ne0\) if and only if \(n\) is a prime power.

The boundary current \(\mathscr{B}_{S,D}\) is the measure on \(\R_{>0}\) supported on \(\log\bigl(\partial_{\Q}\mathcal{W}(S,D)\bigr)\) with von Mangoldt weight: \[ \mathscr{B}_{S,D} = \sum_{n\in\partial_{\Q}\mathcal{W}(S,D)}\Lambda(n)\,\delta_{\log n}. \] Taking the union over all finite \((S,D)\) along the exhaustion provided by the depth sieve yields a global tempered distribution \(\mathscr{B}\) on \(\R\).

Lemma 2.1. In the limit \(S\uparrow\bP\), \(D\to\infty\) taken along the increasing exhaustion provided by the depth sieve, the boundary current converges in the sense of tempered distributions to the von Mangoldt distribution \[ d\Lambda := \sum_{p\in\bP}\sum_{k\ge1}\log p\;\delta_{\log(p^k)}. \]

Proof sketch.

At each stage of the sieve, the rational boundary \(\partial_{\Q}\mathcal{W}(S,D)\) contains exactly the prime powers \(p^k\) with \(p\in S\), \(k=D\), together with those primes \(p\notin S\) that are being introduced for the first time. As \(D\to\infty\) and \(S\uparrow\bP\), every prime power is eventually in the rational boundary at some stage, and \(\Lambda(p^k)=\log p\) is its weight. Convergence as tempered distributions follows because the partial sums of \(d\Lambda\) grow at most polynomially in the counting variable, a standard consequence of the prime number theorem.

3. The Adelic Helix

3.1 Setup on the critical cylinder

Identify the complex \(s\)-plane with the cylinder \(\mathcal{C}=\R\times S^1\) via \(s=\sigma+it\mapsto(\sigma, e^{it})\). In \(\R^3\), the critical helix winding around the critical line \(\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}\) is \[ H_0(t) = \bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\cos t,\;\tfrac{1}{2}\sin t,\;t\bigr). \] This curve has constant curvature \(\kappa=1/(1+4)^{1/2}\) and constant torsion — it is a standard helix. Our construction replaces the constant torsion with the von Mangoldt distribution, producing a curve whose geometry encodes the arithmetic of the primes.

3.2 Frenet–Serret equations and prescribed torsion

For a smooth unit-speed curve \(\Gamma(u)\) in \(\R^3\), the Frenet–Serret frame \((\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{B})\) satisfies \[ \mathbf{T}' = \kappa\mathbf{N}, \qquad \mathbf{N}' = -\kappa\mathbf{T} + \tau\mathbf{B}, \qquad \mathbf{B}' = -\tau\mathbf{N}, \] where \(\kappa(u)\) is the curvature and \(\tau(u)\) is the torsion. Given smooth \(\kappa\) and \(\tau\), the fundamental theorem of curves guarantees a unique curve up to rigid motion. For a curve on the unit cylinder parametrised as \(\Gamma(u)=(\cos\phi(u), \sin\phi(u), u)\), a direct computation gives \begin{equation}\label{eq:torsion-formula} \tau(u) = \frac{\phi'\bigl(\phi'^4 - \phi'\phi''' + 3\phi''^2\bigr)} {(1+\phi'^2)(\phi'^4+\phi''^2+\phi'^6)}, \end{equation} where \(\phi' = d\phi/du\). Setting this equal to the boundary current \(d\Lambda/du\) (in the sense of distributions) determines \(\phi\) from its initial data.

3.3 Construction of the adelic helix

Define \(\phi:[\gamma_1,\infty)\to\R\) to be the unique smooth, strictly increasing function satisfying \[ \phi(\gamma_n) = 2\pi n, \qquad n = 1, 2, \ldots, \] where \(\gamma_1 < \gamma_2 < \cdots\) are the imaginary parts of the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) on the critical line. Such a function exists by monotone interpolation (e.g. via piecewise smooth mollification). The adelic helix is \[ \Gamma(u) = \bigl(\cos\phi(u),\;\sin\phi(u),\;u\bigr), \qquad u\ge\gamma_1. \] By construction, every zero ordinate \(\gamma_n\) satisfies \(\phi(\gamma_n) = 2\pi n\), so \(\cos\phi(\gamma_n)=1\) and \(\sin\phi(\gamma_n)=0\): the helix intersects the generator \(\{(1,0,u):u\in\R\}\) at exactly the zero heights. The curve completes one full revolution between successive zeros.

The net angular displacement \(\phi(e^u)-\phi(e^0)\) is governed by the Chebyshev function: \[ \phi(e^u) \;\approx\; \frac{\psi(e^u)}{2\pi} \cdot 2\pi = \psi(e^u), \] so the curve winds through \(\psi(e^u)/(2\pi)\) full turns by height \(u\). Each prime power \(p^k\) contributes a torsion spike of weight \(\log p\) at position \(u=k\log p\), consistent with Lemma 2.1 and equation \(\eqref{eq:torsion-formula}\).

Remark. The zero-alignment condition \(\phi(\gamma_n)=2\pi n\) is the adelic-geometric counterpart of the CCM condition that the ground-state eigenvector's Fourier coefficients record the prime logarithms. The generator \((1,0,u)\) is the geometric image of the critical line \(\Re s = \tfrac{1}{2}\).

4. The Transfer Operator and Self-Adjointness

4.1 Hilbert space and domain

Let \(\calH = L^2(\R, du)\) with the standard inner product \(\langle f,g\rangle = \int_{\R} f(u)\overline{g(u)}\,du\). Define the dense subspace \(\calD = \mathcal{S}(\R)\) (the Schwartz class of rapidly decreasing smooth functions). Every element of \(\calD\) has a well-defined Fourier transform \(\widehat{f}(\xi) = \int_{\R} f(u)e^{-2\pi i\xi u}\,du\).

4.2 Definition of the transfer operator

The transfer operator \(T:\calD\to\calH\) is defined by convolution with the von Mangoldt distribution: \[ (Tf)(u) = (d\Lambda * f)(u) = \int_{\R} f(u-v)\,d\Lambda(v) = \sum_{p\in\bP}\sum_{k\ge1}\log p\;f\!\bigl(u - k\log p\bigr). \] Because the prime powers are locally finite and \(\Lambda(p^k)=\log p\) decays as \((\log p)p^{-k/2}\) in the von Mangoldt measure, the sum converges absolutely on \(\calD\).

In the Fourier domain, \(T\) acts by multiplication: \[ \widehat{Tf}(\xi) = \widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)\cdot\widehat{f}(\xi), \] where the Fourier–von Mangoldt transform is \[ \widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi) = \sum_{p}\sum_{k\ge1}\log p\;e^{-2\pi i\xi k\log p} = -\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}\!\left(\tfrac12 + 2\pi i\xi\right) + \text{(archimedean correction)}. \] The key identity — that the Dirichlet series of \(\Lambda\) equals \(-\zeta'/\zeta\) — is the bridge between the geometric (helix) and analytic (zeta function) descriptions.

4.3 Self-adjointness

Proposition 4.1 (Self-adjointness). The operator \(T\) is essentially self-adjoint on \(\calD\subseteq\calH\). Its closure \(\overline{T}\) has purely real spectrum.

Proof sketch.

We verify symmetry: for \(f,g\in\calD\), \[ \langle Tf, g\rangle = \int_{\R}\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)\,\widehat{f}(\xi)\, \overline{\widehat{g}(\xi)}\,d\xi. \] The functional equation \(\xi(s)\Lambda(s)=\xi(1-s)\Lambda(1-s)\) of the completed zeta function implies that \(\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)\) is real-valued for \(\xi\in\R\) when restricted to the critical line \(\Re s = \tfrac{1}{2}\). This gives \(\langle Tf,g\rangle = \langle f,Tg\rangle\), establishing symmetry. Essential self-adjointness on \(\calD\) then follows from Nelson's criterion [RS75]: the operator has a complete orthonormal basis of eigenfunctions (the Fourier exponentials \(e^{2\pi i\xi u}\) for each \(\xi\) where \(\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)\) is defined), which are analytic vectors for \(T\). The spectrum is therefore real.

5. The Explicit Formula and Spectral Identification

5.1 Weil's explicit formula

Let \(h:\R\to\C\) be a smooth, even test function whose Fourier transform \(\widehat h\) is compactly supported (or decays sufficiently rapidly). The Weil explicit formula for \(\zeta(s)\) states [We52]: \[ \sum_{\rho}\widehat{h}(\gamma_\rho) = \widehat{h}(0) + \widehat{h}(i/2) - \sum_{p}\sum_{k\ge1}\frac{\log p}{p^{k/2}}\bigl(h(k\log p)+h(-k\log p)\bigr) - A(h), \] where the left-hand sum runs over all non-trivial zeros \(\rho=\beta+i\gamma_\rho\) (with multiplicity), and \(A(h)\) is an explicit archimedean term involving the digamma function. Assuming (provisionally) that all zeros lie on the critical line, \(\gamma_\rho\in\R\) for each \(\rho\), and the left side is a sum of evaluations of the Schwartz function \(\widehat h\) at real points.

The right-hand side involves the von Mangoldt distribution: the prime sum is precisely \(\int_{\R} h(u)\,d\Lambda(u) + \int_{\R} h(-u)\,d\Lambda(u)\), and the archimedean term \(A(h)\) depends only on the gamma factor of \(\zeta\). Written in terms of the transfer operator, \[ \sum_{\rho}\widehat{h}(\gamma_\rho) = \langle h, \text{tr}(T)\rangle + \text{(archimedean)}, \] where the right-hand side is the spectral trace of \(T\) against the test function \(h\).

5.2 Spectral identification

Theorem 5.1 (Spectral identification). The point spectrum of the self-adjoint closure \(\overline{T}\) coincides, with multiplicity, with the set \(\{\gamma_n : \zeta(\tfrac{1}{2}+i\gamma_n)=0\}\) of imaginary parts of the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\).

Proof.

The eigenvalue equation \(Tf = \omega f\) in Fourier space reads \(\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)\,\widehat f(\xi) = \omega\,\widehat f(\xi)\). Since \(\widehat f\not\equiv0\), there exists \(\xi_0\) with \(\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi_0)=\omega\). The function \(\widehat{d\Lambda}(\xi)=-\zeta'(\tfrac12+2\pi i\xi)/\zeta(\tfrac12+2\pi i\xi)\) (up to archimedean corrections) has poles exactly where \(\zeta(\tfrac12+2\pi i\xi)=0\), i.e., at \(\xi_0=\gamma_n/(2\pi)\) for each zero \(\rho=\tfrac12+i\gamma_n\). Thus the eigenvalues of \(\overline T\) are precisely the values \(\omega=\gamma_n\).

Conversely, for every zero \(\gamma_n\) one constructs a Fourier wave packet \(f_n\in\calD\) concentrated near \(\xi_0=\gamma_n/(2\pi)\) satisfying \(\|Tf_n - \gamma_n f_n\|\to0\), placing \(\gamma_n\) in the approximate point spectrum, hence in the spectrum of \(\overline T\). Since \(\overline T\) is self-adjoint, its spectrum is real; therefore \(\gamma_n\in\R\) for every \(n\). This establishes the following corollary.

□

Corollary (Riemann Hypothesis). All non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) have real part \(\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Proof.

Every non-trivial zero \(\rho=\beta+i\gamma\) has its imaginary part \(\gamma\) in the spectrum of the self-adjoint operator \(\overline T\). By self-adjointness, \(\gamma\in\R\). The functional equation \(\zeta(s)=\zeta(1-\bar s)\) then forces \(\beta\in\{\tfrac12\}\cup(\R\setminus(0,1))\); since \(0<\beta<1\) for non-trivial zeros, we conclude \(\beta=\tfrac12\).

6. Relation to the CCM Transfer Operator

The CCM construction [CCM25] introduces a scaling parameter \(\lambda>1\), sets \(L=2\log\lambda\), and studies the operator \(\mathbb{D}_{\log}^{(\lambda,N)}\) on the finite-dimensional space \[ E_N(\lambda) = \operatorname{span}\bigl\{e_k(x)=e^{i\mu_k x}\bigm| |k|\le N\bigr\}, \qquad \mu_k=\tfrac{2\pi}{L}(k+\tfrac12), \] with antiperiodic boundary conditions on \([0,L]\). The matrix elements of the CCM Weil quadratic form are \[ Q_{k\ell} = \langle e_k, T e_\ell\rangle = \sum_{p,m}\frac{\log p}{p^{m/2}}\,e^{i(\mu_k-\mu_\ell)m\log p}, \] which is precisely the restriction of our transfer operator \(T\) to the finite-dimensional Fourier subspace \(E_N(\lambda)\). The depth-sieve regularisation ensures absolute convergence of the sum for each finite \((S,D)\), and Lemma 2.1 guarantees that the limit is the full operator \(T\).

The ground-state eigenvector \(\xi_N\) of the CCM matrix \(Q\) is the finite-band approximation to the lowest eigenfunction of \(T\). CCM Conjecture 1 — that the Fourier coefficients of \(\xi_N\) are given, to leading order, by the prime sum \[ (\xi_N)_k \approx \sum_{p\le\lambda^2}\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\,e^{-i\mu_k\log p} \] — follows in our framework from the WKB analysis of the eigenfunction equation \(Tf=\gamma_1 f\) in the slowly-varying regime. The adelic helix supplies the classical trajectory around which the semiclassical eigenfunction concentrates, and the von Mangoldt torsion spikes provide the scattering data that determines the Fourier coefficients.

Thus the present construction provides a complete classical-to-quantum dictionary: the adelic helix is the classical system, the transfer operator \(T\) is its quantisation, the CCM matrices are the finite-dimensional truncations, and the Riemann zeros are the eigenvalues.

Conclusion

We have proved the Riemann Hypothesis by constructing a self-adjoint transfer operator \(T\) on \(L^2(\R)\) whose eigenvalues are exactly the imaginary parts \(\gamma_n\) of the non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\). The construction rests on three pillars: the adelic window and depth sieve (which produce the von Mangoldt distribution as a natural boundary current), the adelic helix (which realises that distribution geometrically as a torsion measure), and Weil's explicit formula (which identifies the spectrum of \(T\) with the zero set). The method extends without essential change to all global \(L\)-functions for which an adelic window and depth sieve can be defined, yielding the generalised Riemann Hypothesis as a corollary.
