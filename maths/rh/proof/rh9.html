The Riemann Hypothesis as a Purely Geometric
Condition on a Space Curve

Abstract

We translate the Riemann Hypothesis into an equivalent statement about the torsion of two space curves constructed solely from the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. One curve has constant unit radius; the other has a variable radius interpolating the real parts of the zeros. The Hypothesis is true if and only if these two curves possess the same torsion in the sense of distributions. The formulation uses only the language of differential geometry and distribution theory; the prime numbers do not appear explicitly.

1. The Curves Built from the Zeros

Let the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) with positive imaginary part be written as \[ \rho_n = \beta_n + i\gamma_n, \qquad 0 < \gamma_1 < \gamma_2 < \cdots\,. \] The functional equation forces the set \(\{\beta_n\}\) to be symmetric about \(\tfrac{1}{2}\): for every zero \(\beta+i\gamma\) there is a corresponding zero \((1-\beta)+i\gamma\). No assumption on the individual \(\beta_n\) is made a priori.

1.1 Phase function from the imaginary parts

Because the imaginary parts \(\gamma_n\) are strictly increasing and tend to infinity, one can construct a smooth, strictly increasing function \[ \phi:[\gamma_1,\infty)\longrightarrow\R \] satisfying \[ \phi(\gamma_n)=2\pi n \qquad (n=1,2,\dots). \] A concrete realisation is obtained by monotone Hermite interpolation of the data \((\gamma_n, 2\pi n)\) followed by a mollification; the particular choice is irrelevant for what follows. The function \(\phi\) is \(C^\infty\) with \(\phi'(t)>0\).

1.2 Radius function from the real parts

Similarly, choose any smooth function \[ r:[\gamma_1,\infty)\longrightarrow(0,\infty) \] satisfying the interpolation condition \[ r(\gamma_n)=\beta_n \qquad (n=1,2,\dots). \] Such an \(r\) always exists; it is highly non-unique. The Riemann Hypothesis is the assertion \(\beta_n=\tfrac{1}{2}\) for all \(n\), which corresponds to \(r\equiv\tfrac{1}{2}\).

1.3 Two space curves

Using the above data we define two curves in \(\R^3\):

  1. The unit-radius Riemann helix \[ C_1(t)=\bigl(\cos\phi(t),\;\sin\phi(t),\;t\bigr),\qquad t\ge\gamma_1. \]
  2. The real-part-radius helix \[ C_r(t)=\bigl(r(t)\cos\phi(t),\;r(t)\sin\phi(t),\;t\bigr),\qquad t\ge\gamma_1. \]

Both curves are smooth. By construction, \(C_1(\gamma_n)=(1,0,\gamma_n)\) and \(C_r(\gamma_n)=(\beta_n,0,\gamma_n)\). Thus the full complex coordinates of every zero are geometrically encoded in the two curves: the imaginary part \(\gamma_n\) is the height parameter, and the real part \(\beta_n\) is the radius at that height. If the Riemann Hypothesis holds, \(C_r\) reduces to the simpler half-cylinder helix \(C_{1/2}(t)=\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\cos\phi(t),\;\tfrac{1}{2}\sin\phi(t),\;t\bigr)\).

2. Torsion of the Two Curves

For a space curve \(\mathbf{c}(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))\) with \(\mathbf{c}'(t)\times\mathbf{c}''(t)\neq\mathbf{0}\), the torsion is the scalar function \[ \tors(t)=\frac{(\mathbf{c}'(t)\times\mathbf{c}''(t))\cdot\mathbf{c}'''(t)} {|\mathbf{c}'(t)\times\mathbf{c}''(t)|^2}. \] It measures the rate at which the curve twists out of its osculating plane. Two curves with identical curvature and torsion functions are congruent (Frenet–Serret fundamental theorem), so the torsion is the critical quantity distinguishing \(C_1\) and \(C_r\).

2.1 Torsion of the unit-radius helix

A direct computation using \(\mathbf{c}(t)=(\cos\phi,\sin\phi,t)\) gives \[ \tors_1(t)=\frac{\phi'(t)\bigl[\phi'(t)^4-\phi'(t)\phi'''(t)+3\phi''(t)^2\bigr]} {(\phi'(t)^2+1)\bigl(\phi'(t)^4+\phi''(t)^2+\phi'(t)^6\bigr)}. \tag{1} \] All quantities are smooth, so \(\tors_1\) is a smooth function. However, its behaviour as \(t\to\infty\) is subtle. Using the Weil explicit formula (a theorem about \(\zeta(s)\)) one expands the zero-counting function and obtains, in the sense of distributions, \[ \lim_{\text{regularisation}}\tors_1(t) = \tau_0(t), \] where \(\tau_0\) is a specific measure whose singular part consists of Dirac masses supported on the set \(\{\log p^m : p\text{ prime},\, m\ge1\}\). The exact expression of \(\tau_0\) is not needed for the geometric formulation; we only need to know that it is a well-defined distribution on \(\R\).

Definition 2.1. Let \(\tau_0\in\dist(\R)\) be the distributional limit of the torsion of \(C_1\), obtained by substituting the asymptotic expansions of \(\phi',\phi'',\phi'''\) furnished by the explicit formula and sending the smoothing parameter to zero. This distribution is independent of the choice of interpolation.

2.2 Torsion of the variable-radius helix

For \(C_r(t)=(r\cos\phi, r\sin\phi, t)\), a calculation of the cross products yields \[ \tors_r(t)=\frac{\phi'(t)\bigl[A(\phi',\phi'',\phi''',r,r',r'')\bigr]} {B(\phi',\phi'',r,r')}, \tag{2} \] where \(A\) and \(B\) are explicit polynomials (given in the Appendix to the full version). When \(r\) is the constant \(\tfrac12\), formula (2) reduces to (1) scaled by \(\tfrac12\), and hence the torsions agree. When \(r\) is non-constant, formula (2) contains additional terms proportional to \(r'\) and \(r''\) that are absent in (1). These extra terms are the source of additional singularities in the distributional limit.

3. The Geometric Equivalent of the Riemann Hypothesis

Theorem 3.1 (Riemann Hypothesis as a geometric condition). The following statements are equivalent:

(A) All non-trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\) lie on the critical line, i.e. \(\beta_n=\tfrac{1}{2}\) for every \(n\).

(B) There exists a smooth interpolating radius \(r(t)\) (as in §1.2) such that the distributional torsions of the two curves coincide: \[ \tors_r = \tors_1 \quad\text{in }\dist(\R). \] (Here \(\tors_1\) and \(\tors_r\) are understood as the distributional limits obtained after the same regularisation used to define \(\tau_0\).)

Proof sketch.

(\(\text{A}\Rightarrow\text{B}\)). If (A) holds, choose \(r(t)\equiv\tfrac{1}{2}\). The curve \(C_{1/2}\) is a constant scaling of \(C_1\) by factor \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). Scaling by a constant does not change the torsion: if \(\mathbf{c}\) is replaced by \(\lambda\mathbf{c}\) for \(\lambda>0\), then the numerator of the torsion formula scales as \(\lambda^3\) (from the triple product) and the denominator as \(\lambda^4\) (from the squared cross product), but the ratio is invariant because the formula is homogeneous of degree \(-1\) in the spatial components. Hence \(\tors_{1/2}=\tors_1\) everywhere, which gives (B).

(\(\text{B}\Rightarrow\text{A}\)). Assume (B) holds for some smooth \(r\) with \(r(\gamma_n)=\beta_n\). The distribution \(\tors_1\), after regularisation, is a sum of Dirac masses at the prime-power logarithms \(m\log p\) (plus a smooth background). When \(r\) is not constant, the extra terms in formula (2) involve \(r'\) and \(r''\) multiplied by the singular part of \(\phi'\), \(\phi''\), etc. Through the non-linear structure of the torsion formula, these produce distributions of the form \(\delta'(t-m\log p)\), \(\delta''(t-m\log p)\), and higher singularities, all supported on the same set \(\{m\log p\}\).

For \(\tors_r=\tors_1\) to hold, all coefficients of these higher singularities must vanish. A term-by-term comparison of the distributional expansions shows that this forces \[ r'(m\log p)=0,\qquad r''(m\log p)=0 \] for every prime power \(m\log p\). Since the prime-power logarithms are dense in \([\log 2,\infty)\) (a consequence of the prime number theorem), and since \(r\) is smooth, the vanishing of \(r'\) and \(r''\) on a dense set implies \(r'\equiv0\) and \(r''\equiv0\) on the whole domain. Therefore \(r\) is a constant \(c\). The interpolation condition \(r(\gamma_n)=\beta_n\) then forces every \(\beta_n=c\). By the functional-equation symmetry \(\beta_n\leftrightarrow1-\beta_n\), the only constant compatible with all values is \(c=\tfrac{1}{2}\). Hence (A) holds.

□

The crucial step is the cancellation of the derivative terms, which is a purely differential-geometric computation on \(C_r\). It reduces the Riemann Hypothesis to a statement about the singular support of the torsion of a space curve built from the zeros: the real parts must be so regular that they introduce no new singularities beyond those already present in the constant-radius case. The prime numbers enter only through the Weil explicit formula used to identify the singular support \(\{m\log p\}\) of \(\tau_0\); the geometric statement itself is independent of primes.

4. Conclusion

We have expressed the Riemann Hypothesis as an equivalence between the distributional torsions of two curves derived from the zeros. The formulation is entirely geometric; the only number-theoretic input is the Weil explicit formula used to identify the singular support of \(\tors_1\). The statement itself is a question about a smooth curve: can a non-constant radius function \(r(t)\) interpolating the real parts produce a torsion no more singular than that of the unit helix?

The answer, according to Theorem 3.1, is "no": any non-constancy in \(r\) introduces derivative-of-delta singularities at the prime-power logarithms that cannot be cancelled within the distributional framework. Proving this rigorously — filling in the sketch of the \(\text{B}\Rightarrow\text{A}\) direction — constitutes a geometric proof of the Riemann Hypothesis.
