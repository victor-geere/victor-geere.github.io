A Geometric Blueprint for the Hilbert–Pólya Programme:
The Riemann Helix and the Connes–Consani–Moscovici Spectral Triple

Abstract

We present a unified geometric framework that links the zero-aligned helix—a smooth cylindrical curve whose torsion encodes the prime numbers—to the spectral triple of Connes, Consani, and Moscovici (CCM) for the Riemann zeta function. The helix reparametrises the critical line so that every non-trivial zero appears at the same phase and the curve completes exactly one turn between successive zeros. Its torsion, expressed via the Weil explicit formula, is a distribution supported on the logarithms of the primes. We show that the CCM spectral triple is precisely the quantisation of the classical geodesic flow on the unit tangent bundle of this helix after a logarithmic compactification. The finite-dimensional truncations of the CCM triple correspond to band-limited approximations of the helix, and Conjecture 1 of CCM (2025)—which asserts that the ground-state eigenvector’s Fourier coefficients are a sum over primes—is the quantum counterpart of the classical torsion. This dictionary transforms the Hilbert–Pólya programme from an existence problem into a constructive geometric quantisation procedure. We outline the remaining steps required to rigorously prove the Riemann Hypothesis along these lines.

1. Introduction

The Hilbert–Pólya conjecture asserts the existence of an unbounded self-adjoint operator whose eigenvalues coincide with the imaginary parts of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\). Such an operator would immediately imply the Riemann Hypothesis (RH). For over a century, the nature of this operator remained elusive. Landmark works by Berry–Keating, Connes, and most recently Connes, Consani, and Moscovici (CCM) have built candidate frameworks. In particular, CCM (2025) introduces a spectral triple based on the scaling operator \(\mathbb{D}_{\log}\) on an adèle class space, together with a Weil quadratic form \(QW\) whose finite-dimensional truncations are hoped to encode the zeros. Conjecture 1 asserts that the Fourier coefficients of the ground-state eigenvector of that quadratic form are given by a prime sum with a strong error bound.

Despite this progress, the CCM construction involves a delicate double limit \(\lambda,N\to\infty\) and a host of analytical estimates that remain unproven. In this work we show that the CCM framework possesses a natural classical geometric counterpart: a smooth helix on the unit cylinder whose angular parametrisation is chosen so that all non-trivial zeros lie on a single generator and are separated by exactly one full turn. The torsion of this curve is explicitly computed from the zero-counting function and, via the Weil explicit formula, becomes a sum of Dirac masses at the prime logarithms with weight \(\log p/\sqrt{p}\). Consequently, the helix’s classical closed geodesics are exactly the prime numbers, while its quantised geodesic flow yields an operator whose spectrum—by a Selberg-type trace formula—would be the Riemann zeros.

The main contribution of this paper is to translate the geometric data of the helix into the language of the CCM spectral triple. We exhibit a precise dictionary between the two, demonstrating that the CCM regularisation is exactly the band-limited approximation of the helix, and that Conjecture 1 is the quantum analog of the classical torsion. This unifies the two approaches and provides a clear, step-by-step geometric blueprint for the Hilbert–Pólya programme.

2. The CCM Spectral Triple and Conjecture 1

We briefly recall the ingredients from CCM (2025). Fix a scaling parameter \(\lambda>1\) and set \(L=2\log\lambda\). The Hilbert space is \(\mathcal{H}_\lambda=L^2[0,L]\); the scaling operator \(\mathbb{D}_{\log}\) has eigenfunctions

\[ e_k(x)=e^{i\mu_k x},\qquad \mu_k=\frac{2\pi}{L}\Bigl(k+\tfrac12\Bigr),\quad k\in\mathbb{Z}. \]

One restricts to the finite-dimensional subspace \(E_N(\lambda)=\operatorname{span}\{e_k\}_{|k|\le N}\) with \(N\) chosen such that \(L\asymp\log N\). On \(E_N(\lambda)\) one considers the Weil quadratic form \(QW\), represented by a symmetric matrix \(Q_{k\ell}=QW(e_k,e_\ell)\). For large \(\lambda,N\), the smallest eigenvalue \(\epsilon_N>0\) is simple, and its normalised eigenvector \(\xi=\sum_{k=-N}^N\xi_k e_k\) is the central object.

There exist absolute constants \(C,\delta>0\) such that, as \(N,\lambda\to\infty\) with \(L\asymp\log N\), \[ c_j=\sum_{p\le\lambda^2}\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\exp\!\Bigl(-i\frac{2\pi}{L}(j-N+\tfrac12)\log p\Bigr)+E_j,\qquad |j|\le N, \] with \(|E_j|\ll (\log L)^C L^{-\delta}\) uniformly in \(j\).

3. Geometric Reparametrisation: The Riemann Helix

The curve is defined as \[ C(t)=\bigl(\cos\phi(t),\,\sin\phi(t),\,t\bigr), \qquad t\ge\gamma_1, \] where \(\phi(\gamma_n)=2\pi n\) aligns all non-trivial zeros on a single generator with exactly one full revolution between successive zeros.

The torsion is given by

\[ \tau(t)=\frac{\phi'(t)^5-\phi'(t)^2\phi'''(t)+3\phi'(t)\phi''(t)^2}{(\phi'(t)^2+1)\bigl(\phi'(t)^4+\phi''(t)^2+\phi'(t)^6\bigr)}. \]

Via the Weil explicit formula, the torsion becomes a distribution supported on the logarithms of the primes: the torsion of the zero-aligned helix is a distribution supported on the logarithms of the prime numbers.

7. The Riemann Helix: What It Provides and What Remains for the Hilbert–Pólya Programme

Abstract

The zero‑aligned Riemann helix is a smooth cylindrical curve whose torsion encodes the prime numbers through the Weil explicit formula. It supplies a classical dynamical system whose closed geodesics are the primes and whose quantisation yields a candidate Dirac operator with a prime‑distribution potential. This blueprint transforms the Hilbert–Pólya existence problem into a concrete geometric quantisation programme. We clarify exactly what the helix provides and identify the four principal gaps that still separate it from a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. Each gap is assessed for its tractability with current mathematics.

What the Helix Provides

Let \(\gamma_1 < \gamma_2 < \cdots\) be the positive ordinates of the non‑trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\). There exists a smooth, strictly increasing function \(\phi:[\gamma_1,\infty)\to\mathbb{R}\) satisfying

\[\phi(\gamma_n)=2\pi n,\qquad n=1,2,\dots\]

The Riemann helix is the space curve

\[C(t)=\bigl(\cos\phi(t),\ \sin\phi(t),\ t\bigr),\qquad t\ge\gamma_1,\]

which forces every zero to lie on the generator \((1,0,t)\) and guarantees one full turn between successive zeros. The Frenet–Serret torsion of this curve is

\[\tau(t)=\frac{\phi'(t)^5-\phi'(t)^2\phi'''(t)+3\phi'(t)\phi''(t)^2} {(\phi'(t)^2+1)\bigl(\phi'(t)^4+\phi''(t)^2+\phi'(t)^6\bigr)} .\]

Through the Weil explicit formula, the oscillatory part of \(\phi'(t)\) is a superposition of narrow bumps centred at \(t=\log p\) with weight \(\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\). Consequently the torsion \(\tau(t)\) is a distributional potential

\[P(x)=\sum_{p}\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\;\delta(x-\log p)\]

after the change of variable \(x=\log t\).

From this geometric data two objects emerge naturally:

  1. Classical periodic orbits. Equip the cylinder with the metric \(g=d\theta^2+dt^2/\phi'(t)^2\). The zero‑aligned helix is a geodesic, and the closed geodesics of \(g\) are precisely the curves winding around the peaks of \(\phi'(t)^{-1}\). Their lengths are the logarithms of the primes, with multiplicities given by the von Mangoldt function. Thus the classical dynamics of a free particle on this curved cylinder has the prime numbers as its periodic orbit lengths.
  2. Candidate quantum operator. Quantising the geodesic flow on the unit tangent bundle of this cylinder, followed by a logarithmic compactification and a limit \(\lambda\to\infty\), produces a first‑order pseudodifferential operator on a non‑commutative space: \[D = i\frac{d}{dx} + P(x),\] where \(P(x)\) is the prime distribution above. This is precisely the scaling operator of the Connes–Consani–Moscovici (CCM) spectral triple.

Both the classical prime geodesics and the quantum potential are completely determined by the zero‑alignment condition. The helix therefore gives a constructive classical shadow of the sought‑after Hilbert–Pólya operator.

What Is Still Missing

The helix alone does not constitute a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. Four major gaps remain, which we list in logical order.

Gap 1. Rigorous construction of \(\phi(t)\) with a prime‑sum derivative.

The phase function must be a smooth, strictly increasing function that interpolates the zero ordinates and simultaneously satisfies

\[\phi'(t)=\log\frac{t}{2\pi} + \sum_{p}\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\,\eta(t-\log p) + \text{error},\]

where \(\eta\) is a narrow bump and the error is controlled by a power of \(\log t\). Existence of such a \(\phi\) is precisely the content of Conjecture~1 in the CCM paper—the ground‑state eigenvector coefficients of the truncated Weil quadratic form are (up to normalisation) the Fourier transform of this prime‑sum derivative. No independent proof is known.

Gap 2. Well‑defined self‑adjoint operator \(D\) with a singular potential.

The formal expression \(D = i\,d/dx + \sum_p \frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\delta(x-\log p)\) is a Dirac operator with infinitely many point interactions. One must specify a domain on which it is essentially self‑adjoint, and in the appropriate non‑commutative limit, show that its spectrum is purely discrete (or at least that the relevant part is discrete). The periodic compactification regularises the singularity to a finite number of point interactions, which are manageable; the limit as the compactification radius grows is more delicate but lies within the scope of self‑adjoint extension theory for point potentials.

Gap 3. Exact trace formula equating the spectral action of \(D\) with the Weil explicit formula.

For a wide class of test functions \(h\), one must prove

\[\operatorname{Tr}\bigl(h(D)\bigr) = \sum_{p}\frac{\log p}{\sqrt{p}}\,h(\log p) \;+\; \text{archimedean and trivial terms},\]

without assuming the spectrum of \(D\). This would be a rigorous Gutzwiller–Selberg trace formula for a singular geodesic flow.

Gap 4. Proof that the spectrum of \(D\) is exactly the Riemann zeros.

Even if a trace formula is established, one must deduce that the eigenvalues of \(D\) are precisely \(\pm\gamma_n\) (and that there are no other eigenvalues). The Weil explicit formula is an identity that holds for the zeros of \(\zeta(s)\). To use it in reverse requires a solution to an inverse spectral problem.

Tractability Assessment

Gap Description Tractability
1. Phase function with prime‑sum derivative Equivalent to CCM Conjecture~1; requires pointwise control of zero‑density fluctuations. Extremely hard – as hard as RH itself.
2. Self‑adjoint Dirac operator with \(\delta\) potentials Define domain and essential self‑adjointness for infinitely many point interactions on a line/circle. Tractable – existing self‑adjoint extension theory can handle the finite case; the infinite limit requires careful norm‑resolvent convergence.
3. Trace formula = Weil explicit formula Rigorous spectral action computation via multiple scattering or heat‑kernel expansion for a singular operator. Very hard – a major open problem, but there is no fundamental obstruction.
4. Spectrum = Riemann zeros Deduce that the eigenvalues are exactly the zeros from the trace formula. Equivalent to RH – this step is the Riemann Hypothesis.

Conclusion

The Riemann helix accomplishes a profound conceptual simplification: it replaces the abstract inverse spectral problem of Hilbert–Pólya with a concrete geometric quantisation programme. The classical system (prime geodesics) and the candidate quantum operator (Dirac operator with prime potential) are visibly encoded in the same curve. However, the programme still demands the rigorous execution of four steps, of which two are equivalent to proving the Riemann Hypothesis itself. The true power of the helix is that it clarifies exactly where the remaining obstacles lie and offers a common language for geometers, analysts, and number theorists to address them.

8. Path to a Rigorous Proof

  1. Prove Conjecture 1.
  2. Take the double limit \(N,\lambda\to\infty\).
  3. Establish the trace formula for the limit operator.
  4. Uniqueness of the spectrum.

9. Consequences and Implications

  • Riemann Hypothesis follows immediately.
  • Unification of number theory and quantum physics.
  • Geometric arithmetic via the helix.
  • New avenues for the Langlands programme.
  • Advances in spectral analysis.

10. Conclusion

We have shown that the Riemann helix is the classical geometric counterpart of the CCM spectral triple. The dictionary between the two frameworks turns the Hilbert–Pólya programme into a constructive geometric quantisation problem. With this blueprint, the century-old quest enters a new, concrete phase.
