Abstract

The ratio μ / σ² (pronounced “mu over sigma squared”) is a powerful but under-appreciated risk-adjusted performance metric that directly measures the volatility drag (or compounding drag) an investor suffers from return volatility. While the Sharpe ratio (μ / σ) is the most famous risk-adjusted measure, it is actually a second-best approximation when returns are continuously compounded and log-normally distributed. The theoretically correct metric for long-term geometric growth is μ / σ².

Volatility drag arises because arithmetic returns compound geometrically, and volatility reduces the realized compound annual growth rate (CAGR) below the arithmetic mean. The difference between arithmetic mean return and geometric mean return is approximately σ² / 2. Therefore, the higher the volatility for a given arithmetic return, the more future wealth is destroyed. The ratio μ / σ² tells you exactly how many “units of excess growth” you get per unit of drag — making it especially useful for long-horizon investors, leveraged strategies, and crypto assets where volatility is extreme.

This ebook explains the mathematics, intuition, practical interpretation thresholds, comparisons to other metrics, and limitations of μ / σ².

1. Introduction

The problem statement is simple: volatility destroys compounding. A strategy with 15% arithmetic annual return and 30% volatility will compound at roughly 15% − (30%)²/2 = 10.5%, not 15%. This hidden “drag” becomes enormous over decades or when leverage is involved.

Traditional metrics like Sharpe ratio treat volatility linearly (dividing by σ), but the actual damage to terminal wealth is quadratic (proportional to σ²). The ratio μ / σ² corrects this mismatch and has deep connections to the Kelly criterion, stochastic control, and continuous-time finance (Ito’s lemma).

2. Mathematical Foundation

For a return series with arithmetic mean μ and variance σ², the approximate geometric (continuously compounded) return g is:

\[ g \approx \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^{2} \]

where:
μ = expected arithmetic return
σ² = variance of arithmetic returns
The approximation is excellent for daily or higher frequency returns and becomes exact in the continuous limit (Black-Scholes / geometric Brownian motion).

Rearranging shows that the “volatility drag” term is exactly σ²/2. To maximize long-term growth g, we want to maximize:

\[ g \approx \mu - \frac{\sigma^{2}}{2} \]

Taking the first derivative with respect to any position size (leverage) and setting to zero recovers the famous Kelly fraction:

\[ f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^{2}} \]

Thus μ / σ² is literally the optimal Kelly leverage (when μ is excess return over risk-free rate in the simple one-asset case). Even without leverage, it remains the cleanest measure of growth efficiency.

3. Interpretation & Practical Thresholds

  • > 2.0 → Excellent (full Kelly or better)
  • 1.0 – 2.0 → Very good (half-Kelly to full-Kelly territory)
  • 0.5 – 1.0 → Acceptable for long-term investors
  • 0.2 – 0.5 → Weak; high volatility drag dominates
  • < 0.2 → Poor; most crypto assets and highly leveraged strategies live here

4. Real-World Examples

Two identical 20% arithmetic return strategies:

  • Strategy A: σ = 15% → μ/σ² = 20/(15²) = 0.89 → g ≈ 20 − 7.5/2 = 18.9%
  • Strategy B: σ = 40% → μ/σ² = 20/(40²) = 0.125 → g ≈ 20 − 80/2 = -20% long-term loss despite positive average return!

Strategy B will almost surely go to zero over decades despite “winning on average.” This is the essence of volatility drag.

6. Critical Analysis

Strengths

  • Theoretically correct for log-utility and long-term growth
  • Directly linked to optimal Kelly leverage
  • Punishes volatility quadratically — exactly as compounding does

Weaknesses & Counterarguments

  • Very sensitive to estimation error in μ and especially σ²
  • Assumes log-normal returns and continuous rebalancing
  • Can be misleading over short samples where high-vol strategies temporarily dominate
  • Ignores skewness and fat tails (common in real markets)

Many practitioners therefore prefer half-Kelly or fractional-Kelly approaches even when μ/σ² is known.
