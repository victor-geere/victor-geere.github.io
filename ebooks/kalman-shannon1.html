Introduction

This ebook explores concepts from portfolio optimization and rebalancing, drawing from discussions on using mathematical models and filters in finance, particularly in cryptocurrency trading. We begin with foundational ideas like the efficient frontier and progress to advanced techniques such as the Kalman filter and Shannon's ghost. The goal is to provide a comprehensive understanding of how these tools can help achieve profit targets through data-driven strategies. Introductory paragraphs in each section set the stage, followed by detailed explanations. Terms like "drift" (mu) and "volatility" (sigma) are linked to definitions where needed.

The Efficient Frontier and Portfolio Optimization

The efficient frontier represents the set of optimal portfolios that offer the highest expected return for a given level of risk. Introduced in modern portfolio theory, it guides investors in balancing assets to minimize risk while maximizing returns. In rebalancing, intelligence work involves analyzing data to identify when assets deviate from this frontier, prompting adjustments.

Comprehensive detail: Algorithms process historical prices, volatility, and correlations to model deviations. For instance, in a 60/40 stocks-bonds portfolio, rebalancing occurs at 5-10% thresholds to realign with the frontier.

Optimum Rebalancing Strategies

Rebalancing maintains target asset allocations. Optimum strategies use thresholds like 5% deviation or dynamic adjustments based on price points. For base assets (e.g., bonds as anchors), ratios adjust via mean-variance optimization.

Comprehensive detail: In crypto like BTC-USD, split floats into operational and reserve parts for harvesting volatility while estimating with filters. Rebalance operational floats hourly at 5% deviations, using reserves for funding at 10% signals.

Black-Litterman Model

The Black-Litterman model blends market equilibrium returns with investor views using Bayesian methods to refine portfolio weights on the efficient frontier.

Comprehensive detail: Start with CAPM-implied returns, adjust with views (e.g., tech outperforms by 5%). The Bayesian formula: posterior returns = prior + (view matrix × confidence inverse) / lambda.

\[ \mathbf{\mu}_{BL} = \left[ (\tau \Sigma)^{-1} + P^T \Omega^{-1} P \right]^{-1} \left[ (\tau \Sigma)^{-1} \Pi + P^T \Omega^{-1} Q \right] \]

Where: \(\mathbf{\mu}_{BL}\) is the posterior expected returns; \(\tau\) is a scalar for uncertainty; \(\Sigma\) is the covariance matrix; \(\Pi\) is the prior returns; \(P\) is the view matrix; \(\Omega\) is the view uncertainty; \(Q\) is the view vector.

Kalman Filter in Finance

The Kalman filter is a recursive algorithm for estimating states from noisy data, useful in smoothing prices toward predictions like BTC hitting $80,000.

Comprehensive detail: Treat prices as states; priors from predictions. Predict and update steps reduce error covariance. For rebalancing, signal deviations to adjust allocations asymptotically toward profit targets (e.g., 10%). Analogy: Like averaging a guessed bike speed with flickering readings.

\[ x_k = F x_{k-1} + B u_{k-1} + w_{k-1} \] \[ z_k = H x_k + v_k \]

Where: \(x_k\) is the state estimate; \(F\) is the state transition; \(B\) is control input; \(u\) is control; \(w\) is process noise; \(z_k\) is measurement; \(H\) is measurement matrix; \(v\) is measurement noise.

Shannon's ghost and Volatility Harvesting

Shannon's ghost rebalances during volatility swings without directional predictions, buying low and selling high in mean-reverting ways.

Comprehensive detail: Mix with Kalman: Filter smooths signals; ghost acts on spikes. For 10% profit, threshold at 80-95% of target. Split floats: 25% each for BTC/USD operational and reserves.

Drift, Volatility, and Mean Reversion in Crypto Assets

Drift (mu) is trend rate; volatility (sigma) is fluctuation. Mean reversion snaps back to average. Vol needed to cover negative drift: \(\sigma > \sqrt{2 |\mu| / \kappa}\).

Comprehensive detail: For DOT-USD: mu ≈ -0.01% hourly (-85% annualized); sigma ≈ 85% annualized. Required sigma ≈ 1.4% hourly (53% annualized). Comparisons: BTC effective with positive drift; ETH moderate; SOL volatile but rewarding; DOT risky but harvestable.

\[ \sigma > \sqrt{2 |\mu| / \kappa} \]

Where: \(\sigma\) is volatility; \(\mu\) is drift; \(\kappa\) is mean-reversion speed.

Fractional Kelly Criterion

Fractional Kelly scales bets to balance growth and risk, using edge/volatility.

Comprehensive detail: Bet size = f × (mu / sigma²). Use 0.25 in mean-reverting regimes; 0.5 in trends. Relate to orders: 7.5% of float per trade if full Kelly is 30%.

\[ f = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2} \]

Where: \(f\) is Kelly fraction; \(\mu\) is expected return (edge); \(\sigma^2\) is variance.

Hidden Markov Models (HMM)

HMM detects hidden states like bull/bear regimes from observations.

Comprehensive detail: In efficient markets like EUR-USD, it spots subtle vol changes but yields low gains. For crypto, flag regimes for adjusting Kelly fractions.

Market-Making and Order Sizing

Market-making quotes bids/asks to profit from spreads. 15-30% of float in orders.

Critical Analysis of the Contents

These strategies amplify gains but amplify risks—Kalman smoothing may miss true volatility, leading to under-harvesting. In efficient markets, HMM adds marginal value due to noise. Crypto's illiquidity exacerbates slippage, potentially eroding 10% targets to net losses. Assumptions like Gaussian noise in Kalman ignore fat tails in markets, risking blowups. Overall, effective in backtests but requires robust risk management in live trading.

People Who Explored Similar Adjacent Ideas

  • Rudolf Kalman: Invented the Kalman filter for state estimation in noisy systems, applied in finance for price smoothing.
  • Claude Shannon: Information theorist; his "ghost" concept exploits volatility rebalancing without predictions.
  • John Kelly: Created Kelly criterion for bet sizing, extended fractionally for financial risk control.
  • Leonard Baum: Co-developed HMM for sequence modeling, used in regime detection in trading.
